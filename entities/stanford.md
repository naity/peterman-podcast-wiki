# Stanford

Stanford appears in three episodes: as the institution that stood behind Martin Hellman during the crypto wars, as the PhD lab network that seeds AI startups, and as a stop on Ryan Williams's theory career.

## What the episodes reveal

- **Backing a researcher against the NSA.** Public-key cryptography and Diffie-Hellman were invented at Stanford, and when the NSA framed publishing cryptography as illegal arms export under ITAR, Stanford's general counsel judged that interpretation unconstitutional and defended Hellman — who personally presented his students' papers at Cornell so the university could defend *him* instead of them. Less gloriously, the Stanford patent was written by a law-school intern rather than a patent attorney, failed to cover RSA, and Hellman made virtually nothing while RSA sold for $250M ([Hellman](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)). Ralph Merkle, whose independent invention at Berkeley had been dismissed, was recruited by Hellman to Stanford ([same](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
- **The lab-to-startup pipeline.** Nimit Sohani's PhD in Chris Ré's Stanford lab shaped his whole career: labmates — including Mamba creator Albert Gu — founded the voice-AI startup Cartesia he later joined; his takeaway is that 90% of research is finding the right problem, and research taste comes from constant literature reading grown out of Stanford contacts ([Stanford PhD/quant](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- **Theory careers.** Ryan Williams's circuit lower bounds — pivoted out of a failed SAT algorithm — won him his Stanford faculty job before he moved to [MIT](mit.md) ([MIT complexity theorist](../sources/mit-complexity-theorist-on-leetcode.md)).
