# Nimit Sohani

AI researcher at voice-AI startup Cartesia, ex-quantitative researcher at Citadel Securities, with a [Stanford](../entities/stanford.md) PhD from Chris Ré's lab — a rare guest who has worked both of the most lucrative "math + computation" tracks, quant finance and AI research ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).

## Career arc

Sohani did his PhD at [Stanford](../entities/stanford.md) in Chris Ré's lab, then chose quant out of curiosity and risk aversion, joining Citadel Securities as a quantitative researcher just before ChatGPT launched — mostly C++ work grounded in stochastic calculus, numerical optimization, and increasingly deep learning. Contrary to reputation, he had great work-life balance there (trader schedules anchor the culture to US market hours). As AI took off he deliberately took [startup](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md) risk after establishing financial stability, leaving Citadel for Cartesia — founded by his labmates including Mamba creator Albert Gu — where he works on voice AI competing with ElevenLabs on latency, cost, and voice cloning, pushing toward end-to-end speech models that replace the lossy STT→LLM→TTS pipeline ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).

## Key views & advice

- A PhD is rarely strictly required but opens doors in [AI research](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md) and quant, and its core skill is problem selection: "90% of the battle in research is actually finding the right problems." Research taste matures gradually from small extensions of existing work to new ideas — skipping that maturation is inadvisable ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- Quant [compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md) has no standardized levels: it's opaque, bonus-weighted, driven by firm/team/strategy performance, with paid garden-leave non-competes of six months to two years. Job security is U-shaped — expensive senior quants can stop being "worth it" even when good ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- Finance is radically more secretive than tech — pods within one firm may unknowingly work on the same strategies; returning to tech he was jarred that people "just tell you that for free." He now thinks quant has *better* work-life balance than hyper-competitive AI research, where people gain edge by outworking rivals ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- His quant firm tier list: Rentech is the mythical gold standard; Jane Street, Citadel, Jump Trading, Hudson River the strong big names; TGS, XTX, Radix the elite secretive shops ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- Big labs have compute but suffer groupthink and risk-aversion; startups can "challenge the orthodoxy" — e.g. Mamba disproving that transformer scaling had solved sequence modeling. SSMs compress context into a fixed-size state (a brain) versus transformers' linearly growing KV cache (a database); hybrids are the text cutting edge, and for redundant modalities like audio SSM compression is "almost a free lunch" ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- Skeptical of both research-only startups (all-or-nothing against resource-rich labs) and product-only wrappers (no moat); the durable position is the product-research intersection ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- For SWEs wanting AI research: build fundamentals until "the opportunities will just come to you"; big companies silo you, so a master's credential or a flexible startup helps ([hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md), [episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
- His biggest [regret](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md) is the time spent regretting decisions that didn't matter; advice to his younger self: focus deep, don't spread thin — "a simple recipe that's very hard to follow" ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).
