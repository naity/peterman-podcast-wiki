# Bell Labs

AT&T's research arm — in [Bjarne Stroustrup](bjarne-stroustrup.md)'s words the best applied engineering lab in the world, and the birthplace of C++, Unix, fiber optics, CCDs, and cellular systems ([Stroustrup episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)).

Stroustrup's account is a portrait of deliberately anarchic research management. There was no formal [interview](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md) process — he flew over on his own tab, chatted with Dennis Ritchie, and got a call a week later — and the research mandate was simply "do something interesting in a year's time," reported on a single sheet of paper; he argues this model out-produced carefully managed research programs ([episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)). It was also a place of extraordinary colleague density: Stroustrup had weekly lunches with Ritchie for years, Ritchie helped design C++'s `const` and endorsed C++ in writing as C's successor, and Ritchie's rejected "fat pointers" idea resurfaced decades later as C++'s span ([episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)). C++ itself began there as a failed attempt to build a distributed Unix, merging C's hardware access with Simula's classes ([programming-languages](../concepts/programming-languages.md)) ([episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)).
