# Edsger Dijkstra

Turing Award-winning Dutch computer scientist (1930–2002) — shortest paths, structured programming, concurrency primitives — who appears in the podcast not as a guest but through the firsthand stories of [Leslie Lamport](leslie-lamport.md) and [Barbara Liskov](barbara-liskov.md), and as an authority [Bjarne Stroustrup](bjarne-stroustrup.md) still invokes.

Lamport, who drank beer with him and shared his one piece of joint work — simplifying the first concurrent garbage-collection algorithm — credits Dijkstra with recognizing that Lamport's "obvious" idea wasn't obvious, and that Lamport's real gift was abstraction: "Dijkstra was smart enough to realize that" ([working-with-legends](../concepts/working-with-legends.md)) ([Lamport episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)). Dijkstra's Dining Philosophers also taught Lamport that a cute story makes a result famous — the deliberate marketing behind the "Byzantine Generals" name ([same](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)). Liskov contextualizes "Goto Statement Considered Harmful": it was a letter, not a paper, its deep point being that reasoning about code correctness is hard at a time when science dismissed CS as trivial; it offended skilled goto users, and "Dijkstra was not the most diplomatic person," but "clearly Dijkstra won the day" ([Liskov episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)). Decades later Stroustrup still channels Dijkstra's verdict against AI coding hype: natural language is too ambiguous to be a programming language ([Stroustrup episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)).
