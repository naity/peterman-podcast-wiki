# David Fowler

Distinguished Engineer at [Microsoft](microsoft.md) — the final partner level (70), below only Technical Fellow — reached via 11 promotions from a 2006 internship; co-creator of NuGet and SignalR and architect of .NET Core ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).

## Career arc

He stood out at a college career fair by demoing a self-built game on a laptop, landing a [Microsoft](microsoft.md) internship in 2006 when interviews were puzzles rather than LeetCode ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)). His early engine was "code is currency": troll forums for customer problems, build the prototype first, then show PMs and architects — first promotion 8 months in. SignalR began as a nights-and-weekends project (inspired by Google Docs co-editing, pre-WebSockets) that Microsoft later made official with a team of ~8. He deliberately attached himself to architect David Ebbo, coding his ideas and mimicking his behaviors until he became an architect himself, and later requested a Technical Fellow mentor (Jeffrey Snover) once he decided he wanted DE. As senior-level co-architect of .NET Core with ~30 engineers he burned out within a year trying to co-review every change, forcing him to learn delegation. His Distinguished promotion was peer-reviewed by Technical Fellows against a decade-long body of work, leaning on ~7 years of deferred .NET Core impact ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).

## Key views & advice

- "Code is currency. I can show you a working sample. It's not PowerPoint, it's not a document" — prototypes, not persuasion ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- "You get a chance to be more permissionless the better your track record is" — success buys leeway to fail and try things ([career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)) ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- Amplify strengths to superhuman rather than fixing weaknesses — "no one gets promoted to these roles for being average" — and go directly to the promotion deciders to ask where you stand ([promotions](../concepts/promotions.md), [mentorship and sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)) ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- The senior-to-principal leap is doing more through others: delegation, trust, and letting people fail ([management](../concepts/management.md)) ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- Refuses to be an architect who doesn't know the code: blocks a minimum of 8 hours a week to think and build ("decline, decline, decline") and still sends pull requests daily ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- Interviews test writing new code but the higher-leverage skill is debugging — he'd hand candidates a crash dump; universities should teach surgical fixes in huge existing codebases, since CS is not software engineering ([hiring and interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md)) ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- Big-company tips: before changing roles, ask when the last reorg was (reorgs reveal where the org is heading); evaluate a job move by what you'd hate, not what you'd love ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- He turned down better pre-IPO Twitter and GitHub offers to stay (network, autonomy, a new project every two years) and was depressed for a week when Twitter IPO'd — yet would tell a mentee today to take the hot outside offer ([compensation and equity](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md), [regrets and advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)) ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- On Microsoft's culture: Ballmer-to-Satya was a shift from competing teams and shouting matches to incentivized collaboration — reviews dinged you for cloning another team's work ([big tech culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md)) ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- Advice to his younger self: "being right means nothing" without patience and empathy; negotiate your salary and get multiple offers ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
