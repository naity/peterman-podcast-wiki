# David Ronca

Retired [Netflix](netflix.md) engineering director (video encoding), later director and principal engineer in [Meta](meta.md)'s video infrastructure org ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).

## Career arc

Survived InterVideo's 24/7 startup culture (10am to 10pm-or-3am, seven days a week), which ended in a stage-3 colon cancer diagnosis in 2004 with a 25% five-year prognosis — the origin of his insistence that crunch be the exception, not the rule. At Netflix he ran video encoding during the culture-memo era; his work with ex-professor Ioannis "Giannis" Katsavounidis — the strongest engineer he ever met — birthed content-based and convex-hull encoding, now the encoding model at both Netflix and Meta. He then joined Meta as an outside director over a well-functioning ~60-person video org, where from April 2020 Facebook video ran at New Year's Eve peak load every day for 3.5B users (vs Netflix's ~300M) ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).

## Key views & advice

- Netflix's "no brilliant jerks" rule was revolutionary; companies survive the departure of "indispensable" difficult heroes just fine — when a hoarding hero quit, everyone else simply moved in. Patty McCord told him in his own interview: "blow us away with what you can do in an eight hour day," and he later forced a burned-out engineer to take vacation regardless of outages — the team stabilized the system precisely because they stepped back ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).
- But Netflix's flat single-level system and "personal top of market" [compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md) broke at scale: without leveling nobody could rationalize pay gaps, and without individual credit the best engineers eventually leave. The most valuable thing Meta taught him was objective performance management — data-driven [calibrations](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md) and PSC packets permanently recording individual achievement, exactly what Netflix's team-first culture lacked ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).
- On [hiring](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md): leetcode "does not tell you anything about an engineer" — the innate foundation is understanding complex systems, technical intuition, and deciding without enough data. He hired a train-automation engineer and a wastewater-treatment civil engineer into video software roles on that basis ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). This contrasts with the structured committee machine [Austen McDonald](austen-mcdonald.md) describes ([episode](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)).
- When in doubt take the lower level at big tech: come in as a 5 executing like a 6 and the bonuses and refreshers follow; come in over-leveled and "there's no way to fix that problem" ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).
- On joining as an outside leader: two weeks into Meta he was told "you've been here two weeks, you're already telling us what we need to do." Lesson: build trust first — "if it's a team that's actually executing well, your job is to not break it first and then help it move forward second" ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).
- [Advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md) to his younger self: don't waste years coasting at large failing companies; "if your job is not difficult, you're not growing"; work for visionary leaders who can execute; start financial planning on day one. His formative book, "12 Secrets to Microsoft Success," he argues predicted the 2021 FAANG overhiring and 2022 contraction ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).
