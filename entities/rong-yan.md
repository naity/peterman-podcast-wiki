# Rong Yan

CTO of AI video startup HeyGen; previously Senior Director at [Snapchat](../entities/snapchat.md), Director at Square, frontline manager at [Meta](../entities/meta.md), and [IBM](../entities/ibm.md) Research scientist with a CMU machine learning PhD. He went frontline manager to Senior Director in about three years ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).

## Career arc

Yan left research at [IBM](../entities/ibm.md) for engineering in service of a lifelong "North Star" — becoming CTO of an AI company — which drove every unconventional move: Facebook's six-week bootcamp (where a VP-level hire sat next to him fixing bugs), frontline management at [Meta](../entities/meta.md), a jump directly to a Director offer at Square (smaller companies will let you perform a level up), where he grew his team from ~25 to 50+ in a year, then Senior Director at [Snapchat](../entities/snapchat.md) — deliberately choosing the camera org over the comfortable data org and learning iOS from scratch in two months to close product-side gaps. He joined Snapchat as roughly its 100th employee and watched it grow to ~3,000 in two years, his org reaching ~250 engineers. He is now CTO of HeyGen ([non-linear-careers](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md), [episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).

## Key views & advice

- "Director is about directing instead of managing": directors add value through strategic direction, cross-department resource gathering, and solving the org's hardest problems — not knowing the most details. He admits he underperformed his first six months until he made that shift ([management](../concepts/management.md), [episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- On [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) and luck: manager growth is heavily constrained by org structure and company trajectory. "Levels and titles are things the company gives you; they are not yours" — don't make promotions the objective; control impact in your current role and treat titles as lagging indicators ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- When hired above people who wanted your role: build trust first (he recommends The Five Dysfunctions of a Team) — show each report you're a promoter of their career and unblock what they can't ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- "Every single time you double your team, it's almost like you're building a new company" — leaders must abandon old cultural assumptions at each doubling ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- Everyone in engineering must stay technical — internalized from Facebook bootcamp and part of the [big tech culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md) he carried forward; as CTO he still writes and reviews code weekly. He scales himself by picking the top three priorities each week — the best leaders win on time allocation, not hours ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- Senior-level recruiting is almost all inbound and personal-network driven — he has never proactively job-searched and weights personal connections heavily, wanting people he trusts through down cycles; an argument for building your [network](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md) early ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- At HeyGen he tells candidates upfront there's only a ~1% chance of huge success, deliberately filtering for people who will stay through hard cycles ([hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md), [startups-and-founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md), [episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- Evan Spiegel stories: pixel-level product review feedback reminiscent of Steve Jobs, a promise never to fire the first 15 engineers, and buying back the original "Blue House" in 2017 for board meetings ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
