# Intel

Intel appears in three episodes as the x86 incumbent of computer-architecture history — the company whose survival trick, standards-committee weight, and internal rigidity all get discussed, though no guest worked there as their main story.

## What the episodes reveal

- **x86's survival and the new pressure.** David Patterson explains that x86 survived the RISC revolution via an early-2000s trick: translating x86 instructions into RISC micro-ops in hardware, overhead justified by the PC binary software base. Google's 2016 TPU announcement (~30x GPU, ~80x CPU inference performance) was among the events that forced Intel and [NVIDIA](nvidia.md) to respond, and the end of Dennard scaling plus the slowing of Moore's Law — which Patterson says the semiconductor world partly denies for emotional reasons — ended the era Intel's business was built on ([Patterson](../sources/turing-award-winner-tpu-vs-gpu-vs.md)).
- **Standards politics.** Bjarne Stroustrup ran "shuttle diplomacy" between IBM and Intel on the C++ committee — the hardware giants' disputes (e.g. over cache/memory-model concerns) had to be personally mediated by the language's creator ([Stroustrup](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)). Bryan Cantrill notes "rank and yank" stack ranking was Intel's own coinage ([Bryan Cantrill](../sources/distinguished-eng-on-stack-ranking.md)).
- **As a workplace.** Igor's comparison from a 14.5-year Google career: switching projects internally was fluid at [Google](google.md) and [Meta](meta.md) but near-impossible at Intel — his escape-hatch criterion for choosing employers ([demotion story](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
