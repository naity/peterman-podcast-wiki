# Carey Nachenberg

Chief Scientist at GoogleX/Chronicle, previously Symantec Fellow (IC10/VP-equivalent, the company's most senior engineer), later at Lyft's autonomous-driving effort, and an adjunct CS professor at UCLA ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).

## Career arc

Joined Symantec's Peter Norton Group as its first intern in 1992 and rose to Fellow — the senior-most engineer in the company — by repeatedly finding gaps: high-impact projects the business needed that nobody was doing, such as detecting polymorphic self-mutating viruses. Symantec never assigned him work; he picked every project himself. He was made Fellow without applying when the Veritas merger forced a company-wide level-set run by a CTO-led committee of technologists ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).

He stayed 21+ years — by his own admission, past the point he stopped growing — until Symantec's ex-COO pulled him into [Google](google.md)X with no application. The loop was ~8 interviews with no coding questions, yet Google still leveled him down from an L10-equivalent to L8 ("we can't just hire fellows"). At GoogleX/Chronicle he worked on Backstory, indexing petabytes of enterprise security telemetry so threat-hunting queries dropped from ~30 minutes to ~2 seconds. At Lyft he drove the shift from hand-parametrized robotics driving algorithms to an end-to-end neural-network driver wrapped in a deterministic safety layer — achieved purely through influence with skeptical roboticists. He also teaches CS at UCLA ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).

## Key views & advice

- [Imposter syndrome](../concepts/imposter-syndrome.md) is his named reason for staying at Symantec too long; his biggest regret is not leaving earlier — stay only as long as you're learning ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
- Intelligence matters less than communication, collaboration, and outcome focus: he knew a 200-IQ engineer stuck at L4 at Google due to poor communication and no business impact. "Just because you have intelligence doesn't mean you're gonna be successful" ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
- Document your accomplishments and claim specific, unembellished credit — he lost credit for the Lyft neural-driver shift because he let others tell the story ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
- On [speaking](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md): design talks for the 30th–50th percentile listener and practice via optional lunchroom talks; people equate communication skill with intelligence, so it compounds into credit and opportunities ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
- On [AI-era engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md): he regrets letting UCLA students use LLMs on projects (project scores diverged sharply from exam understanding) and is banning them for projects while allowing them for learning. If AI reaches "AGI-good" coding, the valuable skills become outcome analysis, customer understanding, and communicating requirements — being a genuinely competent product manager ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
- Advice to his younger self: don't let fear of failure hold you back, focus on outcomes over technology, and find a manager who trusts you and gives you rope — a manager can make or break your career ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
