# Martin Hellman

Turing Award winner and [Stanford](stanford.md) professor emeritus, co-inventor (with Whitfield Diffie) of public-key cryptography and the Diffie-Hellman key exchange ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).

## Career arc

A self-described Bronx "street fighter with my mouth and my mind," Hellman worked in [security and cryptography](../concepts/security-and-cryptography.md) at Stanford when every colleague told him he was crazy to compete with the NSA's budget and head start ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)). He fought the first crypto war on two fronts: the NSA claimed his published papers violated ITAR (crypto as an "implement of war") — after a 1977 NSA letter to the IEEE, he presented his students' papers at Cornell himself so Stanford could defend him rather than them — and he and Diffie exposed DES's 56-bit key as a crude trapdoor, estimating a $20M machine could break it for ~$10,000 per solution ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)). He recruited Ralph Merkle, whose independently invented public-key distribution had been dismissed as a Berkeley term project and rejected by CACM ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)). Stanford's patent, drafted by a law school intern, failed to cover RSA — RSA sold for $250M while the Stanford inventors made virtually nothing; Hellman deliberately made peace with RSA because "friends are better than enemies" ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)). Since cryptography he has worked on nuclear risk, a [non-linear career](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md) turn, including pushing H.R. 3564 to require more than sole presidential authority to launch ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).

## Key views & advice

- Strong encryption cannot be given only to good guys; key escrow (Clipper Chip, San Bernardino) is unsolvable — his 1995 CRISIS committee found classified briefings "filled in details but did not change our fundamental conclusions" ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
- The best work looks crazy a priori (GPS, multiple Nobel stories); he credits a contrarian identity forged by childhood bullying ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
- Digital signatures — the piece GCHQ's claimed earlier invention lacked entirely — are what let an iPhone verify [Apple](apple.md) software updates with only a public key on the device ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
- Simultaneous invention is normal: ideas ripen, so Merkle, GCHQ, and Diffie-Hellman converged independently ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
- He estimates a child born today has worse-than-even odds of living out a natural life due to nuclear weapons: "We need to save humanity from an infant death" ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
- [Advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md) to his younger self: get famous and rich first, then save the world — reputation and money at 35 gave him leverage to work on what mattered ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
