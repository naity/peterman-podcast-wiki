# Joakim Recht

Former Distinguished Engineer at [Uber](uber.md), based in Denmark; now an engineer at an AI enterprise-automation startup ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).

## Career arc

Joining [Uber](uber.md) from small tech with real [imposter syndrome](../concepts/imposter-syndrome.md), he found "the big-tech people were not much better after all" ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)). His Distinguished-level project, Odin, began as his Denmark team running MySQL shards in Docker with declarative intent-based management when Kubernetes was barely in alpha; it grew into the platform for all of Uber's stateful workloads — ~120,000 physical servers and ~500,000 databases run by a team of about 20. His Senior→Principal→Distinguished promotions each followed a stage of Odin's growth. He left Uber when new management imposed top-down decisions engineers couldn't stand behind — notably a cloud migration justified on cost that no engineer believed, which only "worked" after a 50% cost cut that would have been cheaper on-prem; refusing to "stick around and take the money," he joined an AI startup rather than follow ex-colleagues to Databricks ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).

## Key views & advice

- Model of [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md): level tracks how many people are affected by your work, so a project with natural scope expansion "drags promotions along automatically — and not just for you, but the entire team" ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- Promo committees are structurally unfair: his first one reviewed hundreds of engineers in a single day with no prep materials, decided by how well each manager pitched ([calibrations and ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md)) ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- "A software engineer needs to write code" at every level, ideally every day; "running code beats perfect code any day"; instead of hunting promotion-worthy scope, provide value and the promotion follows ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- Contrarian delegation take: delegate the hard interesting work down to people who can barely manage it so they grow fast, and keep the boring "shoveling shit" work yourself, since you have nothing left to prove ([management](../concepts/management.md)) ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- Platform adoption strategy: prove it on your own team, expand through friendly orgs to skeptics — but a final top-down mandate is still necessary, because full alignment never happens bottom-up alone ([influence and leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)) ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- Engineers distrust authority without demonstrated understanding; his best influence outcome is overhearing someone champion, as their own conviction, an idea they once rejected from him ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- Senior-level [politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md): "nobody's in control, there's an illusion of control"; he coped by saying no a lot and using the Denmark timezone as a shield against SF meetings ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- [Mentorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md): pick mentees outside your own org to learn other parts of the company and plant culture change; keep it informal ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- On Uber's biggest engineering mistakes: chronic inability to effect global change (a tracing mandate re-emailed for ~3 years never got done); the early "let builders build" freedom became deadly at scale ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
