# Uber

Uber appears in two episodes through senior ICs who grew up in its hypergrowth era: Brian Attwell (Senior Staff by 25, later CloudKitchens CTO under Travis Kalanick) and Joakim Recht (Distinguished Engineer from the Denmark office).

## What the episodes reveal

- **Hypergrowth meritocracy — at a price.** Attwell credits his rise to a meritocratic, hypergrowth environment plus extreme work ethic: he and a peer worked 90–100 hour weeks prototyping the options the team was debating, becoming de facto tech leads within months and top-3 committers across Uber mobile — "promotions are inevitable" at that point. He also once collapsed on the street walking home, unable to move for 15 minutes ([CloudKitchens CTO](../sources/cloudkitchens-cto-on-intelligence.md)).
- **Scope-driven promotion, organically.** Recht rode one project — Odin, a containerized platform for stateful workloads started as a local Docker-for-MySQL experiment — to ~120,000 physical servers and ~500,000 databases run by ~20 people; each stage of its expansion (team → org → platform → company) dragged a promotion along "automatically," across a company that had ~52 database technologies in use ([Uber DE](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- **Promo committees, warts and all.** Recht's first committee reviewed hundreds of engineers in a single day with no preparation or materials, purely on manager pitch quality — "if you have a bad manager, you have a bad case"; even structured processes leave unavoidable unfairness (ghosting dependencies, canceled projects, isolated teams denied without ever being told what good looks like). His antidote: engineers must write code at every level, and "just relax... focus on providing value — the other thing will come" ([Uber DE](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- **Culture's failure to mature.** The "let builders build" freedom that was right early on became "an insane strategy that's going to kill you" ten years in — a tracing mandate was re-emailed for ~3 years and never done, and a Node-based networking stack took years to untangle; Uber never managed the freedom-to-structure transition well ([Uber DE](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)). Recht ultimately left when new management brought top-down decisions engineers couldn't stand behind — a cloud migration justified on cost "no engineer believed" ([same](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- Working from Denmark was an asset: uninterrupted engineering days with only a couple of evening hours exposed to San Francisco meetings and hallway VP grabs ([Uber DE](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
