# UC Berkeley

UC Berkeley threads through all four systems-oriented Turing-laureate episodes: it is where Ingres and Postgres were built, where RISC was named, and part of the origin stories of Liskov and Merkle.

## What the episodes reveal

- **Databases.** Mike Stonebraker started building Ingres at Berkeley in 1972 — one year after Codd's relational paper — knowing nothing about databases; it earned him tenure in 1976 and spread to ~100 universities as a free database on Unix. Postgres followed there, with its defining extendable type system. His distillation: "think crazy thoughts and try and do them" ([Stonebraker](../sources/turing-award-winner-postgres-disagreeing.md)).
- **Computer architecture.** David Patterson coined "RISC" as a Berkeley professor and, with Hennessy, brought quantitative methods to a field that had argued by intuition; ~99% of all processors today are RISC ([Patterson](../sources/turing-award-winner-tpu-vs-gpu-vs.md)).
- **Origin stories.** Barbara Liskov took her math degree at Berkeley before Princeton rejected her graduate application by postcard ("we do not admit women") and she became a programmer instead ([Liskov](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)). Ralph Merkle independently invented public-key distribution as a Berkeley student in a term project his professor dismissed and CACM rejected — Hellman later recruited him to [Stanford](stanford.md) and insists he deserves co-credit ([Hellman](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
