# Jayendra Jog

Co-founder of Sei Labs (a $1B+ crypto company that raised $35M), previously a software engineer at Robinhood from its 2018 new-grad class ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).

## Career arc

A "LeetCode and Chill" study night happened to cover the exact question (number of islands) he later got in his Facebook internship onsite at [Meta](meta.md) — a lucky break he credits for his entire subsequent career ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). After UCLA he joined Robinhood as a 2018 new grad; roughly a third of that class went on to found venture-backed companies. Nine months in, months of 2 AM nights and brutal on-call earned him a 5/5 rating with no promotion or comp change, and he deliberately dropped to "close to average" effort — yet still got promoted, because in a 10x-growth company a competent engineer becomes load-bearing by default. Golden handcuffs ($400K to exercise his options) kept him 3.5 years until the July 2021 IPO; his equity ended up an order of magnitude above an equivalent big-tech stint. He then left to co-found Sei Labs, raising a $5M seed led by Multicoin and $35M three weeks after the Terra collapse ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).

## Key views & advice

- Careers move in step functions, not linearly; luck at a few key moments dominates ([non-linear careers](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md)) ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
- Choose employers by what you're optimizing for: max comp upside means very-early-stage with a big exit; fastest learning means a small company growing quickly. The best controllable heuristic is surrounding yourself with really smart people ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
- Openly jaded about [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md): after effort and rewards decoupled, he coasted — and argues growth companies promote competent coasters anyway ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
- Insider account of the GameStop halt: Robinhood turned off buys on Jan 28, 2021 because T+2 settlement demanded billions in overnight collateral, not to manipulate prices — and he believes the company miscommunicated this "potentially on purpose" while fundraising ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
- Engineers over-index on the risk of quitting: competent people always find work, and a failed founding attempt makes you more valuable to early-stage startups because you learn product validation, marketing, BD, and go-to-market ([startups and founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md)) ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
- Fundraising: "If you get a tier one lead investor, everything else is super easy after that" — other funds herd behind the stamp ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
- Founding requires little engineering skill ("a competent L4 can do perfectly fine"); most business problems are mundane. Advice to his younger self: think decisions through, then pull the trigger quickly — he wishes he'd left Robinhood earlier ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
