# Dwayne Reeves

Senior Staff Engineer (IC7) at [Meta](meta.md) and tech lead for Hack, Meta's most-used programming language; ~14-year tenure from pre-IPO new grad ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).

## Career arc

An [MIT](mit.md) grad who interviewed at pre-IPO Facebook only as practice for [Google](google.md), he was won over by real engineering problems (memcache scaling, a custom PHP runtime) and care signals like an unsolicited offer increase and CTO Bret Taylor taking MIT new grads to lunch ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)). He says MIT's career value was opportunity access and name recognition, not the education — equally capable colleagues without degrees reached Facebook only by chance. His breakout project, typing an untyped core PHP API with Hack, was scoped at 1–2 weeks, took a quarter, and surfaced real bugs in ~20% of migrated call sites, making Meta take Hack seriously. IC5 came from the half where he wrote the least code of his career (raising Hack coverage from ~20–30% to ~60–70% by influencing others); IC6 from handing off his year-long collections redesign, the handoff itself demonstrating next-level scope. He became a tech-lead-manager reluctantly, scaled to ~13 reports, then returned to IC because replacing a manager for 6–7 people was easier than replacing a tech lead for 35–40; IC7 came while still a manager, credited to his technical vision — by then virtually the whole codebase was strict Hack, up from 5–10% of files in 2015 ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).

## Key views & advice

- "Writing code is just not what my job as a software engineer is. My job is to identify and solve problems" — the lesson of his highest-rated, least-code half ([career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)) ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
- Both his [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) surprised him because his model of the next level was wrong; his manager's rocket-ship framing ("I only cared about you getting us out of orbit — now pass this off") taught him that letting go is the promotion evidence ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
- Static typing wins at industry scale because large codebases are an information-communication problem: types communicate intent, power tooling, and provably eliminate error classes ([programming languages](../concepts/programming-languages.md)) ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
- His "uncanny valley of type systems": as a codebase nears full static typing, remaining unreliable types feel increasingly off-putting, so things get worse just before 100% — the concept became the Hack team's rallying vision ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
- Working with legends like type theorist Andrew Kennedy taught him not to fear pedigree; accomplished seniors should stay humble and approachable ([working with legends](../concepts/working-with-legends.md), [imposter syndrome](../concepts/imposter-syndrome.md)) ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
- On staying ~14 years: "I don't want to make permanent decisions based off of temporary circumstances" — outside offers never fixed the actual (solvable) problem ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
- Advice to his younger self: when told to write code, ask how they knew it was the right code to write, and aim to be in the rooms deciding what to build ([regrets and advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)) ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
