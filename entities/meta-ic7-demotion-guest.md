# Igor (Meta IC7 demotion story)

Semi-anonymous guest (first name only): a senior staff engineer (IC7/L7) who spent 14.5 years at [Google](../entities/google.md), joined [Meta](../entities/meta.md) as an E7, and — in the show's most striking counterpoint to promotion stories — deliberately negotiated a *downlevel* back to L6 at Google because he prefers hands-on engineering to senior-staff leadership work ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).

## Career arc

Igor rose L3 to L7 over 14.5 years at [Google](../entities/google.md) — his L7 promo project was porting ads' 10-year-polished CPU-based ML training infrastructure to first-generation TPUs, ordering chips 18 months ahead of delivery. He left for Cruise, then joined [Meta](../entities/meta.md) as an E7. After 14 months he self-assessed as having ramped only to ~E6 performance, and more importantly discovered he didn't enjoy E7 work. Meta's HR had no process to demote him at his own request, so he boomeranged to Google, negotiating a return at L6 with a large [compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md) cut — Google amusingly almost required a coding interview for the *lower* level ("who knows if you wrote code while you were out") though L7 would have needed none ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).

## Key views & advice

- Joining a big company at senior staff means racing a clock: in [PSC calibrations](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md) you're judged against tenured E7s, and with Meta's bottom-10% [layoff](../concepts/layoffs.md) culture you have roughly a year to match old-timers. You effectively re-climb every level internally; Meta is especially bad at onboarding senior external hires because nearly all its senior people grew up inside ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- The best quality-of-life level is senior engineer (E5/L5): shielded from upward reporting, still doing the enjoyable work — coding, debugging, [mentoring](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md). People chase promos blind because below-senior engineers can't see what principal-level life is like. His line: "I'm capable of running a marathon, but I don't like running" ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- [Meta vs Google culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md): Meta sets deliberately aggressive arbitrary deadlines with constant status pressure; deadlines slip and "everybody is fine with that" — "like elementary school, where the teacher is yelling but kids are still playing." Constant pressure stops working ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- Onboarding [regret](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md): he read docs too long instead of learning by building in parallel ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- On [imposter syndrome](../concepts/imposter-syndrome.md): comparing yourself to a *group* of peers means comparing against a composite no individual matches. "I'm sure Elon Musk has imposter syndrome" ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- On employer loyalty: the pre-COVID Google Atlanta office shutdown taught him "at the end of the day, I'm just a cell in the spreadsheet." He can be publicly candid only because he's financially safe, and warns others not to post about work troubles unless equally safe ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- Ask whether your company really benefits from what you're building — leadership later killed projects for the exact pointlessness he'd sensed; project-switching fluidity varies by company (easy at Google/Meta, near-impossible at [Intel](../entities/intel.md) and reputedly [IBM](../entities/ibm.md)) ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- "Don't underestimate your interns": an intern he hosted later became the manager of the very team he interned on ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
