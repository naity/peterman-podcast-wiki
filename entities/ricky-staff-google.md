# Ricky (Staff @ Google by 28)

Staff Engineer at [Google](google.md) by age 28; appeared as co-speaker with host Ryan Peterman at a UCLA UPE talk on what they wish they'd known before graduating ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)).

## Career arc

The episode is an advice talk rather than a career retrospective: Ricky reached Staff at [Google](google.md) by 28 and, with Peterman (Staff at Meta), distilled early-career lessons for UCLA students ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)).

## Key views & advice

Views below are jointly presented by Ricky and Ryan Peterman in the talk ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)):

- Luck influences [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md), but you can expand your "luck surface area" by taking initiative to seek opportunities and being prepared when they arrive.
- Perfect code isn't enough: the best engineers weren't necessarily the top programmers from college but had balanced technical and soft skills — [influencing others](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md), collaborating, and writing concisely.
- People decide promotions, so advocate for yourself; visibility matters alongside quality work.
- Choose Big Tech over [startups](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md) straight out of college for instant credibility and foundational skills, then pivot to startups later with brand equity and expertise.
- Lines of code != impact: the industry rewards results, not effort; less code with greater impact is preferable.
- Big Tech won't make you crazy rich: climbing levels reliably buys financial stability and freedom, but exceptional wealth requires breakout startup [equity](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md).
- Job hopping helps early, but promotions beyond Senior/Staff depend on influence and credibility built over time at one company, making late-career hopping counterproductive.
- [LLMs](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md) are productivity tools like the many non-AI tools engineers already use; SWE jobs look secure for roughly five years, with longer-term impacts unpredictable.
- For recruiting: resume projects are the minimum; persistence and joining smaller companies early accelerates experience. For internship return offers, finish well-defined projects quickly with high-quality code, then propose improvements.
