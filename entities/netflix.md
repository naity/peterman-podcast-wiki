# Netflix

Streaming company famous for its culture memo; appears in the podcast through [David Ronca](david-ronca.md), who joined in 2007, built the encoding technology team from one person (himself) to ~55, and left as an engineering director for [Meta](meta.md) in 2019.

Ronca gives an insider's verdict on the legendary culture. The "no brilliant jerks" rule was genuinely revolutionary in the early 2000s — every prior employer tolerated indispensable prima donnas — and chief people officer Patty McCord told him in his own interview that Netflix doesn't value 24/7 work: "blow us away with what you can do in an eight hour day" ([Retired Netflix director](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). But the culture didn't scale: a single flat "senior software engineer" level plus "personal top of market" [compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md) (match any competing offer) worked for a small all-senior team, yet with growth produced irrational pay gaps nobody could rationalize, and the team-first ethos of no individual credit meant leaders got the recognition — which eventually drives out your best engineers ([same](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). Ronca says the most valuable thing Meta later taught him was exactly what Netflix lacked: objective performance management via [calibrations](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md) and permanent records of individual achievement ([same](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). Technically, Netflix is where Ronca's work with Ioannis Katsavounidis — the strongest engineer he ever worked with — birthed content-based and convex hull encoding, now the encoding model at both Netflix (~300M users) and Meta ([same](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)).
