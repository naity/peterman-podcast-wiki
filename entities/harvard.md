# Harvard

University that appears in the podcast as the home of CS50, the world's most famous intro computer science course, taught by [David Malan](david-malan.md) since 2007 and freely available online ([CS50 episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)).

Malan is himself a Harvard product: a government concentrator who took CS50 under Brian Kernighan sophomore year, found homework fun for the first time in 19 years, and switched fields ([episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)). His CS50 rejects the MOOC playbook — ~3-hour lectures built on theatrical "memorable moments" rather than 3–5 minute chunks, starting with C so students build hash tables and linked lists themselves before week 6 collapses it all into a Python dictionary ([teaching-and-communication](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md)) ([episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)). The episode also shows Harvard as an exhibit of institutional self-preservation in education: even during COVID Malan couldn't get Harvard to let students take a Stanford/[MIT](mit.md)/Yale course online, despite education's massive redundancy of thousands of teachers writing the same lectures ([episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)). On [AI](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md), CS50 bans ChatGPT/Copilot for problem sets but allows unlimited CS50.ai, a deliberately "less helpful" rubber-duck tutor; the course has always disciplined 5–10% of students per semester for academic dishonesty, and AI hasn't measurably increased detections — only made "prosecution" harder ([episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)).
