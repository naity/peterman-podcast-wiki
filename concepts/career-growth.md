# Career growth

The podcast's central theme: how software engineers actually grow from junior to staff, principal, distinguished, and executive levels — what drives the growth, what merely correlates with it, and what the people who reached the top would do differently.

## What the guests say

### Impact beats output, and outcomes beat intelligence

The most repeated finding across episodes is that code volume and raw intelligence are not the growth engine. Dwayne Reeves earned his highest-ever rating (and his IC5 promo) in the half where he wrote the *least* code of his career, by influencing others to adopt Hack typing — "the job is identifying and solving problems, not code output" ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)). Carey Nachenberg knew a 200-IQ engineer stuck at L4 at Google for lack of communication and business impact, and argues relentless focus on the company's and customer's *outcomes* matters more than being smart ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)). Ricky (Staff at Google by 28) puts it as "lines of code != impact — the industry rewards results, not effort" ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)), and Evan King's Staff promo came from a simple project with extraordinary results, not technical sophistication ([episode](../sources/staff-engineer-meta-by-age-25-evan.md)). Simon Kindström's fast IC3→IC6 run was powered by work with direct revenue impact, which "converted directly into top ratings and accelerated [promotions](promotions.md)" ([episode](../sources/26-year-old-meta-staff-eng-ic6-on.md)).

### Agency: the near-universal growth engine

Nearly every fast riser tells a story of doing valuable work nobody asked for. David Fowler's mantra is "code is currency" — troll forums for customer problems, build the prototype first, then show PMs; SignalR started as his nights-and-weekends project, and "you can just do things" ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)). David Singleton, as a junior Google engineer, ignored "it's too hard" pushback and removed the google.com mobile redirect, saving ~70 years of user time per year ([episode](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md)). Boris Cherny's "side quests" (Undux, a TypeScript book) drove his growth more than assigned work ([episode](../sources/boris-cherny-creator-of-claude-code.md)); Carey Nachenberg picked every project himself for two decades by "finding gaps" the business needed filled ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)); Brendan Burns says you can hide ~10% of your effort for self-directed bets, expecting portfolio economics where one hit out of five beats grinding for ratings ([episode](../sources/the-creator-of-kubernetes-on-building.md)); Laurent Charignon pitched his own move into management rather than waiting to be asked ([episode](../sources/airbnb-staff-eng-on-how-to-not-get.md)).

### Luck is real — but you can grow its surface area

Michael Novati estimates his career was "50/50 luck versus hard work — the only factor in your control" ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on-zuck.md)), and Jayendra Jog credits a random study night covering his exact interview question for his entire career: careers move in step functions, not lines ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). The counter-move, repeated by several guests, is expanding "luck surface area": Marius Schulz positioned himself loudly as the go-to web expert so the lucky opportunity landed on him ([episode](../sources/instagram-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on.md)); John Myles White jumped from "random IC5" to de facto TL of Meta's A/B testing framework when every senior engineer left within six months — "sudden-vacuum opportunities are underrated" ([episode](../sources/msl-eng-director-promo-hacking-industry.md)); Brian Attwell argues the deeper mechanism is superlinear returns: strategic work only goes to the already-proven ([episode](../sources/cloudkitchens-cto-on-intelligence.md)).

### Where you work dominates how hard you work

Ethan Evans's top advice is to always prefer high-growth companies — Amazon grew ~100x under him: "my ladder was an escalator" ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md)). Jake Bolam's version is "go where you're valued": his career stalled on a team that didn't value his frontend skillset and took off when he moved to one that did ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-engineer-ic8.md)). James Cowling says choose working with the best people over 20% more pay ([episode](../sources/dropboxs-former-most-senior-eng-building.md)); James Everingham regrets seven years in Pennsylvania "like an actor never trying Hollywood" ([episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)); David Ronca regrets six years coasting at large failing companies ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)); Jayendra Jog notes a third of his Robinhood new-grad class became venture-backed founders ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).

### Disagreement: hours worked

Brian Attwell reached Senior Staff at Uber by 25 on 90–100 hour weeks and claims career growth versus hours is superlinear — while admitting he once collapsed on a street walking home ([episode](../sources/cloudkitchens-cto-on-intelligence.md)). David Ronca is the direct counter: Netflix's Patty McCord told him "blow us away with what you can do in an eight hour day," and his own 24/7 startup years ended in stage-3 colon cancer ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). Rong Yan splits the difference: the best leaders win on *time allocation*, not hours ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)), and Jayendra Jog found that deliberate coasting in a hypergrowth company still produced promotions ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). Jake Bolam works ~40 hours a week at IC8 thanks to systems that protect focus time ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-engineer-ic8.md)).

### Disagreement: job hopping vs. long tenure

Steve Huynh advises hopping frequently early, then favoring stability at Senior+ ([episode](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)); Stefan Mai agrees hopping helps ambitious juniors but is a red flag at staff+ (moves every 18–24 months get second-guessed) — while warning that deep tenure's "credibility snowball can become a shackle" ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)). The long-tenure guests complicate this: David Fowler stayed 17+ years at Microsoft, turned down pre-IPO Twitter and GitHub, and says the tenure built judgment job-hoppers never get — yet would tell a mentee today to take the hot outside offer ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)). Carey Nachenberg's biggest regret is the inverse: imposter syndrome kept him at Symantec 21+ years after he stopped growing — "stay only as long as you're learning" ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)). Marc Brooker changed organizations 4 times inside Amazon and thinks 5–6 would have been optimal ([episode](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md)); David Singleton's test for staying was re-checking every 18–24 months that he was still learning ([episode](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md)).

### Specialize or generalize?

Ilya Grigorik's signature philosophy is "don't be the best, be the only": a portfolio of 80–90% competencies whose intersection makes you uniquely capable ([episode](../sources/distinguished-engineer-at-shopify.md)). David Fowler's mentor taught the adjacent lesson — amplify strengths to superhuman rather than fixing weaknesses: "no one gets promoted to these roles for being average" ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)); Sash Zats agrees: keep weaknesses out of your way and position yourself where strengths shine ([episode](../sources/instagram-staff-ic6-promo-despite.md)). Philip Su adds a stage-dependence: generalists win at smaller companies, specialists have more headroom at large ones ([episode](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md)), while Nimit Sohani warns against spreading thin at all: "focus on deep technical skills — a simple recipe that's very hard to follow" ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)).

## Practical takeaways

- Optimize for demonstrated business impact and communicate it well; writing about your accomplishments with the impactful parts first is a career lever in itself ([Simon Kindström](../sources/26-year-old-meta-staff-eng-ic6-on.md), [Austen McDonald](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)).
- Build the prototype before asking permission; a good track record buys leeway to fail ([David Fowler](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- Pick high-growth companies and talent-dense teams — the environment multiplies everything else ([Ethan Evans](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md), [James Cowling](../sources/dropboxs-former-most-senior-eng-building.md)).
- Expand luck surface area: become the loud, obvious go-to person for a domain, and take more shots ([Marius Schulz](../sources/instagram-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on.md), [Adrien Friggeri](../sources/new-grad-to-principal-engineer-ic8.md)).
- Audit every 18–24 months whether you are still learning; leave when the answer is no ([David Singleton](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md), [Carey Nachenberg](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
- The senior-to-staff jump is a mindset change — independently identify, pitch, and solve problems end to end rather than solving problems others chose ([Laurent Charignon](../sources/airbnb-staff-eng-on-how-to-not-get.md)).
- Match your specialty to business priorities; if your domain stops mattering to the org, move to where it does ([Marius Schulz](../sources/instagram-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on.md), [Jake Bolam](../sources/instagram-principal-engineer-ic8.md)).
