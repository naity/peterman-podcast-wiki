# Hiring and interviews

How tech companies actually decide who to hire and at what level — the coding/behavioral interview machinery, hiring committees, referrals, and leveling — told from both sides of the table by the interviewers, hiring-committee leads, and candidates who lived it.

## What the guests say

### Do LeetCode-style interviews measure anything real?

This is the sharpest disagreement on the podcast. [Brian Attwell](../entities/brian-attwell.md) defends the underlying idea: IQ correlating with job performance is "one of the most well-researched results in applied psychology," LeetCode is just a *gameable* proxy for it, and "an interview that anyone can pass regardless of IQ if they can practice enough does not build a strong engineering team" — he wants interviews that force on-the-spot reasoning (read a 5-page postmortem in 5 minutes, then debate restructuring the team) and is seriously considering literal IQ screens ([episode](../sources/cloudkitchens-cto-on-intelligence.md)). [David Ronca](../entities/david-ronca.md) flatly counters that LeetCode "does not tell you anything about an engineer": the innate foundation is understanding complex systems and making good decisions without enough data, and on that basis he successfully hired a train-automation engineer and a wastewater-treatment civil engineer into [Netflix](../entities/netflix.md)-caliber video roles — "the coding can be taught" ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). [Carey Nachenberg](../entities/carey-nachenberg.md) splits the difference from the other end: raw intelligence itself is overrated — he watched a 200-IQ engineer stall at L4 at [Google](../entities/google.md) for lack of communication and business impact ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).

The false-negative evidence is vivid: [Ryan Olson](../entities/ryan-olson.md) failed his 2011 Facebook internship interview so badly the interviewer cut it short and said "you're just not gonna be able to make it at Facebook" — he later reached IC8 at [Instagram](../entities/instagram.md), and needed prescribed beta blockers to get through full-time loops ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-eng-ic8-on-building.md)). Even the canon itself is shaky: [MIT](../entities/mit.md) complexity theorist [Ryan Williams](../entities/ryan-williams.md) shows the "optimal" n² answer to 3SUM, the most popular LeetCode question, can actually be beaten by roughly polylog factors ([episode](../sources/mit-complexity-theorist-on-leetcode.md)). [David Fowler](../entities/david-fowler.md) adds that interviews test writing fresh code while the higher-leverage real-world skill is debugging — he'd hand candidates a crash dump instead ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)). And [Stefan Mai](../entities/stefan-mai.md) reports the format is now breaking operationally: AI cheating tools have made remote interviews "horrible," anti-cheat only catches the unsophisticated, and he expects assessments to evolve (low-level design, AI-allowed assignments) rather than anti-cheat to win ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)).

### What actually decides senior offers: the behavioral interview

Practitioners converge on a deflating point: the technical rounds are a gate, not the decision. [Steve Huynh](../entities/steve-huynh.md) says coding and system design "just get you in the door" — behavioral interviews determine leveling and cultural fit ([episode](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)). [Austen McDonald](../entities/austen-mcdonald.md), who led mobile hiring at [Meta](../entities/meta.md), confirms from inside the committee room: the first thing he opened in a senior packet was the behavioral interview, because scope, influence, and insight drive leveling far more than coding performance; at staff+ levels technical rounds become pass/fail checkmarks and coding flubs are forgiven. Every story should hit three dimensions — technical depth, organizational depth, business impact — and Meta's rubric (built by industrial-psychology "selection scientists") scores five signals you can get from your recruiter in advance ([episode](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)). Mai adds employer-specific wrinkles: [OpenAI](../entities/openai.md)'s behavioral rounds want raw technical achievement — the staff-engineer "seven docs and six aligned teams" story lands poorly there ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)), while McDonald maps [Anthropic](../entities/anthropic.md)'s "Hold Light and Shade" to explicitly narrating risk assessment in AI project stories ([episode](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)).

Can you just fabricate scope? McDonald argues no: staff+ phone screens include scope-check project walkthroughs, calibrated senior interviewers expose vague or AI-generated stories with follow-ups, and in thousands of interviews he never saw a confirmed successful liar ([episode](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)). But he concedes the process is not purely objective either — likability bias "scales exactly with level," since senior roles are legitimately about influencing people.

### Leveling: negotiate up or deliberately take less?

McDonald says you can negotiate level mid-process — get the loop run at the target level, anchor every interviewer in your opening, tell your highest-scope stories — and warns that non-FAANG titles get downleveled hard (a "principal architect" managing 50-75 people elsewhere may map to managing 8-10 Meta engineers) ([episode](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)). Ronca argues the opposite optimization: when in doubt take the *lower* level — come in as an IC5 executing like an IC6 and the bonuses and refreshers follow; come in over-leveled and "there's no way to fix that problem" ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). [Igor](../entities/meta-ic7-demotion-guest.md)'s demotion story is the cautionary tale for the upside case: joining Meta at E7 meant racing a roughly one-year clock against tenured senior staff amid bottom-10% cuts, and he ultimately boomeranged to Google at a deliberately negotiated L6 ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)). Even eminence doesn't override the machine: Google leveled Nachenberg down from a Symantec Fellow (L10-equivalent) to L8 — "we can't just hire fellows" ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)) — and when Google acquired [Ilya Grigorik](../entities/ilya-grigorik.md)'s startup PostRank, the entire team including the founder/CTO still went through the full interview panel to determine levels ([episode](../sources/distinguished-engineer-at-shopify.md)).

### Referrals and networks vs. cold applications

The senior consensus is that past a certain level, the funnel inverts: jobs find you. Nachenberg was pulled into GoogleX by Symantec's ex-COO with no application and a loop of ~8 interviews containing no coding at all ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)). [Rong Yan](../entities/rong-yan.md) says senior recruiting is nearly all inbound via personal networks — he has never proactively job-searched ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)) — and [David Singleton](../entities/david-singleton.md) notes senior hires "almost always begin as advice conversations": he joined [Stripe](../entities/stripe.md) after asking Patrick Collison for startup advice ([episode](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md)). Huynh himself entered [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) through a friend's referral ([episode](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)).

McDonald quantifies what referrals actually buy: they matter most "on the bubble," where a VP/director who genuinely worked with you stakes reputation and can advocate inside the committee room (draft the referral content for them) — but no referral overrides a unanimous no-hire for engineers, though senior manager/director hiring "is a little bit more about who you know" ([episode](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)). For students, [Jia Chen](../entities/jia-chen.md) points to hackathons as a live recruiting pipeline where recruiters scout and fast-track interns ([episode](../sources/21x-hackathon-winner-turned-college.md)).

### Credentials vs. portfolio

Guests lean portfolio, with caveats. Fowler landed his [Microsoft](../entities/microsoft.md) internship by demoing a self-built game on a laptop at a career fair ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)). [Vlad Feinberg](../entities/vlad-feinberg.md) says the best signal for frontier-lab jobs is public evidence you built something useful — contributions to vLLM, SGLang, or TensorRT — and made a standing offer: do The Scaling Book exercises handwritten on video and he'll interview you for his DeepMind team ([episode](../sources/google-deepmind-pre-training-lead.md)). Chen picks hackathon teammates on GitHub activity and drive over resumes and tells non-target-school students to make *themselves* the outlier ([episode](../sources/21x-hackathon-winner-turned-college.md)). But Olson's experience shows portfolios work through people, not process: interviewers refused to even look at his open-source tool FLEX — it got him the Instagram job only because one engineer saw it, pulled him into the pipeline, and advocated after a mixed loop ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-eng-ic8-on-building.md)). On the credential side, [Dwayne Reeves](../entities/dwayne-reeves.md) is honest that MIT's value was opportunity access and name recognition rather than the education — equally capable colleagues without degrees reached Facebook only by chance ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)), and [Nimit Sohani](../entities/nimit-sohani.md) says a PhD is rarely strictly required but opens doors in AI research and quant, where you still need concrete evidence of the skill set ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)). [Ricky](../entities/ricky-staff-google.md) and host Ryan Peterman set the floor for new grads: resume projects are the bare minimum; persistence and early stints at smaller companies build the real signal ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)).

### The market backdrop

Huynh describes a bifurcated market — limited demand for junior roles, extraordinary demand for senior+, with the real competition concentrated in pre-screening rather than the interviews themselves ([episode](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)). Olson's answer to shrinking junior demand: "learn the tools of your time" — he broke in because iOS was new; today's equivalent is [AI tooling](ai-era-engineering.md), where new grads can out-skill seniors ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-eng-ic8-on-building.md)). The mirror of external hiring is internal [promotions](promotions.md) and [calibrations](calibrations-and-ratings.md) — committees pattern-match external candidates against the same scope bars.

## Practical takeaways

- Start behavioral prep when you start LeetCode, not after: at senior+ the behavioral rounds decide the offer and level, and a crisp "tell me about yourself" hitting technical depth, organizational depth, and business impact is the highest-leverage artifact ([McDonald](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md); [Huynh](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)).
- Choose stories by scope > relevance > recency > uniqueness; if 30 seconds pass without an action verb, restructure. Get the company's behavioral rubric from your recruiter beforehand ([McDonald](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)).
- Treat the recruiter call as an evaluation, avoid early compensation anchoring, and hold multiple simultaneous offers — the single biggest negotiation lever ([McDonald](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)); Fowler's advice to his younger self is the same: negotiate, get multiple offers ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- Think hard before pushing for the highest possible level: taking the lower level with room to over-execute avoids the over-leveled trap and the layoff-adjacent ramp clock ([Ronca](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md); [Igor](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)) — but if you do target up, get the whole loop run at that level rather than negotiating after ([McDonald](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)).
- Build public artifacts (open source, demos, exercises) *and* a human who will champion them — the artifact alone rarely survives the process ([Olson](../sources/instagram-principal-eng-ic8-on-building.md); [Feinberg](../sources/google-deepmind-pre-training-lead.md); [Fowler](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).
- From mid-career on, invest in your network before you need it: senior roles are filled by inbound calls and advice conversations, not applications ([Rong Yan](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md); [Singleton](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md); [Nachenberg](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)).
- Don't fabricate scope — calibrated interviewers expose it — but do draft your own referral for the senior person vouching for you ([McDonald](../sources/meta-hiring-lead-on-behind-the-scenes.md)).
