# Non-linear careers

Careers that refuse the ladder: voluntary demotions, lateral and downward moves, dropouts, boomerangs, blocked doors, and detours — and the podcast's recurring claim that careers move in step functions, not straight lines.

## What the guests say

### Stepping down on purpose

The podcast's two demotion stories bracket the possibilities. Philip Su *requested* a demotion from IC9 to IC7 at Meta because he wanted more technical work — it relieved the pressure of IC9 expectations and preserved his flexibility to keep switching between IC and EM (he did so six times) ([episode](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md)). Igor tried the same and hit a wall: Meta had no process for dropping a level within the same job category, so his E7→E6 request was refused and he instead boomeranged to Google at a deliberately negotiated *lower* level (L6) with a large pay cut, because he loves E5/E6 work — coding, debugging, mentoring — and not the meetings-and-docs job above it. His line to peers urging him upward: "I'm capable of running a marathon, but I don't like running" ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)). Ilya Grigorik made the equivalent move earlier in the arc: told he was on track for director in Google Analytics, he took a lateral, likely down-leveled IC role in "Make the Web Fast" for self-direction and a domain he loved — the right call in retrospect — and treats IC↔manager as "tours of duty" chosen per problem ([episode](../sources/distinguished-engineer-at-shopify.md)). Laurent Charignon, Simon Kindström, and Dwayne Reeves each also tried management and deliberately returned to IC ([episodes](../sources/airbnb-staff-eng-on-how-to-not-get.md), [26-year-old Meta Staff](../sources/26-year-old-meta-staff-eng-ic6-on.md), [Hack tech lead](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)) — collectively normalizing the pendulum against the one-way-ladder assumption.

### Doors that closed — and what opening looks like

Barbara Liskov's career is the canonical case: Princeton rejected her graduate application with a postcard saying "we do not admit women"; she entered CS as a programmer because it was her best job offer, and when no faculty offers followed her PhD she spent four years at MITRE retooling from AI to systems "without self-pity" — so when Title IX opened academia, "I was ready." Her framing: careers are doors opening and closing; you have to decide whether to step through ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)). Martin Hellman's detour ran the other way — he did cryptography *first*, becoming famous and financially secure, then spent decades on nuclear risk: "get famous and rich first, then save the world... the apparent detour was the shortest distance" ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).

### Dropouts, firings, and layoffs as inflection points

James Everingham was kicked out of Penn State at 18 with a 0.0 GPA while the university simultaneously hired him full-time because they were already using his shareware; the failed startup that sold to Yahoo later positioned him perfectly for the Instagram head-of-engineering job ([episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)). Jia Chen dropped out to found a company but warns against doing it on a whim — set preconditions first ([episode](../sources/21x-hackathon-winner-turned-college.md)). The anonymous chief architect, laid off after 25 years, did a "switcheroo" to put himself on the list in place of junior engineers and is now exploring semi-retirement and YouTube — an unplanned exit reframed as an opening ([episode](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)). Ethan Evans's entire arc runs through being fired twice ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md)).

### Step functions, boomerangs, and side doors

Jayendra Jog's thesis is explicit: careers move in step functions, not linearly — a lucky interview question, a hypergrowth company, an IPO, a founding. He argues engineers vastly over-index on the risk of quitting: competent people always find work, and a failed founding year makes you *more* valuable to startups ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). Sash Zats calls leaving Meta for startups "a two-way door: financially irrational but a real test of what your ideas are worth, and big tech will hire you back" ([episode](../sources/instagram-staff-ic6-promo-despite.md)) — which Igor's re-hire and Adrien Friggeri's two Meta boomerangs (both via former managers; he has never applied for a job through a website) empirically confirm ([episodes](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md), [New grad to IC8](../sources/new-grad-to-principal-engineer-ic8.md)). Rong Yan shows the sideways-up move: joining smaller Square directly at director — "smaller companies will let you perform a level above" — then Snapchat, then CTO ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).

### The counterpoint: the linear career, examined

David Fowler is the corpus's most linear career — intern to Distinguished Engineer, 11 promotions, 17+ years at Microsoft — and even he complicates the picture: he turned down better pre-IPO Twitter and GitHub offers, was "depressed for a week" when Twitter IPO'd, and says that despite tenure working for him, he would tell a mentee today to take the hot outside offer ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)). The disagreement between his lived path and his given advice is the concept in miniature: linearity worked, but he wouldn't prescribe it.

## Practical takeaways

- Down-leveling and demotion are legitimate tools for matching your job to the work you love — but companies often have no process for it, so you may need to move companies to move down ([Igor](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md), [Philip Su](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md)).
- Treat IC↔manager as reversible tours of duty chosen per problem, not an identity change ([Ilya Grigorik](../sources/distinguished-engineer-at-shopify.md)).
- Leaving big tech is usually a two-way door; the boomerang path is real and often runs through former managers ([Sash Zats](../sources/instagram-staff-ic6-promo-despite.md), [Adrien Friggeri](../sources/new-grad-to-principal-engineer-ic8.md)).
- When a door closes, use the interregnum to retool deliberately so you're ready when one opens ([Barbara Liskov](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)).
- Smaller companies let you perform a level above your big-tech title — a lateral move can be a promotion in disguise ([Rong Yan](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
- Don't over-index on the risk of jumping; competent people find work again, and failure teaches skills ladders never do ([Jayendra Jog](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
