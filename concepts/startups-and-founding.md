# Startups and founding

Leaving big tech (or school) to start a company — when to jump, how to fund it, what founding actually demands, and how startup life compares to the big-tech ladder. Roughly a third of the podcast's guests have founded something; their accounts range from triumphant to explicitly disillusioning.

## What the guests say

### Big tech first, or startup first?

An explicit disagreement. Ricky and host Ryan Peterman would both choose big tech straight out of college — instant credibility and foundational skills, then pivot to startups later with brand equity; they add that big tech alone won't make you crazy rich, since exceptional wealth requires breakout startup opportunities ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)). Evan King argues the reverse: "startups promote faster technical growth than big tech does" — big tech gives depth, startups breadth ([episode](../sources/staff-engineer-meta-by-age-25-evan.md)). Jayendra Jog splits the difference by objective: max compensation upside requires a very early company that hits big, learning is fastest at a small company growing quickly, and the best controllable heuristic is surrounding yourself with really smart people — a third of his 2018 Robinhood new-grad class went on to found venture-backed companies ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). Mike Stonebraker chose academia-plus-startups over big tech entirely, because big companies impose bosses, rules, and politicking ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-postgres-disagreeing.md)).

### Deciding to jump

Jog argues engineers over-index on the risk of quitting: competent people always find work again, and a failed founding attempt makes you *more* valuable to early-stage startups because you learn product validation, marketing, and go-to-market — though golden handcuffs held him 3.5 years because exercising his options would have cost $400,000 ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). Sash Zats frames leaving Meta as a two-way door: financially irrational but a real test of what your ideas are worth, and big tech will hire you back ([episode](../sources/instagram-staff-ic6-promo-despite.md)). Bryan Cantrill's version: boom/bust cycles are endemic, so be your own judge — people said you couldn't start a computer company in 2019, and he's glad he ignored them ([episode](../sources/distinguished-eng-on-stack-ranking.md)). The sobering counterweight is Adrien Friggeri, who left Meta in 2018 to found a startup and learned he wasn't an entrepreneur — founders must delegate the interesting hard problems and scale through others: "I left the company to do a startup, and I ended up taking a sabbatical instead" ([episode](../sources/new-grad-to-principal-engineer-ic8.md)). James Cowling's motivation ran the other way: promotion systems that reward complexity angered him into founding Convex ([episode](../sources/dropboxs-former-most-senior-eng-building.md)).

On what founding requires, Jog is deflationary: "a competent L4 can do perfectly fine" since most businesses face mundane problems ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). Ryan Olson prices the tradeoff honestly: satisfaction and learning way up, compensation way down — he and his Retro co-founder pay themselves less than every employee ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-eng-ic8-on-building.md)). Rong Yan tells HeyGen candidates upfront there's only a ~1% chance of huge success, deliberately filtering for people who stay through hard cycles ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).

### Fundraising

Jog raised $35M three weeks after the Terra collapse; his lesson is to land a tier-one lead investor, after which follow-on funding and BD herd behind the stamp ([episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)). Charlie Marsh never pitched: investors preemptively initiated all three Astral raises, and one investor's "you can give the money back in six months" framing lowered the pressure to start; he calls most startup advice survivorship bias — pick your principles (remote, solo founder, minimal fundraising time) and stick to them ([episode](../sources/openai-eng-and-dev-tools-founder.md)). Cantrill's contrasting experience: a VC who had emailed "I will fund literally anything you put in front of me" refused Oxide's computer-company pitch in 45 seconds; when a VC asks "how can I help?", have a concrete answer — his was an intro to the founder of a failed similar startup, from whom he learned it died of a tiny real market plus premature go-to-market scaling ([episode](../sources/distinguished-eng-on-stack-ranking.md)). David Singleton's networking corollary: "anytime you want money, ask for advice; anytime you want advice, ask for money" — his advice conversation with Patrick Collison became a recruiting pitch that made him Stripe's CTO ([episode](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md)).

### Where startup ideas come from

Three repeatable patterns. Commercializing internal big-tech infra: John Myles White observes Statsig is essentially Meta's Deltoid and Airflow literally is Meta's DataSwarm — "a repeatable startup playbook" he regrets not running himself ([episode](../sources/msl-eng-director-promo-hacking-industry.md)); James Everingham's Guild productizes the governance layer his Meta dev-infra org needed around agents ([episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)). Comparative perspective across ecosystems: Marsh saw JavaScript embrace native tooling while Python lagged, and shipped the Ruff prototype nine days after the blog post that tested the thesis ([episode](../sources/openai-eng-and-dev-tools-founder.md)). Research conviction: Nimit Sohani argues big labs suffer groupthink and risk-aversion while startups can challenge orthodoxy (Mamba, Cartesia's H-Nets), but the durable position is the product-research intersection — research-only startups are all-or-nothing and product-only wrappers have no moat ([episode](../sources/stanford-phd-ai-researcher-and-quant.md)). Everingham's filter for any AI-era idea: don't build "for the future past," filling gaps the next model closes — "you build a product on a bus stop, not where the end of the line is" ([episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)). Stonebraker's founding pattern is oldest of all: Ingres Corporation (1980) existed because academic software couldn't be adopted commercially ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-postgres-disagreeing.md)).

### Building the company: culture, scaling, truth-seeking

Rong Yan: every doubling of a team is "almost like building a new company," and old cultural assumptions must be dropped — he watched Snapchat grow from ~100 to ~3,000 in two years ([episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)). Brian Attwell's CloudKitchens offers the podcast's most developed account of engineering a truth-seeking culture: after Travis Kalanick rejected an Uber-style leveling ladder as lazily over-indexed on scope, Attwell distilled ~1,000 real performance examples into 250 attributes and PCA-collapsed them into 12 skill gradients that reward *being right when questioning foundational decisions* rather than position-oriented, politics-rewarding competencies like "influencing other teams." His broader claim: standards fall entropically until a company dies or a trauma resets them, so startups must institutionalize honesty early; his deepest regret is a moment he knew the truth mid-project and stayed silent (killing his own project in front of Steve Jobs) ([episode](../sources/cloudkitchens-cto-on-intelligence.md)). CloudKitchens also illustrates strategic stealth — staying dark to avoid press distraction and copycats while chasing the $2.5T US food market ([episode](../sources/cloudkitchens-cto-on-intelligence.md)). Opinionated product principles as moat: Olson's Retro is friends-only, ad-free ("ads plus algorithmic feeds create misaligned, 'a little bit evil' incentives"), capped at 250 friends, and subscription-funded ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-eng-ic8-on-building.md)). Ilya Grigorik, whose PostRank was acquired by Google, notes even acquisitions re-interview the whole team for leveling, and that live investor conversations taught him more than entrepreneurship coursework ([episode](../sources/distinguished-engineer-at-shopify.md)). Jia Chen's founder basics from the hackathon circuit: ship continuously, avoid burnout, stay open to connections that don't seem immediately useful ([episode](../sources/21x-hackathon-winner-turned-college.md)).

## Practical takeaways

- Don't over-index on the risk of quitting: failure teaches go-to-market and makes you more valuable to early-stage companies (Jog [episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md); Zats [episode](../sources/instagram-staff-ic6-promo-despite.md)).
- Before founding, honestly test whether you want to delegate the interesting problems — that's the job (Friggeri [episode](../sources/new-grad-to-principal-engineer-ic8.md)).
- Get a tier-one lead investor; everything else herds behind the stamp (Jog [episode](../sources/quitting-robinhood-and-raising-35m.md)).
- Treat startup advice as survivorship bias; pick your own principles and hold them (Marsh [episode](../sources/openai-eng-and-dev-tools-founder.md)).
- When a VC offers help, have a concrete ask — e.g. an intro to a failed competitor's founder (Cantrill [episode](../sources/distinguished-eng-on-stack-ranking.md)).
- Mine internal big-tech infra for validated startup ideas (White [episode](../sources/msl-eng-director-promo-hacking-industry.md); Everingham [episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)).
- Design incentive systems that reward truth-seeking over politics before entropy sets the culture for you (Attwell [episode](../sources/cloudkitchens-cto-on-intelligence.md)).
- Expect to rebuild the company culturally at every doubling (Rong Yan [episode](../sources/frontline-manager-at-meta-to-senior.md)).
