# Layoffs

Layoffs — mass cuts, quota-driven "unregretted attrition," PIPs, and firings of one — recur across the podcast as the dark counterpart to promotion culture: guests who ran the machinery, guests who were cut by it, and guests who watched a company shrink through 35 rounds of it.

## What the guests say

### Why the cuts are happening

[Asian Dad Energy](../entities/asian-dad-energy.md), laid off from a big-tech chief architect role after 25 years in industry, offers the bluntest macro theory: companies under pressure to show returns on massive AI investment cut headcount to keep revenue flat while dropping costs, earning "AI efficiency" PR that pumps the stock — and he expects more of it ([episode](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)). [John Myles White](../entities/john-myles-white.md) frames the same shift as a structural power flip: the engineer labor market went from chronic undersupply to perceived oversupply (frontier-lab AI researchers excepted), so employers across Silicon Valley make fewer retention sacrifices in comp, perks, and employee voice — layoff fear now dominates employee stress and makes previously "unforgivable" perk cuts tolerable ([episode](../sources/msl-eng-director-promo-hacking-industry.md)). [David Ronca](../entities/david-ronca.md) argues the 2022-era contraction was predictable overcorrection: Bill Gates's deliberate hiring freeze in *12 Secrets to Microsoft Success* taught a lesson FAANG leaders forgot during the 2021 overhiring ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). [Bryan Cantrill](../entities/bryan-cantrill.md) supplies the long view: booms and busts are endemic to software — Sun lost 98% of its value after the dot-com bust and ran ~35 rounds of layoffs over eight years, with engineers scripting the employee directory into a daily "obits" report; in his judgment McNealy's *delay* in starting layoffs was the mistake ([episode](../sources/distinguished-eng-on-stack-ranking.md)).

### Who gets cut: performance, politics, or spreadsheet arbitrariness?

Guests genuinely disagree here. [Steve Huynh](../entities/steve-huynh.md) observed that [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) layoffs typically hit people lacking *cultural fit* rather than poor performers ([episode](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)). [Stefan Mai](../entities/stefan-mai.md) describes a more mechanical reality: low-performer quotas are universal at scale — roughly 7-15% (up to 20%) pushed into the bottom bucket each cycle — and managers are *first* on the chopping block when companies target manager-to-IC ratios and span-of-control reductions ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)). [Igor](../entities/meta-ic7-demotion-guest.md) counters that selection can be entirely orthogonal to performance: [Google](../entities/google.md)'s Atlanta office shutdown cut a few hundred people because one senior VP sponsor pulled out and nobody would take the headcount — "at the end of the day, I'm just a cell in the spreadsheet" ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)). Asian Dad Energy's story shows the list is also *malleable*: he learned through his network he was on it, then did a "switcheroo" to put himself at the top in place of more junior engineers, buying them a few months ([episode](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)). And Cantrill argues quota systems corrupt selection upstream: stack ranking is "organizational cancer" that incentivizes managers to keep dead weight as sacrificial fodder for the next cut — which he watched happen at Sun — while teaching you "that your team are adversaries" ([episode](../sources/distinguished-eng-on-stack-ranking.md)). [Ethan Evans](../entities/ethan-evans.md) adds the individual-scale version: a manager can fire any *one* person they choose, striking first with the narrative (700 pull requests can be spun as upleveling the team or as nitpicking), and HR will not rescue you ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md)).

### Can you avoid it by being visible and communicating?

Huynh argues yes, partially: communicate directly with your manager about expectations — early, transparent feedback prevents surprises ([episode](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)). Evans supports the visibility thesis from the executive chair: quiet, dutiful high performers get unconsciously deferred for resources because management assumes they'll accept less — "you are a very nice person... and that is not a compliment in this context" ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-reveals-everything-hes.md)). But Igor's Atlanta story cuts against the whole premise — when a sponsor pulls out, an entire office goes regardless of anyone's visibility ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)) — and Asian Dad Energy's experience suggests the realistic value of a network is *advance warning*, not immunity ([episode](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)). Igor also flags a structural exposure: joining a company at a senior level starts a roughly one-year clock to match tenured peers before bottom-10% cut culture catches you ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)). Mai punctures the common IC assumption that becoming a manager is a hedge — it's the opposite during span-of-control reductions ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)).

### PIPs and "unregretted attrition": escapable or a formality?

Evans is maximally cynical: Amazon's unregretted-attrition goal (~4-7%/year) returned after an experiment showed managers stop having hard conversations without it, and PIPs are self-fulfilling — every person he coached through one was fired anyway, so "spend that time on the PIP looking for your next job" ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md)). Mai, who administered the machinery at both Amazon and [Meta](../entities/meta.md), pushes back: PIPs are first a legal paper trail, but people genuinely escape them *because* the trail loses legal credibility if nobody survives — Amazon HR was "savage" box-checking while Meta HR (in his era) exhausted every avenue before termination to protect its employer brand ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)). Huynh notes Amazon also runs an explicit up-and-out policy for SDE1s — promotion within a timeframe or termination — that relaxes at higher levels ([episode](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)).

### The aftermath — and the severance twist

Asian Dad Energy details a structured, generous package (garden leave, months of severance, PTO payout, education stipends), believes severance is technically negotiable but wasn't in the mental state to try, and found the post-layoff market cold enough that he pivoted to semi-retirement and YouTube — the viral layoff vlog began as therapy ([episode](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)). Cantrill saw severance invert the incentive entirely: late-Sun engineers *lobbied to get on the layoff list* for the rich package, while he quit Oracle within ~45 days out of shame ([episode](../sources/distinguished-eng-on-stack-ranking.md)). Evans reframes getting cut as survivable and even formative: he was let go twice around the .com bust — once as a "layoff of one" for abrasiveness — and an interviewer's remark that truly valuable people get kept triggered the soft-skills rebuild that eventually made him a VP ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md)). The financial-preparedness thread runs throughout: Ronca urges financial planning from day one ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)), and Igor notes he could only be publicly honest about his setback because he had no visa dependency, no mortgage, and years of runway ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)). On job security generally, [Ricky](../entities/ricky-staff-google.md) and Ryan Peterman's 2025 read was that AI leaves SWE jobs secure for roughly five years, with the longer term unpredictable ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)).

## Practical takeaways

- Have the expectations conversation with your manager early and often; surprises in a cut list usually trace back to feedback that was never delivered ([Huynh](../sources/amazon-principal-engineer-on-layoffs.md)).
- Maintain a real network inside and outside your company — in practice it buys advance warning of rounds and lists ([Asian Dad Energy](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)), and Evans's broader point stands: build reputation and relationships early so companies call *you* when things go wrong ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md)).
- If you're put on a PIP, job hunt immediately — treat survival as the exception ([Evans](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md)) — while knowing escape is genuinely possible, especially at companies protective of their employer brand ([Mai](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)).
- Don't become a manager as a layoff hedge: span-of-control reductions put managers first on the chopping block ([Mai](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)).
- Joining at a senior level is a bet with a ~1-year ramp clock against tenured peers under bottom-bucket quotas; weigh taking a lower level ([Igor](../sources/meta-senior-staff-ic7-engs-honest.md)).
- Build financial runway from day one — savings, index funds, always taking "free money" like ESPP and 401k match — because packages are good but markets after mass cuts are cold ([Ronca](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md); [Asian Dad Energy](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)).
- Ask about severance terms and negotiate if you can; and if you're an executive reading a list, remember it's more malleable than the official narrative admits ([Asian Dad Energy](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)).
