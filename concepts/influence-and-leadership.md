# Influence and leadership

How engineers get other people — peers, skeptical teams, executives, whole industries — to adopt their ideas without (or beyond) formal authority. It is the defining skill of the staff+ levels on this podcast, and the guests disagree productively about how it's done: persuasion vs. proof, bottom-up adoption vs. top-down mandate, and how hard you can push before influence curdles into being "annoying."

## What the guests say

### The core playbook: make yes the easy answer

[Adam Ernst](../entities/adam-ernst.md) ([Meta](../entities/meta.md) IC9) has the most explicit influence-without-authority recipe: talk in person rather than in writing, open by sympathizing with the other side, do overwhelming research (his team disassembled [Apple](../entities/apple.md)'s closed-source Core Data to prove why it couldn't scale), and above all "do the work for them" — complete the migration yourself so teams only have to say yes ([episode](../sources/meta-distinguished-eng-ic9-on-influencing.md)). [Laurent Charignon](../entities/laurent-charignon.md) ran a 140-engineer org at [Stripe](../entities/stripe.md) as uber tech lead "by asking questions, simply asking questions and getting people to change their mind themselves" — he claims he never outright rejected a design, embedded in each team for weeks to build credibility, and refused to justify decisions with "it worked at Facebook" ([episode](../sources/airbnb-staff-eng-on-how-to-not-get.md)). [Joakim Recht](../entities/joakim-recht.md) defines the win condition: the best influence outcome is overhearing someone champion, as their own conviction, an idea they once rejected from you ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).

Other guests weaponize artifacts over persuasion. [Sash Zats](../entities/sash-zats.md) argues prototypes are the highest-fidelity communication medium — "you can have 500 decks or you can have one prototype that works," and his GPT-3-era RAG demo drew ~50 cold reach-outs across Meta ([episode](../sources/instagram-staff-ic6-promo-despite.md)). [Michael Bolin](../entities/michael-bolin.md) built the Buck build system against near-universal advice by deliberately scope-couching it ("I'm *just* making an Android build system") and borrowing credibility from a respected supporter, then letting adoption come to him — the iOS team asked for it a year later ([episode](../sources/openai-codex-tech-lead-on-how-his.md)). [Boris Cherny](../entities/boris-cherny.md) seeds consensus structurally: give experts a straw man instead of an open question, befriend the strongest skeptics of a migration first, and — for one org-wide data-model fight — run a 3-hour two-team design competition whose 80%-identical outputs created instant alignment ([episode](../sources/boris-cherny-creator-of-claude-code.md)). [Adrien Friggeri](../entities/adrien-friggeri.md) applies the same pre-work to [promotions](promotions.md): socialize a principal-promo case with VPs a cycle early and convert the most critical D2 into a monthly mentor, so "you don't have a debate in the room" ([episode](../sources/new-grad-to-principal-engineer-ic8.md)).

### Bottom-up adoption vs. top-down mandate

Recht's platform (Odin, which grew to run ~500,000 databases at [Uber](../entities/uber.md)) is the canonical bottom-up story — prove it on your own team, expand through friendly orgs to skeptics — yet he concedes the ending: a final top-down mandate ("you will not be allowed to have your own service any longer") was still necessary, because full alignment never happens bottom-up alone. The same episode shows the failure mode of mandates *without* earned credibility: an Uber tracing mandate was re-emailed for ~3 years and never got done ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)). Friggeri's gentler mechanism: capture newcomers at bootcamp in a fast-growing company and let network effects convert the stragglers, while supporting the legacy system to build trust ([episode](../sources/new-grad-to-principal-engineer-ic8.md)).

[Brendan Burns](../entities/brendan-burns.md) took giving up control to its logical extreme: Kubernetes won partners by offering a real seat at the design table, and [Google](../entities/google.md) deliberately donated it to the CNCF with a literal written governance constitution so no one company could dominate — influence through relinquishing authority ([episode](../sources/the-creator-of-kubernetes-on-building.md); see [open-source](open-source.md)). [James Everingham](../entities/james-everingham.md) regrets the opposite call on Libra: years of re-architecting to satisfy regulators who still blocked it — "we probably should have just released it and asked for forgiveness" ([episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)). [James Cowling](../entities/james-cowling.md) offers a synthesis for org conflict: demand 100% alignment on *why*, then trust the team completely on *how* ([episode](../sources/dropboxs-former-most-senior-eng-building.md)).

### Technical excellence doesn't win by itself

Ernst's two-year ComponentScript framework "checked every technical box" and still "was a total and complete failure": it targeted no specific engineer persona, he refused to compromise while a less-principled rival shipped, and he went wide instead of landing one committed anchor team — "just because it was technically excellent, didn't mean it was going to win" ([episode](../sources/meta-distinguished-eng-ic9-on-influencing.md)). Everingham learned the macro version at Netscape when [Microsoft](../entities/microsoft.md) bundled IE for free: "it's not a technology war, it's a distribution war" ([episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)). [Carey Nachenberg](../entities/carey-nachenberg.md) saw the individual version — a 200-IQ engineer stuck at L4 at Google for poor communication and no business impact ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)) — and [Ricky](../entities/ricky-staff-google.md) generalizes it: the best engineers weren't the top programmers from college but the ones who could influence, collaborate, and write concisely ([episode](../sources/industry-secrets-we-wish-we-knew.md)). Bolin adds that influence skill is project-relative: after his E8 promo he flopped on an alignment-heavy "hero quest" fixing facebook.com speed, concluding he wins only on write-lots-of-code-from-scratch problems ([episode](../sources/openai-codex-tech-lead-on-how-his.md)).

### Credibility comes from staying close to the work

[Marc Brooker](../entities/marc-brooker.md) ([AWS](../entities/aws.md) Distinguished Engineer) states the hands-on rule for the AI era: "if you aren't doing it hands on, your opinion about it is very likely to be completely wrong" ([episode](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md)). Recht explains *why* authority without demonstrated understanding fails: engineers give polite nods and silent non-compliance — "people will say, yeah, that's a pretty good idea, and then completely gaslight you" ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)). [David Ronca](../entities/david-ronca.md) learned it as an incoming [Netflix](../entities/netflix.md)-to-Meta director when a report told him, two weeks in, "you've been here two weeks, you're already telling us what we need to do" — build trust before direction ([episode](../sources/retired-netflix-engineering-director.md)). [Michael Novati](../entities/michael-novati.md) treats trust as the code machine's currency, once publicly staking his resignation on a risky change to rebuild credibility after a rushed bug ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on-zuck.md)), and Charignon warns that one confident wrong claim costs all of it: "it's much better to say I don't know than to say something wrong" ([episode](../sources/airbnb-staff-eng-on-how-to-not-get.md)).

Leading from the front divides guests, though. [Ryan Olson](../entities/ryan-olson.md) admires Mike Krieger coding the Stories neon brush and reviewing diffs at 2 AM with the team ([episode](../sources/instagram-principal-eng-ic8-on-building.md)), while Cowling argues leading by example is *passive* — his team concluded on-call heroics were "James's job" — and real leadership is explicit teaching and delegated ownership ([episode](../sources/dropboxs-former-most-senior-eng-building.md)).

### How hard to push: strategic annoyance vs. not being annoying

[Ethan Evans](../entities/ethan-evans.md) argues influence and politics are the same skill set distinguished only by motive ("Darth Vader had executive presence in spades"), and that pushing works if worded unassailably — "my career is very important to me, and I need to know if it's as important to [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md)" — admitting leaders often promote the polite agitator over the patient team player. His refinement is being "strategically annoying" rather than abrasive: if people stop disagreeing to your face, they organize behind your back ([episode](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md); [politics episode](../sources/amazon-vp-reveals-everything-hes.md)). [Bobby Holley](../entities/bobby-holley.md) counters from the [Mozilla](../entities/mozilla.md) CTO chair: promo-hacking and box-checking pressure is "extremely annoying" to leadership — "you don't want the people in leadership to find you to be annoying" — and ambition should be framed as impact and team credit, not status ([episode](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)). Bolin's delayed E9 promo is the cautionary middle case: he pushed too aggressively on a correct prediction (Nuclide's Atom foundation losing to VS Code), and the coaching was about *how* he pushed, not what he argued ([episode](../sources/openai-codex-tech-lead-on-how-his.md)).

On contrarian courage, [Martin Hellman](../entities/martin-hellman.md) is the existence proof — every colleague called him crazy to publish cryptography against the NSA's wishes, and he argues the best work looks crazy a priori ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)) — but [David Patterson](../entities/david-patterson.md) attaches the invoice: fortune favors the bold and someone must stand up to weak arguments even from leaders, yet "friends come and go, enemies accumulate" ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-tpu-vs-gpu-vs.md)), an aphorism Everingham repeats almost verbatim from his own career ([episode](../sources/ex-head-of-eng-at-instagram-career.md)). Recht's deflationary view helps calibrate the whole game: at senior levels "nobody's in control, there's an illusion of control" — so say no a lot and avoid dead-end fights ([episode](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).

### Communication is the leverage multiplier

Brooker calls writing the way to "scale out the impact of your expertise in space and time," writing docs he never shares just to sharpen thinking ([episode](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md)); Holley agrees writing *is* thinking — and warns against letting an LLM write for you, because you lose the thinking that drives growth ([episode](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)). [David Singleton](../entities/david-singleton.md) adds the spoken side: senior leaders are "always on stage," speaking improves only with deliberate reps, and dynamic range is what makes you believable ([episode](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md)). Nachenberg's blunt observation closes the loop: people equate communication skill with intelligence, and opportunities flow accordingly ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)). [Marius Schulz](../entities/marius-schulz.md) shows what leverage looks like on the ladder: the IC6 way to fix a problem is not fixing it yourself but writing the direction-setting note and lint rules so the entire company applies the fix without you ([episode](../sources/instagram-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on.md)) — the archetypes Holley cites as Distinguished Engineers (HTTP/2, WebAssembly, AV1 authors) are the same move at industry scale ([episode](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)). See [teaching-and-communication](teaching-and-communication.md).

## Practical takeaways

- Before asking a team to adopt your idea, do the work for them: research their constraints deeply, show up with the diagnosis (or the finished migration), and make yes the cheap answer ([Ernst](../sources/meta-distinguished-eng-ic9-on-influencing.md)).
- Prefer a working prototype or a straw-man proposal to a deck or an open-ended question — artifacts convert skeptics faster than persuasion ([Zats](../sources/instagram-staff-ic6-promo-despite.md), [Cherny](../sources/boris-cherny-creator-of-claude-code.md)).
- Launching a disruptive project without credibility: scope-couch it, borrow a respected ally's credibility, prove it on one committed anchor team, and let adoption pull — but expect the last 10% to require a mandate ([Bolin](../sources/openai-codex-tech-lead-on-how-his.md), [Recht](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md), [Ernst](../sources/meta-distinguished-eng-ic9-on-influencing.md)).
- Influence by asking questions rather than issuing verdicts, and never bluff — "I don't know" preserves credibility; one confident wrong claim destroys it ([Charignon](../sources/airbnb-staff-eng-on-how-to-not-get.md)).
- Stay hands-on in whatever you claim opinions about; authority without demonstrated understanding earns silent non-compliance ([Brooker](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md), [Recht](../sources/uber-distinguished-eng-on-unfair.md)).
- Push for what you want with unassailable wording and warm delivery — but audit whether your pushing reads as strategic or merely annoying to the leaders deciding your case ([Evans](../sources/amazon-vp-on-promotions-getting-fired.md), [Holley](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md), [Bolin](../sources/openai-codex-tech-lead-on-how-his.md)).
- Invest in writing as your scaling mechanism — direction-setting docs, postmortems, and self-clarifying drafts move more engineers than any meeting ([Brooker](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md), [Schulz](../sources/instagram-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on.md)).
