# Working with legends

Several guests worked directly alongside the most famous names in computing — Dennis Ritchie, Edsger Dijkstra, Mark Zuckerberg, John Carmack, Jeff Dean — and several guests (Lamport, Liskov, Hellman, Stroustrup) *are* the legends. The episodes probe what these people are actually like up close, whether they differ in kind or only in focus and luck, and whether proximity to a famous collaborator lifts a career or swallows the credit.

## What the guests say

**What legends look like up close.** [Philip Su](../entities/philip-su.md) says what distinguished Mark Zuckerberg was "constant devotion to growth," and that John Carmack was among the most impressive engineers he ever worked with for sheer prolific ability — Carmack could drop into a codebase he knew nothing about and give excellent feedback ([episode](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md)). [Michael Novati](../entities/michael-novati.md)'s Zuckerberg is more human: they built emoji-reactions at a 2009 hackathon and the code was too bad to ship — though the idea became the industry norm — and a company-wide bet once got Zuckerberg to personally merge a PR in exchange for Novati giving up office ripsticking ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on-zuck.md)). [Bjarne Stroustrup](../entities/bjarne-stroustrup.md)'s Dennis Ritchie was a lunch companion for years at [Bell Labs](../entities/bell-labs.md) — a place that hired him with no formal interview and a job description of "do something interesting in a year's time" — who helped design C++'s `const` and endorsed C++ as C's successor while their communities fought "ridiculous" language wars ([episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)). [Leslie Lamport](../entities/leslie-lamport.md)'s one collaboration with Dijkstra — simplifying a concurrent garbage-collection algorithm — earned him unexpected co-authorship for an idea Lamport thought obvious; "Dijkstra was smart enough to realize that" it wasn't ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)).

**Different in kind, or just focus and luck?** The legends themselves mostly refuse the different-in-kind story. Lamport insists his gift "is not in some sense raw intelligence. It's abstraction" — a specific skill he only realized in the last decade that he had more of than others ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)). [Barbara Liskov](../entities/barbara-liskov.md) frames her Turing-winning insight as timing: "there's sort of a time when an idea is ready and I happened to see it," and she entered the field at all only because Princeton rejected women and programming was her best remaining job offer ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)). [Martin Hellman](../entities/martin-hellman.md) locates it in temperament plus substrate: every colleague told him he was crazy to fight the NSA, and simultaneous invention (him/Diffie, Merkle, GCHQ) shows the moment matters as much as the mind ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)). Novati agrees on contingency — he rates his own rapid rise "50/50" luck (landing at 2009 Facebook) versus hard work ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on-zuck.md)). But the observers push back: Su's account of Carmack is a difference in kind ([episode](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md)), and Novati reports that in [Meta](../entities/meta.md)'s IC7+-only group, every archetype shared extreme conscientiousness, attention to detail, and a sharpness that moved through new technical proposals in minutes ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on-zuck.md)) — traits he treats as real, if trainable. See [imposter-syndrome](imposter-syndrome.md) for the flip side of this debate.

**Does proximity to a legend lift you or overshadow you?** Both, and the episodes disagree on which dominates. On the lifted side: [Vlad Feinberg](../entities/vlad-feinberg.md)'s spot bonus from Jeff Dean at [Google](../entities/google.md) came from deliberately unglamorous work — hyperparameter tuning and "golfing the XLA compiler" for the first Bard release — after his manager pushed him off maximizing first-author papers; the menial-but-important engagement snowballed into leading Gemini Flash pre-training ([episode](../sources/google-deepmind-pre-training-lead.md)). Stroustrup built C++ literally on Ritchie's work ("we can have Dennis Ritchie's mistakes, which we know... so we'll take Dennis's") and benefited from his written endorsement ([episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)). Lamport's Paxos survived eight years of rejection because one influential peer, Butler Lampson, grasped it and proselytized ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)). On the overshadowed side, Liskov is the cautionary tale: Viewstamped Replication and Paxos are "essentially the same idea," but Lamport "went around giving all the talks and she implemented it," to the point that Google misattributed its own file system's replication to Paxos ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)). Hellman's ledger is similar: Ralph Merkle's independent invention of public-key distribution was dismissed by his own Berkeley professor and rejected by CACM, and Stanford's badly drafted patent let RSA sell for $250M while the actual inventors made virtually nothing ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)). The lesson the two threads share: proximity pays when you attach usefulness to the legend's mission; credit does not allocate itself. See [teaching-and-communication](teaching-and-communication.md) on why the person who gives the talks wins the mindshare.

**How should you behave around them — and how should they behave around you?** [Dwayne Reeves](../entities/dwayne-reeves.md), who worked with type theorist Andrew Kennedy on Hack at Meta, distills it to: don't fear pedigree — you always have value to add — and accomplished seniors owe juniors approachability and humility in return ([episode](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)). The legends model both poles: Dijkstra "was not the most diplomatic person" yet won his arguments ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)) and was generous with co-authorship ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)); Hellman actively rescued the dismissed Merkle by recruiting him to [Stanford](../entities/stanford.md) and rewriting the paper with him ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)); Zuckerberg personally encouraged Novati to keep writing an internal newsletter that was causing his executives damage-control headaches ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on-zuck.md)). Feinberg's operating advice for getting into the room at all: "be the kind of coworker that people would want to see succeed" — amicability beats cynical workplace game theory for landing large multi-person projects ([episode](../sources/google-deepmind-pre-training-lead.md)). And once there, get feedback from people whose taste you aspire to, not peers barely ahead of you — Novati's core growth loop ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-staff-eng-ic7-on-zuck.md)), and a direct argument for seeking legends out rather than avoiding them ([mentorship-and-sponsorship](mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)).

## Practical takeaways

- Attach yourself to a legend's mission through useful, even menial, work rather than through prestige projects — that's how Feinberg went from XLA-compiler golfing to a Jeff Dean bonus to leading pre-training ([episode](../sources/google-deepmind-pre-training-lead.md)).
- Don't fear pedigree: you always have value to add next to a famous collaborator, and if you become the senior one, you owe juniors the same approachability ([Reeves](../sources/tech-lead-for-metas-most-used-programming.md)).
- Assume credit will follow the person who talks about the work, not the person who did it — give the talks, or accept Liskov's fate on your own results ([Liskov](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md); [Lamport](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)).
- Treat "everyone said I was crazy" as weak evidence you're wrong: the best work often looks crazy a priori, especially when it challenges an incumbent with a bigger budget ([Hellman](../sources/turing-award-winner-nsa-public-key.md)).
- Build on a giant's known mistakes rather than your own unknown ones when it buys you an ecosystem — Stroustrup's rationale for keeping C compatibility ([episode](../sources/creator-of-c-bell-labs-negative-overhead.md)).
- Diagnose what specifically makes the impressive person impressive (Carmack's codebase-agnostic feedback, Zuckerberg's devotion to growth, Lamport's abstraction) and train that trait, rather than treating "legend" as an unanalyzable category ([Su](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md); [Lamport](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)).
