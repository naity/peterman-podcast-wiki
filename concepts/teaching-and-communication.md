# Teaching & communication

Across the podcast, the guests who reached Distinguished Engineer, CTO, Fellow, or Turing-Award level almost unanimously treat communication — writing, speaking, storytelling, teaching — not as a soft skill but as the mechanism by which technical work becomes impact, credit, and [career growth](career-growth.md). Where they disagree is on which medium carries the leverage, and how much simplification the truth can survive.

## What the guests say

**Writing is the leverage skill — because writing is thinking.** [Marc Brooker](../entities/marc-brooker.md) makes the strongest positive case: "writing lets you scale out the impact of your expertise in space and time in a way that's really hard to do in other media," and he sometimes writes documents he never shares purely to sharpen his own thinking ([episode](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md)). [Leslie Lamport](../entities/leslie-lamport.md) states the same idea as an epistemic law: "if you're thinking without writing, you only think you're thinking" — writing hierarchically structured proofs and instruction-manuals-before-programs exposes the "obvious" unstated steps where errors hide ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)). [Bobby Holley](../entities/bobby-holley.md)'s top advice for aspiring Distinguished Engineers at [Mozilla](../entities/mozilla.md) is simply "write," with a sharp corollary for the AI era: do not have an LLM produce text for you, because "the process of writing is the process of thinking" and outsourcing it forfeits the growth ([episode](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)). The career mechanics agree: [Simon Kindström](../entities/simon-kindstrom.md) credits writing well — putting the impactful part at the top instead of burying the lede in technical detail — as a major lever in reaching [Meta](../entities/meta.md) Staff by 26 ([episode](../sources/26-year-old-meta-staff-eng-ic6-on.md)); [Philip Su](../entities/philip-su.md) adds that the way to write well is to read examples of good writing and steal from them ([episode](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md)); and [Stefan Mai](../entities/stefan-mai.md) says walking into his director's office with a written plan of things he didn't yet know how to do was "the moment his director knew he'd succeed" ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)).

**But talking — and marketing — is what wins mindshare.** [Barbara Liskov](../entities/barbara-liskov.md) is the standing counterexample to write-and-they-will-come: her Viewstamped Replication and Lamport's Paxos are "essentially the same" protocol, yet Paxos got the fame — in Lamport's own words, "I went around giving all the talks and she implemented it" — to the point that [Google](../entities/google.md) believed its own file system ran Paxos when a former student recognized it as Viewstamped Replication ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)). Lamport himself confirms that packaging beats purity: he invented the "Byzantine Generals" story as deliberate marketing after watching Dijkstra's Dining Philosophers make a result famous, while his Paxos paper sat unpublished for eight years because referees didn't get the framing ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)). Some guests reject documents as the primary medium altogether: [Adam Ernst](../entities/adam-ernst.md) says he is weak at docs, communicates best anchored to concrete technical problems, and runs his influence playbook by talking in person and reviewing 1,600+ diffs a half as a viral channel ([episode](../sources/meta-distinguished-eng-ic9-on-influencing.md)), while [David Fowler](../entities/david-fowler.md)'s motto is "code is currency... It's not PowerPoint, it's not a document" — show a working prototype first ([episode](../sources/intern-to-microsoft-distinguished.md)).

**Simplify for the audience, or insist on rigor?** [David Malan](../entities/david-malan.md) knowingly trades depth for motivation: CS50's "memorable moments" (tearing a phone book for binary search, students sorting on stage) are theatrical anchors, and he overspends lecture time on cherry-picked topics because engagement is what carries students through the firehose ([episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)). [Carey Nachenberg](../entities/carey-nachenberg.md) similarly designs every explanation for the 30th–50th-percentile listener ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)), and the anonymous chief architect "Asian Dad Energy" tells engineers to "explain like the audience is five," having watched a brilliant colleague fail because he couldn't ([episode](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)). Lamport pushes back hard on where simplification ends: the Raft paper that user studies found "more understandable" than Paxos contained a later-discovered bug — "for me, understanding means you can write a proof of it. What understanding means for most people is a warm fuzzy feeling" ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)). Liskov's Dijkstra story splits the difference: "Goto Considered Harmful" was blunt to the point of offense — "Dijkstra was not the most diplomatic person" — but the argument was rigorous and "clearly Dijkstra won the day" ([episode](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)). Malan would note the two positions serve different audiences: his stated goal is not outputting programmers but people who understand first principles, and his other knob is time, not dumbing-down ("take 24 weeks, take 52") ([episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)).

**Communication is read as intelligence — and converts directly into credit.** Nachenberg is blunt: he knew a "200-IQ" engineer stuck at L4 at Google for poor communication, and a single internal Stuxnet talk had GoogleX colleagues calling him one of the smartest people they'd met — "people associate [communication] with intelligence" ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)). The dark side is losing the narrative: he drove Lyft's shift to a neural-network driver but lost the credit because he let others tell the story ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)). Managers weaponize the same skill deliberately: Mai calls storytelling a bedrock [management](management.md) skill — narrating the team's trajectory and defining "winning" is what keeps [corporate politics](corporate-politics.md) from festering ([episode](../sources/meta-senior-manager-m2-on-manager.md)) — and [David Singleton](../entities/david-singleton.md) says speaking improves only through deliberate reps (his manager booked him 1,000-person keynotes) because senior leaders are "always on stage" ([episode](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md)). [Ryan Peterman](../entities/ryan-peterman.md) built the podcast itself as a speaking feedback loop: publishing forces him to pore over every word in editing, a stronger commitment mechanism than Toastmasters ([episode](../sources/behind-the-scenes-of-this-podcast.md)).

**How much of your time should communication get?** Here the guests draw different lines. Brooker's "doing vs discussing" 2x2 warns that 100% at either pole is a failure mode; his sweet spot is ~75/25 practitioner-to-communicator, and long-term "it is better to be underrated than overrated" ([episode](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md)). Holley agrees from the [promotions](promotions.md) angle: frame updates as visibility and credit for the team, because self-promoting promo-hackers are "extremely annoying" to leadership ([episode](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)). Nachenberg counters with the cost of modesty — document your accomplishments and claim specific, unembellished credit, or committees will assign it elsewhere ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)). Teaching itself, though, pays back into the engineering: [Brendan Burns](../entities/brendan-burns.md) says the writing, arguing, and teaching skills from his PhD directly shaped how Kubernetes was organized to be learnable ([episode](../sources/the-creator-of-kubernetes-on-building.md)), and [Ryan Williams](../entities/ryan-williams.md) rewrote his own breakthrough proof repeatedly — expanding the one footnote that convinced a colleague into a "warmup" section — because a result nobody can follow persuades nobody ([episode](../sources/mit-complexity-theorist-on-leetcode.md)).

**Should AI do your communicating?** Holley says never for writing — you lose the thinking ([episode](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)) — while "Asian Dad Energy" happily uses AI tools to polish his "halting, clumsy syntax" ([episode](../sources/laid-off-from-big-tech-after-25-years.md)). The educators land in between with guardrails: Nachenberg banned LLMs on student projects after project scores diverged sharply from exam understanding ([episode](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)), and Malan bans off-the-shelf Copilot for problem sets but gives students unlimited CS50.ai, a deliberately less-helpful tutor tuned to lead rather than answer ([episode](../sources/harvard-professor-cs50-what-matters.md)). See [ai-era-engineering](ai-era-engineering.md).

## Practical takeaways

- Write to think, not just to publish: draft docs even when no one will read them, and record which design decisions were reasoned versus arbitrary ([Brooker](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md); [Lamport](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md)).
- Put the impactful, interesting part at the top — engineers habitually bury the lede in technical details their audience doesn't need ([Kindström](../sources/26-year-old-meta-staff-eng-ic6-on.md)).
- Design explanations for the 30th–50th-percentile listener and practice in low-stakes venues (lunchroom talks, optional keynotes) before high-stakes ones ([Nachenberg](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md); [Singleton](../sources/stripe-cto-on-what-grew-his-career.md)).
- If the work matters, market it: a memorable story or name can decide which of two equivalent ideas gets the credit ([Lamport](../sources/turing-award-winner-on-working-with.md); [Liskov](../sources/turing-award-winner-data-abstraction.md)).
- Claim specific, unembellished credit in writing rather than letting others narrate your work ([Nachenberg](../sources/googlex-chief-scientist-on-imposter.md)) — but frame updates as team visibility, not self-promotion ([Holley](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)).
- Keep a hard floor on hands-on work so communication never becomes the whole job — ~75/25 practitioner-to-communicator, and better underrated than overrated ([Brooker](../sources/aws-distinguished-eng-learnings-from.md)).
- Don't delegate first-draft writing to an LLM; the thinking is the point ([Holley](../sources/mozilla-firefox-cto-on-browser-war.md)). Reading great writing is the fastest way to improve your own ([Su](../sources/openai-and-meta-distinguished-engineer.md)).
