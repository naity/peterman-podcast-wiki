# 26 Year Old Meta Staff Eng (IC6) On Promotions, Redefining Expectations, Secret Equity Bonuses

**Author:** Ryan Peterman — **Date:** July 7, 2025 — **Guest:** Simon Kindström

## Intro

Ryan had a conversation with Simon Kindström, who got promoted from New Grad (IC3) to Staff (IC6) at Meta in 3 years, received the "Redefines Expectations" performance rating twice, and received secret bonuses (discretionary equity). The conversation also covered Simon's experiment with switching to management, which didn't work out, and his return to the IC track.

## Main takeaways

### 1. Story behind the Redefines Expectations rating

When experiments produced unexpected unfavorable results, Simon didn't abandon them — he investigated the underlying causes, implemented modifications, and reran the experiments iteratively until he either understood the failure mechanisms or achieved improved results. "He didn't give up on those experiments until he understood why," and subsequently made changes and reran them. This relentless problem-solving approach, coupled with eventual success, produced his highest-impact work and directly earned him the "redefines expectations" rating.

### 2. Writing well was an important part of his career

Technical writing was a critical leverage point. Simon emphasized writing about his accomplishments, since effective communication amplifies impact visibility. "It's important to put the impactful, interesting parts of the writing at the top." A common engineer pitfall he identified: getting excessively absorbed in technical details that audiences find irrelevant or uninteresting. Prioritizing engaging, business-relevant findings over granular technical exposition was a career accelerant.

### 3. Impact drove his insane career growth

Simon's manager recruited him to the team by "explaining the business impact his work would have and how that'd lead to career growth." Once on the team, Simon's contributions drove multiple significant revenue wins for Meta, directly resulting in high performance ratings and accelerated promotions. Business-impact-oriented work creates a virtuous cycle: meaningful outcomes lead to visibility, recognition, and rapid advancement.

### 4. His manager was a key part of his story

"His manager was coaching him and working closely with him throughout." The manager had deep organizational knowledge and had personally experienced rapid advancement before transitioning to management, making him a credible mentor. Simon absorbed significant professional wisdom from this relationship and leveraged it to navigate career decisions and growth opportunities.

### 5. Scaling himself was a critical skill for growth to Staff

Simon initially struggled with delegation — not with assigning work, but with failing to keep ownership of others' results and outcomes, leading to scrambling for strong results when performance review cycles approached. The transformative principle he internalized: "Delegate, don't abdicate" — maintain accountability for delegated work while empowering others to execute. This ownership mindset was essential for scaling impact beyond individual contributions.

### 6. Transitioning to management

Simon pursued a management role as a deliberate skill-development experiment and to contribute organizationally. The experience proved misaligned with his preferences and strengths: he found himself increasingly distant from the technical work he enjoyed and encountered substantial organizational friction and thrash. Recognizing the mismatch, he made a transparent decision to switch back to the IC track, and communicated the journey openly rather than pretending the transition succeeded.
