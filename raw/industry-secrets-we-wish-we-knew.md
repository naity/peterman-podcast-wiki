# Industry Secrets We Wish We Knew Before Graduating | Staff Eng Talk @ UCLA

Host: Ryan Peterman — Co-speaker: Ricky ("Staff @ Google by 28") — Published: May 2, 2025

## Host intro (as relayed by fetch; near-verbatim with quoted fragments)

Ryan Peterman and Ricky (identified as "Staff @ Google by 28") were invited to speak at UCLA for UPE (Upsilon Pi Epsilon), an old club both had participated in during their college years. Peterman "got really excited because there's so much that no one taught me in college that I had to learn in industry myself." The event received over 250 RSVPs. To maximize usefulness for attendees, the speakers structured the session as a Q&A. Peterman credits Jordan, Ashley, and Lune from UCLA UPE+ACM for hosting and recording the event. He also mentions investing more effort into video editing at the beginning of productions, reasoning that "if that part isn't good, then most people won't watch the whole thing."

## Takeaways (headings verbatim; bodies near-verbatim)

1. "Maximizing luck for promotions" — Luck influences promotions, but you can increase your luck to some extent. Taking initiative to seek new opportunities and remaining prepared when they arrive expands your "luck surface area."

2. "Perfect code isn't enough to be a good engineer" — The best engineers they've encountered weren't necessarily the top programmers from college but rather had balanced technical and soft skills. Knowing "how to influence others," collaborating effectively in teams, and being able to "write concisely" are all critical to software engineering success.

3. "You need to advocate for yourself" — Since people ultimately decide promotions, "Visibility is therefore an important part of growing your career" alongside performing quality work.

4. "Big Tech vs. Startups" — Both Ricky and Peterman agreed they'd choose Big Tech over startups after college because it provides instant credibility and solid foundational skills. After several years, you can pivot to startups with established brand equity and learned expertise.

5. "Lines of code != impact" — Impact is concrete, measurable work outcomes. The tech industry rewards results, not effort; writing less code while achieving greater impact is preferable.

6. "Big Tech won't make you crazy rich" — Advancing within large tech companies provides a reliable path to financial stability and freedom but won't generate extraordinary wealth. Those targeting exceptional riches should consider breakout startup opportunities instead.

7. "Job hopping early vs late career" — Early-career job hopping can be beneficial. However, promotions beyond Senior/Staff levels depend on influence and credibility that require time to develop at each company. Later-career job hopping becomes counterproductive.

8. "AI won't replace SWE jobs, at least for the next few years" — Engineers already use numerous non-AI tools for coding; LLMs function as productivity-enabling tools. Software engineering jobs appear secure for approximately five years, with longer-term impacts remaining unpredictable.

9. "College recruiting" — Resume projects are the minimum requirement. Beyond that, persistence and willingness to join smaller companies early accelerates experience building.

10. "Getting a return offer from an internship" — Internships typically feature well-defined projects. Companies assess whether interns complete projects quickly with high-quality code. Proposing improvements afterward strengthens candidacy further.
