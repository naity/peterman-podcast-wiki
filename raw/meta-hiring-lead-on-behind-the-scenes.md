## What the rubric looks like

So at Meta, there's five of them. They are "driving results, embracing ambiguity, resolving conflicts, growing continuously, and communicating effectively." Every company is going to have different ones. They might purely just be the company values and we can talk about that. But for example, at Meta, those are not the five company values at Meta. [00:46:26] So these, you know, whatever five to eight things, generally they fit into a broader set of categories that I talk about in the book that I call signal areas. And there's eight of them. ## Hiring Committee Structure & Process

**What Happens Behind the Scenes:**
Austen explains that when reviewing senior-level candidates, the first step involves examining the behavioral interview to understand "what is the scope that this engineer has operated at in the past, what's their level of influence, what's their level of insight."

The hiring process involves multiple roles: sourcers identify candidates based on years of experience and previous titles, recruiters manage the workflow and advocate for candidates, phone screeners conduct initial technical assessments, and hiring committees make leveling decisions. Engineering directors provide final approval.

## Leveling Determination

**Initial Placement:**
Sourcers rely heavily on years of experience and title when initially assessing candidates. However, they account for "level inflation in different parts of the industry," sometimes downleveling candidates from non-FAANG companies who claim high titles.

**Critical Signal:**
The behavioral interview reveals scope through four key dimensions:
- Technical depth of problems solved
- Organizational complexity navigated
- Business impact delivered
- Leadership and mentorship provided

Ryan notes that a performance improvement project framed as "organized across 100 engineers" with "multiple staff level engineers" required to solve technical issues signals much higher impact than simply stating "I improved performance in the Facebook app."

## Story Selection & Delivery

**The "Decode, Select, Deliver" Framework:**
Candidates should identify what signal an interviewer seeks, choose stories showcasing appropriate scope, then deliver them engagingly. Austen emphasizes: "Your job as the candidate is to guide the interviewer towards that signal, towards that place in your career."

**Scope Hierarchy:**
- Junior/New grad: Tasks assigned by manager
- Mid-level: Features with multiple tasks
- Senior: Projects with multiple features requiring delegation
- Staff: Goals requiring multiple projects
- Principal+: Organizations with multiple goals

**Story Priorities (In Order):**
1. **Scope** - Breadth and ambition of work
2. **Relevance** - Fit to question asked
3. **Recency** - Stories within 2-3 years preferred
4. **Uniqueness** - Different stories for multiple behavioral rounds

## Common Mistakes Senior Candidates Make

**Problem #1 - Wrong Story Choice:**
Selecting projects that don't demonstrate appropriate scope for the target level, missing opportunities to showcase organizational impact.

**Problem #2 - Over-Explanation:**
Providing excessive context, technical details, or backstory. Austen's guideline: "If it's been like 30 seconds since you told me something you did, some kind of action, if it's been that long, then you should rethink that."

**Problem #3 - Defensive Framing:**
Presenting stories vulnerable to unfavorable interpretation. For example, blaming technical debt without explaining how you assumed responsibility for solving it.

**Problem #4 - Missing Non-Technical Elements:**
Neglecting to discuss mentorship, delegation, cross-team influence, roadmap alignment, and conflict resolution—critical for staff-level assessment.

**Problem #5 - Poor Story Organization:**
Long stories (multiple years) lack structure. Solution: Provide a "table of contents" framework at the start listing three to five key narrative components, allowing the interviewer to ask follow-ups while keeping you anchored.

## Unethical Candidates & Lying

Austen observes that deliberately fabricating experience is harder than candidates think. The phone screen for staff-level positions includes "scope check" conversations requiring candidates to walk interviewers through large projects with technical depth. Follow-up questions from "highly calibrated" senior interviewers typically expose inconsistencies in fabricated stories. Austen states: "I do not have stories about people that we hire that we thought was totally lying to us."

## Mid-Process Leveling Negotiations

If placed in an unwanted level, candidates should discuss this directly with recruiters. The approach: anchor every conversation with interviewers at the target level by emphasizing large-scope projects in opening statements. This "resets their mind about what to expect," potentially prompting upleveling during hiring committee discussions.

## Referral Impact & Bias

**Referral Power:**
Referrals matter most "when you're on the bubble." A strong referral from a senior leader (VP/Director) who directly worked with the candidate significantly increases chances of follow-up interviews. Level of referrer and content quality matter—a detailed referral describing specific working relationship outweighs generic recommendations.

Austen shares his own experience: "I got a follow up because I had a referral from somebody that I was in school with who worked directly with me."

However, he notes that even strong referrals typically don't override unanimous "no hire" consensus—they primarily influence borderline decisions.

**Bias at Different Levels:**
At junior levels, structured rubrics reduce subjective "would I grab a beer with this person" bias. At senior and principal levels, interpersonal dynamics and perceived cultural fit become increasingly influential.

## Company-Specific Preparation

**OpenAI Values:**
- "Feel the AGI" signal requires demonstrated optimism about AI's positive potential
- Candidates should show excitement about AI applications in new domains

**Anthropic Values:**
- "Light and Shade" emphasizes understanding both positive and negative implications of AI
- Candidates must discuss how they considered and mitigated potential risks in past AI projects

## Interview Prep Time Allocation by Level

**Junior Engineers:**
Prioritize technical interviewing (coding and system design); behavioral focus on demonstrating thinking and motivation despite limited accomplishments.

**Mid-Level Engineers:**
Balance technical and behavioral preparation. Austen notes: "I think you need about balance... you need to shine across all different interview types." Agentic coding is reshaping expectations—ownership signal now matters more than raw coding ability.

**Senior+ Engineers:**
Heavily emphasize behavioral interviews (several weeks of preparation). Austen prioritizes behavioral assessment for staff candidates: "First thing I would do when I look at a staff level packet is I would go to the behavioral interview."

Principal and distinguished engineer candidates face additional behavioral rounds focusing on cross-functional collaboration and project deep-dives.

## Compensation Negotiation Risks

Early anchoring in salary conversations creates negotiating constraints. Austen recommends understanding local compensation disclosure laws (California and New York require salary band transparency) and orchestrating multiple offers to land simultaneously, enabling better negotiation leverage.

## Interview Timing & Engagement Signals

**Managing Monologues:**
Limit responses to 2-4 minutes maximum without extraordinary storytelling ability. Watch for interviewer engagement signals (eye contact, note-taking) and proactively ask: "Is this what you're looking for?" to avoid wasting time.

**Interviewer Styles Vary:**
Some interviewers listen silently and assess based on story choices; others interrupt frequently, pick apart details, and jump between questions. Expect senior-level interviewers to be "a little rude" while pursuing signal acquisition.

## Specialist Engineers & Scope Signaling

Non-managerial specialists solving problems staff-level engineers couldn't address can signal exceptional ability by framing context: discussing why previous attempts failed and what unique insight led to their success. Austen emphasizes: "That kind of judgment and reflection is a big part of assessing someone at a senior level."

## Behavioral Interview Rubric

Meta uses five behavioral signals: driving results, embracing ambiguity, resolving conflicts, growing continuously, and communicating effectively. Companies hire selection scientists (PhDs in industrial psychology) to codify values into assessable signal areas.

Candidates should request rubrics from recruiters in advance and map personal stories against each area.

## Interview Performance vs. Job Performance

Correlation between strong interview performance and downstream job success exists but isn't perfect. Confidence level in hiring committee decisions correlates with future performance better than number of individual interview passes/failures. However, interviewing is a distinct skill from job performance—some candidates "rock the interview and don't do well," while others perform contrary to expectations.

## Subjectivity & Likability Bias

Bias scales with seniority. At junior levels, structured rubrics minimize subjective preferences. At senior+ levels, interpersonal dynamics increasingly influence decisions because these roles require influencing others. Candidates must demonstrate "casual excellence"—confidence, comfort, and ability to build rapport while showcasing expertise.

## Final Hiring Decision

Decisions typically emerge through consensus among hiring committee members, with escalation to engineering directors for final approval. In contentious cases, voting occurs, but consensus-building is preferred.
