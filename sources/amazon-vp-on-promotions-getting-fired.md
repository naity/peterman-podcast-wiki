# Amazon VP On Promotions, Getting Fired Twice, Working With Bezos | Ethan Evans

Ethan Evans went from being fired twice at startups for poor soft skills to Vice President at [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md), where he led 800+ engineers, launched Prime Video-era initiatives, ran the Amazon App Store, and served as integration liaison for the $970M Twitch acquisition under Andy Jassy. Ryan calls it his most brutally honest interview so far: Evans explains how director and VP [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) really work, why PIPs almost never end well, and the raw power dynamics between managers and reports.

## Key takeaways

- Evans was let go twice around the .com bust — the second time as a "layoff of one" — both driven by his abrasiveness; an interviewer's remark ("if you were that valuable, they'd have found a way to keep you") triggered him to deliberately rebuild his personality around questions, listening, and the psychology of work ([regrets and advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)).
- Being "strategically annoying" beats being abrasive: you must have opinions and push, but if you push so hard people stop disagreeing to your face, they organize behind your back — and without allies (B, C, D who'd warn you about A), you dig your own grave in [corporate politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md).
- His director promotion came from an outsized event, not a lifetime-achievement grind: he told his SVP "unless you order me not to do this, I'm going to do it," bet on the TiVo partnership, and the promo doc's key line was "without Ethan, we wouldn't have had TiVo."
- He pressured for the promotion with deliberately unassailable wording: "My career is very important to me, and I need to know if it's as important to Amazon" — a polite up-or-out threat; he admits that as a leader he too sometimes promoted the agitator over the patient team player, because triage is real ([promotions](../concepts/promotions.md)).
- His "magic loop" for the VP promotion: a 2.5–3 year explicit partnership with his manager — "I'll do everything you need done; you make sure I'm rewarded for it" — culminating in his VP staking his own credibility on the case ([mentorship and sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md), [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)).
- Promotion failure story: he promised a star SDE1 a promo in good faith but didn't know Amazon's twice-yearly cycle, missed the window, and the engineer left (costing him about a year). Lesson: the report is the pig and the manager the chicken — know the process yourself and verify your manager does too.
- As Twitch integration liaison he deliberately gave up his 800-person org to influence CEO Emmett Shear with zero authority — an exercise in pure [influence](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md); Twitch's gaps were venture-funded indifference to profitability (while Jassy demanded it) and Shear learning to hire over loyal early employees.
- Bezos vs. Jassy: as founder, Bezos could "take the whole company to Vegas and bet it on black" (telling his CFO "how much is that? because I might want to spend it"), and felt emotionally supportive; Jassy, the hired classic business leader, asked probing questions and left Evans feeling "waiting to see if I'd screw up."
- VP reviews reward ticking goals, not achieving visions: he was rated well every year while Prime Gaming never approached the Tencent-scale vision, and he predicted a decade early that the App Store would hit every goal "and it's gonna be meaningless" — big companies keep zombie products because shutdown is an admission of failure ([big tech culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md)).
- Amazon's unregretted attrition (URA) goal (~4–7% a year) exists because when Amazon removed it, managers simply stopped having hard conversations; but it also means PIPs are largely self-fulfilling — every PIP'd person he has coached was fired anyway, so his advice is to spend the PIP period job hunting ([calibrations and ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md), [layoffs](../concepts/layoffs.md)).
- A manager can fire any one person they want (not everyone): they strike first with the narrative, and the same facts — e.g. 700 pull requests — can be spun as "upleveling the whole team" or "nitpicking instead of contributing." HR is not there to rescue you; if your boss is vindictive, make peace or leave.
- Advice to his younger self: always prefer high-growth companies ("my ladder was an escalator" — Amazon grew 100x while he was there) and wake up sooner to the fact that jobs are with humans: build reputation and relationships, which even introverts can do from a keyboard. Amazon called him, not the other way around.
