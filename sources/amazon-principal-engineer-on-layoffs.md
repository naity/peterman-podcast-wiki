# Amazon Principal Engineer On Layoffs, Interviewing & Career Growth | Steve Huynh

Steve Huynh took an unconventional route into tech — a Liberal Arts degree, then joining [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) through a friend's referral — and over nearly two decades climbed from Support Engineer to Principal Engineer before launching his career-development YouTube channel, A Life Engineered. The episode digs into what interview prep advice gets wrong, Amazon's explicit up-or-out performance culture, who actually gets cut in [layoffs](../concepts/layoffs.md), and what it really takes to reach Principal Engineer.

## Key takeaways

- Most interview advice overemphasizes coding and system design; per Huynh, "these tests just get you in the door." Behavioral interviews are what determine leveling and cultural fit, so engineers should focus on articulating their experience to match the target level ([hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md)).
- Market dynamics are bifurcated: junior engineering positions face limited demand while Senior+ roles see extraordinary demand, and the real competition is concentrated in the pre-screening phase rather than in the interviews themselves.
- [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) maintains an explicit up-and-out policy for SDE1s — promotion within a timeframe or termination — that relaxes at higher levels ([big-tech-culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md), [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md)).
- Layoffs at Amazon typically hit people lacking cultural fit rather than poor performers, in Huynh's observation ([layoffs](../concepts/layoffs.md)).
- The Principal Engineer promotion requires delivering nearly two levels' worth of contribution simultaneously, and candidates get conflicting feedback — adopt Principal behaviors while also showing stronger individual contribution — so starting preparation early is critical ([career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)).
- Huynh's promotion-defining project was overhauling Prime Video's outdated catalog infrastructure, a high-leverage system that hundreds of dependent engineers relied on, which attracted senior-level investment.
- To avoid layoffs: direct communication with your manager about expectations matters most — early, transparent feedback prevents surprises ([management](../concepts/management.md)).
- Career strategy: job-hop frequently early in your career for rapid advancement, but favor stability once you reach Senior+, where meaningful growth opportunities require sustained tenure.
- His regrets: not switching teams earlier, and focusing too much on people-pleasing and external expectations ([regrets-and-advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)).
