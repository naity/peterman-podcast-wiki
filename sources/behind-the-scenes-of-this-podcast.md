# Behind the Scenes of this Podcast So Far

A short (~15 minute) meta-episode released when the podcast hit 25,000 subscribers: host Ryan Peterman is interviewed by Brandon about why he started The Peterman Post, which episodes took off and why, how he sources hidden super-senior guests, what podcasting taught him, and the cost of making content alongside a full-time [Meta](../entities/meta.md) job. It's the origin-story key to the whole series — explaining the "career stories from super senior ICs" format that most other episodes follow.

## Key takeaways

- Ryan started the podcast primarily as a deliberate-practice mechanism for speaking ([teaching-and-communication](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md)): unlike Toastmasters, publishing forces him to pore over every word in editing — "a very powerful commitment mechanism and a very powerful feedback loop." He committed to a deliberately lax goal of one episode per month to avoid overload.
- The show's format was inspired by internal Facebook/[Meta](../entities/meta.md) career-story posts written by very senior ICs — 50-minute reads Ryan devoured early in his career — hence the focus on having super-senior engineers tell their [career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) stories.
- The Philip Su episode was the first breakout: within a day or two "the graphs were all going vertical," which Ryan read as product-market fit — proof that others love these hidden-senior-IC stories as much as he does.
- The Ethan Evans episode confirmed the pattern; Ryan calls Evans "one of the best speakers I've seen in tech." Its aggressive trailer ("This is the most brutally honest...") worked but sparked feedback; Ryan plans trailer-free or minimal-trailer versions on Spotify/Apple for recurring listeners while keeping attention-grabbing trailers on YouTube for new viewers.
- His dream guest list is deliberately anti-celebrity: hidden super-senior engineers famous only inside their orgs at Google, Facebook, Apple, Microsoft. Sourcing is hard (LinkedIn search is bad, these people aren't on social media), so he uses his big-tech network plus asking the audience "who is that senior IC in your org you're super impressed by?" — which has directly produced upcoming guests.
- Hardest podcasting-specific skill: suppressing verbal backchannel ("uh-huh, right, yeah") while guests speak and nodding vigorously instead, since acknowledgment noises ruin the audio for listeners.
- On balancing content with a full-time job: for about two years he stayed up until 4:00 AM every Thursday writing the newsletter. Gergely Orosz (The Pragmatic Engineer) warned him about "burning the candle at both ends" — Ryan concedes he was right, and that with a family he "would literally be drowning."
