# Instagram Principal Eng (IC8) On Building IG Stories, 1 Promo Per Half, Small Teams

Ryan Olson went from a failed 2011 Facebook interview (the interviewer cut it short and told him he'd never make it) to IC4-to-IC8 at [Instagram](../entities/instagram.md), leading iOS for the Whiteout redesign, Instagram Stories, IGTV, and IG Labs before leaving [Meta](../entities/meta.md) to co-found the friends-only social app Retro. The episode is a masterclass in small-team product engineering — Stories was built in two to three months by a core of two iOS engineers — plus unusually frank material on promo mechanics ("1 promo per half"), the Facebook/Instagram antitrust-era tensions, and why he thinks Instagram would run better with half the people.

## Key takeaways

- Olson failed his 2011 Facebook internship interview so badly (shaking hands, a triple-nested for loop on a since-banned question) that the interviewer cut it short and said "you're just not gonna be able to make it at Facebook." He later reached IC8 there — his standing case against leetcode-style [interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md); for full-time interviews he used prescribed beta blockers to break the physical nerves feedback loop.
- His Flipboard internship (~50 people) sat him next to future billionaires: fellow interns Dylan Field (Figma) and Devin Finzer (OpenSea). What distinguished them wasn't raw skill but determination to found companies and conviction in a technical insight (Field's bet on WebGL) that they iterated toward product-market fit.
- His open-source iOS debugging tool FLEX (started as Flipboard's internal "FLTweaker") got him the Instagram job — not because interviewers looked at it (they refused, to his frustration) but because engineer Jonathan Dan saw it, pulled him into the pipeline, and advocated in candidate review after a mixed loop.
- Early Instagram (~10 iOS engineers, rebuilt after "warring factions" over Scott Goodson's AsyncDisplayKit destroyed each other) was full of low-hanging fruit: he cut cold start 20% by deferring profile-tab work and cut crashes 80% with a one-line fix to assertion behavior in AFNetworking — winning "the ax," Instagram's physical weekly award for outsized impact.
- On the Whiteout redesign he refused the long-lived-branch approach, building behind a feature flag with semantic colors instead — which saved the launch when a Facebook exec killed the ship plan the night before and demanded A/B tests. His synthesis: A/B testing is invaluable for conversion mechanics but high-level product direction needs strong opinions, because pure experimentation traps you in incrementalism ("easy to get those 1% wins, but you're never going to get that 50% jump").
- Instagram Stories was built in ~2-3 months after he *cut* the team down to a core of two iOS engineers with no dedicated server engineer: "if you want to go fast, go small." Full ownership, no coordination overhead, and 16-18 hour days (sacrificing his U.S.-team rock climbing career) produced a launch so polished it beat [Snapchat](../entities/snapchat.md) at everyday sharing — helped by Instagram's unfair advantage, the existing friend graph — and added format inventions like hold-to-pause, which he built simply because his thumb expected it to work.
- His [promotion](../concepts/promotions.md) hack: as a mis-leveled IC4 performing near his IC8 counterpart Will Bailey, he asked his director for a 4-to-6 double promo, knowing HR would refuse ("we've done this twice... it worked out terribly") — deliberately seeding the idea so refusal converted into back-to-back promos: IC5 for Whiteout, IC6 for Stories. His frame: levels are an incentive system, a dangled carrot, and delays are often pacing rather than performance.
- IGTV (his IC7 project, a small elite team with Kevin Systrom and designer Ian Silber) failed despite anticipating vertical video: long-form vertical inventory never materialized, creators were "horrified" by his patented AI landscape-to-vertical reformatting, and Instagram's hubris that it could single-handedly change creator behavior didn't pan out — though it informed Reels. The era's confusing strategy calls (like turning off Facebook-to-Instagram traffic) only made sense to him years later reading the antitrust lawsuit's internal memos on Facebook's fear of Instagram cannibalization — [corporate politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md) invisible even to the IC8 building the product.
- IG Labs, his IC8 promo, was a deliberately tiny, high-talent-density innovation group (initially just him and designer Vivian Wong) for projects that didn't slot into the org chart — producing collaborative posts (another patent) and serving as the talent pool that seeded Threads.
- As a rare Tech Lead Director he rejected Facebook's IC/[management](../concepts/management.md) separation, preferring Tesla's model (his wife is Senior Staff there) where ICs manage and managers code — citing Taleb's skin in the game. The cost was real: dual-role ratings averaged down ("you're exceeding as an IC but you're meeting as a manager, so you're just meeting"), acceptable only because he never intended to stay and always answered "one year" on retention surveys.
- On the Thanos-snap hypothetical he says yes — Instagram would likely be better off with half the people: each marginal person adds invisible coordination and codebase cost (he was spending 4+ hours a day waiting for builds before he left), and post-Elon Twitter, cut ~80% yet still running and newly profitable, is partial vindication.
- Retro, his [startup](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md) with Stories PM Nathan Sharp, is opinionated anti-"brain rot" social: mutual-friends-only, a 250-friend cap, no ads (ads plus algorithmic feeds create "a little bit evil" misaligned incentives), monetized by subscription. Nearly half of daily users post — far above Instagram — and it hit #1 on Taiwan's App Store; he's open that refusing creator-driven growth loops makes growth harder. Founder tradeoffs: satisfaction and learning way up, compensation way down (he and his co-founder pay themselves less than every employee).
- The talent he admires: Mike Krieger led from the front, personally coding the Stories neon brush and reviewing diffs at 2 AM alongside the team; Will Bailey wrote a Stories vision doc that turned out to be the accurate five-year roadmap, modeling the product-engineer path Facebook's "real engineers do infra" culture dismissed; Thomas Dimson (feed ranking, later acquired by [OpenAI](../entities/openai.md)) is "the smartest person I've ever met" who approached infrastructure with a product mindset.
- [Advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md) to new grads: "learn the tools of your time" — he stood out because iOS was new and senior engineers hadn't learned it natively; today's equivalent is AI tooling, where new grads can out-skill seniors, and mastery of those tools is the answer to shrinking junior demand.
