# Anthropic Eng Leader and Ex-Senior Director at Meta on Advice That Changed Her Career

Fiona Fung supports the Claude Code team at [Anthropic](../entities/anthropic.md) after eleven and a half years at [Microsoft](../entities/microsoft.md) (Visual Studio, TypeScript) and a Senior Director run at [Meta](../entities/meta.md) spanning Facebook Marketplace, AR/VR operating systems (Quest 2, Ray-Ban Stories, Horizon OS). The episode is a [management](../concepts/management.md)-craft conversation: balancing impact against team health in war rooms, becoming a manager of managers, [mentoring](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md) versus coaching, why relentless dogfooding defined her leadership brand, and the single piece of feedback-on-receiving-feedback that changed her career.

## Key takeaways

- On impact vs team health: war rooms/lockdowns are valuable for focus but tax morale — her Marketplace growth war room ("we won't exit until we hit the number," 75 experiments launched at once, rooms in Menlo Park and Seattle) dragged on ~2+ months; her regret is not being realistic and upfront with the team about duration ([management](../concepts/management.md), [regrets and advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)).
- First-time manager-of-managers advice: treat it as an explicit partnership ("what are you strong at, what am I good at, how do we divide and conquer"), and build a "trust but verify" muscle so you don't lose touch with projects; her nightmare is the "this is fine" dog meme — she asks for fast, transparent two-way feedback instead.
- Mentoring vs coaching are different modes: mentoring is listening plus advice, coaching is being a mirror so the person finds answers themselves; the most effective relationships have mentee-set goals and an explicit "what does success look like in 3/6 months" conversation ([mentorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)).
- 1:1 advice for ICs: move status reporting to async (Slack, a shared living doc) and spend the live time on real conversations — learning, digging deeper, career ([career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)).
- She left Microsoft in 2015 only after shipping TypeScript 1.0, recruited by a former Visual Studio colleague to build what became Facebook Marketplace; the draws were mission (affordable used goods, environmental reuse, small businesses) and escaping the "safe cocoon" of the Microsoft stack after 11.5 years.
- Culture contrast: Facebook sprints were a week vs four weeks on Visual Studio, and she loved the poster "nothing at Facebook is somebody else's problem" ([big tech culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md)).
- Dogfooding is her signature practice, from using Visual Studio to build Visual Studio, to selling household items to seed Marketplace inventory in the Seattle/Hawaii test launch, to reproducing VR floor-height bugs at home — she frames it as a manager's "maker time," a trust-builder with engineers and PMs, and now "using Claude Code to build Claude Code" ([developer tools](../concepts/developer-tools.md)).
- Practical dogfooding tips: tie it to things that bring you joy (working out and knitting-while-watching-movies in VR), and run weekly leadership dogfooding sessions with PM/design partners for fast feedback on nearly-shipped features.
- Kindness as an engineering value crystallized during COVID when a report cheerfully let her cancel a 1:1 to take a rare FaceTime slot with her grandmother in assisted living: "in a world where you can be anything, be kind."
- She wasn't job hunting; using Claude (Sonnet) via Meta's internal DevMate tool convinced her AI was "already here," and Anthropic's genuine safety-first mission-orientation — visible from day-one onboarding, not just a tagline — pulled her from a VR role she loved ([AI-era engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md), [AI coding tools](../concepts/ai-coding-tools.md)).
- Claude Code's iteration loop (idea → build → internal launch → feedback → public launch) feels faster than even early Marketplace; preserving that agility as the team grows is her top concern.
- The feedback that changed her career was feedback on receiving feedback: don't "debug" the giver with questions in the moment — stay in read-only mode, listen, and sit with it until the next day, because making someone justify constructive feedback discourages them from ever giving it again.
