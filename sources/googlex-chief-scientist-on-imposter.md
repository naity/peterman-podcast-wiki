# GoogleX Chief Scientist On Imposter Syndrome, Career Growth, Project Taste | Carey Nachenberg

[Carey Nachenberg](../entities/carey-nachenberg.md) went from being the first intern at Peter Norton Computing in 1992 to the senior-most engineer (Fellow, IC10/VP-equivalent) at Symantec, then Chief Scientist of a stealth cybersecurity moonshot at [Google](../entities/google.md) X (which became Chronicle), then autonomous vehicles at Lyft — all while lecturing CS at [UCLA](../entities/ucla.md) for 25 years. The episode is unusually candid about how [imposter syndrome](../concepts/imposter-syndrome.md) kept a top-of-ladder IC at one company for two decades, how top-level IC recruiting actually works, why project taste beats raw intelligence, and how AI is changing what his students learn and what skills will matter for engineers.

## Key takeaways

- Nachenberg's growth to Fellow came from repeatedly finding gaps — impactful projects the business needed that nobody was stepping up to do (e.g., detecting self-mutating polymorphic viruses, which became his master's thesis and shipped in the product). Symantec never assigned him work; he picked everything himself. See [career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) and project taste.
- At the Distinguished/Fellow level, Symantec [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) ran through a company-wide committee of technologists led by the CTO, reviewing portfolios quarterly for cross-division consistency; Nachenberg himself was made Fellow without applying, after the Veritas merger forced a level-set.
- Top-level [hiring](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md) is network-driven: Symantec's former COO pulled him into GoogleX with no application — a meet-and-greet, ~8 interviews (no coding, one design, mostly leadership questions and sales pitches), offer in six weeks. But Google "downgraded" him from a Symantec L10-equivalent to L8, saying "we can't just hire fellows."
- He stayed at Symantec 21+ years largely because of [imposter syndrome](../concepts/imposter-syndrome.md) — comfortable, known, but stagnant and unhappy; what broke the logjam was an external pull ("we want to interview you"). His top regret: he should have left earlier, and his advice is to stay only as long as you're learning.
- Intelligence is overrated for [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md): he saw a 200-IQ engineer stuck at L4 at Google for lack of communication, collaboration, and business impact. What matters more: communication skills, collaboration, and relentlessly focusing on the company's/customer's *outcomes* and the metrics that measure them, not your own technical outcomes.
- At GoogleX he helped build Chronicle's product Backstory — ingesting petabytes/week of enterprise security telemetry and indexing it so threat investigations that took 30 minutes per query ran in ~2 seconds ("Google-search speed" for security data).
- At Lyft he drove the architecture shift from hand-coded robotics algorithms (hundred hand-tweaked parameters) toward an end-to-end neural-network driver wrapped in a deterministic safety layer — done purely through [influence](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md) with skeptical roboticists since he had no AV background. He then lost credit for it because he let others tell the story; lesson: document your work and "toot your own horn" without embellishing, and claim credit only for the parts you did or committees will smell it ([corporate politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md)).
- On [speaking well](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md): design explanations for the 30th–50th percentile (himself as the "lower common denominator"), practice constantly (give optional lunchroom talks), and know that people equate great communication with intelligence — his Stuxnet talk at X made people call him "one of the smartest people I've ever met."
- On [AI-era engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md): he let students use LLMs on projects and regretted it — project scores diverged sharply from exam understanding — so he's banning them for projects while allowing them for learning. If AI stays "good but not AGI-good," software engineering remains a great field; if it reaches AGI-level coding, the valuable skills shift to outcome analysis, customer understanding, and clearly communicating requirements — essentially being a genuinely competent product manager, of which he says he's met very few.
- [Advice to his younger self](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md): don't let fear of failure hold you back; focus on outcomes and speak to your audience's needs (senior leaders don't care how the algorithm works); find a manager who trusts you and gives you rope — "a manager can make or break your career and your life and your happiness."
