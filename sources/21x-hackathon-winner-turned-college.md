# 21x Hackathon Winner Turned College Dropout | Jia Chen Interview

Jia Chen is a 21-year-old who won 21 hackathons and co-founded her own startup, Sprint.dev, all while being a content creator. She succeeded in tech despite attending a non-target school and recently dropped out of college to work on her startup full time. The episode covers how to actually win hackathons (team dynamics and judging, not tech-stack flexing), how to stand out when your school isn't a brand name, and how to decide whether dropping out to found a company is a sound bet — a rare early-career and [startups-and-founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md) perspective in a podcast otherwise dominated by senior big-tech engineers.

## Key takeaways

- Winning hackathons comes down to team dynamics, judge interaction, and smart project execution — not stacking credentials or showing off a complicated tech stack. Many strong teams lose by spending their whole presentation discussing the tech stack instead of letting judges interact with the project.
- Pick hackathon teammates based on skills and drive, not credentials: look at past projects, GitHub activity, and ambition rather than resumes. Jia has won with community-college students who had less experience but stronger determination.
- A common technical failure mode at hackathons is splitting into frontend and backend teams and merging late; connecting both early and iterating together works far better.
- Hackathons are a [hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md) channel, not just resume filler: recruiters attend larger hackathons scouting future interns and fast-track candidates for internship applications.
- Coming from a non-target school: "if your school isn't an outlier, make sure you are" — take leadership roles in clubs, spearhead big projects, and build a personal brand ([career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)).
- Jia's core meta-skill is "agency" — the ability to take action and think on your feet. Hackathons trained this, along with time efficiency and a "get it done and move on" mindset.
- On college: it provides structure if you don't know what to learn, but isn't necessary; for Jia it was helpful early on but dulled her creativity and passion for learning ([teaching-and-communication](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md)).
- Don't just drop out on a whim — set preconditions first: build projects, test the idea, evaluate your technical skills and investor network, and verify the prospects look good before leaving school ([startups-and-founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md), [regrets-and-advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)).
- As a founder: move fast and continuously ship, but guard against burnout; stay open to connections and learning opportunities even when they don't seem immediately useful.
- Her personal brand grew out of vlogging hackathon experiences at a friend's encouragement; she optimizes for authenticity over planned content.
