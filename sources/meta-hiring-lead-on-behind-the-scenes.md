# Meta Hiring Lead On Behind The Scenes of Senior+ Eng Hiring

Austen McDonald led mobile hiring at [Meta](../entities/meta.md), sat on hiring committees, and conducted hundreds of interviews; he now coaches candidates and has written a book on behavioral interviewing. The episode is an insider's tour of senior+ engineering [hiring](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md): what hiring committees actually look at first, how leveling gets decided (and negotiated mid-process), why lying rarely works, how much referrals really move the needle, what Meta's behavioral rubric literally contains, and how [OpenAI](../entities/openai.md) and [Anthropic](../entities/anthropic.md) assess their AI-specific values.

## Key takeaways

- The first thing McDonald did with a senior packet in a [Meta](../entities/meta.md) hiring committee was open the behavioral interview: "I would want to understand what is the scope that this engineer has operated at in the past" — scope, influence and insight drive leveling far more than coding performance at senior+ levels ([hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md)).
- The pipeline is: sourcer (screens on years of experience + previous title) → phone screen → onsite → hiring committee (hire/no-hire + level, may request follow-ups) → company-wide director approval. A staff engineer at [Google](../entities/google.md) gets slotted as staff at Meta, but non-FAANG "principal architects" get downleveled significantly — a manager of 50-75 people elsewhere may map to managing 8-10 FAANG engineers ([calibrations-and-ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md)).
- Lying about seniority basically doesn't survive the process: staff+ phone screens include a scope-check walkthrough of a large project, onsites are run by "highly calibrated" senior interviewers whose follow-ups expose vague or AI-generated stories, and in thousands of interviews McDonald never saw a confirmed successful fabrication.
- Leveling is communicated through three dimensions in every story: technical depth, organizational depth, and business impact. Contrast "I improved performance in the Facebook app" with "I organized across 100 engineers... 12 month performance improvement project... required multiple staff level engineers" — everyone in the loop is listening for the same thing, so a crisp "tell me about yourself" is the highest-leverage prep.
- The recruiter call is an evaluating call ("always be closing"): recruiters are incentivized by hires and advocate for you, but "they are not your friend" — be careful especially about early [compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md) anchoring; know salary-band disclosure laws (CA/NY) and line up multiple offers, which is the single biggest negotiation lever.
- You can negotiate level mid-process: ask the recruiter to run the loop for the target level so the committee gets all the signal at once, anchor every interviewer at the higher level in your opening, and pick the highest-scope stories — "there's always a question behind the question," and interviewers are pattern matchers checking whether you match the target level.
- Referrals matter most "on the bubble": a VP/director who actually worked with you puts their reputation on the line (and can even show up to advocate in the committee room), and you should effectively write the referral for them. But no referral overrides unanimous no-hire consensus for engineers — though at senior manager/director levels "it is a little bit more about who you know" ([mentorship-and-sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)).
- Meta's behavioral rubric — designed by "selection scientists" (industrial-psychology PhDs) — has five signals: driving results, embracing ambiguity, resolving conflicts, growing continuously, communicating effectively. Get the rubric from your recruiter in advance and run the "decode, select, deliver" loop: decode the question behind the question, select the fitting high-scope story, deliver it like a movie trailer remixed for the asked signal.
- Story choice priorities in order: scope, relevance, recency (2-3 years, scaled by career length), uniqueness across rounds. Top senior-candidate mistakes: wrong-scope stories, over-context (if 30 seconds pass without an action verb, rethink), stories open to uncharitable interpretation (who created that tech debt?), skipping non-technical work (mentoring, delegation, roadmap influence), and unstructured multi-year epics — fix with an upfront "table of contents."
- Prep allocation by level: juniors should nail technical and show thinking/motivation (slope, not accomplishments — he once pushed through a modest-resume candidate on idea-quality who later made staff); mid-levels now need balance because [agentic coding](../concepts/ai-coding-tools.md) makes end-to-end ownership the differentiator ([ai-era-engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md)); senior+ should start behavioral prep "when you start doing your leetcode" — behavioral rounds are the differentiator and technical rounds become checkmarks, with coding flubs forgiven at staff/principal/manager levels.
- Company-values tailoring for AI labs: [OpenAI](../entities/openai.md)'s "Feel the AGI" wants genuine optimism about what AI can do; [Anthropic](../entities/anthropic.md)'s "Hold Light and Shade" wants you to articulate both positive and negative implications — when telling an AI project story there, explicitly cover how you assessed and mitigated risks.
- Likability bias "scales exactly with level": structured rubrics suppress beer-test vibes for juniors, but senior roles are explicitly about influencing people, so confidence and "casual excellence" in the room legitimately count ([influence-and-leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
