# Industry Secrets We Wish We Knew Before Graduating | Staff Eng Talk @ UCLA

This episode is a recorded Q&A talk that host Ryan Peterman gave together with Ricky (Staff Engineer at [Google](../entities/google.md) by 28) at [UCLA](../entities/ucla.md) for UPE (Upsilon Pi Epsilon), a club both belonged to as students — the event drew over 250 RSVPs. It's a rapid-fire distillation of everything two Staff engineers wish someone had told them before graduating: how luck and visibility drive promotions, Big Tech vs startups, job hopping, AI and job security, and how to convert internships into return offers. Note: this early episode's page carries only the host's written summary, not a transcript.

## Key takeaways

- Luck influences [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md), but you can expand your "luck surface area" by taking initiative to seek new opportunities and being prepared when they arrive.
- Perfect code isn't enough: the best engineers they've met weren't necessarily the top programmers from college but had balanced technical and soft skills — knowing how to influence others, collaborate in teams, and write concisely ([influence and leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md), [teaching and communication](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md)).
- Since people decide promotions, you must advocate for yourself: visibility is an important part of growing your [career](../concepts/career-growth.md) alongside doing quality work.
- Both speakers would pick [Big Tech](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md) over [startups](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md) straight out of college — it gives instant credibility and solid foundational skills — then pivot to startups later with brand equity and expertise in hand.
- Lines of code != impact: the industry rewards results, not effort, and writing less code while achieving greater impact is preferable.
- Big Tech won't make you crazy rich: climbing levels is a reliable path to financial stability and freedom, but exceptional wealth requires breakout startup opportunities ([compensation and equity](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md)).
- Job hopping helps early in your career, but promotions beyond Senior/Staff depend on influence and credibility that take time to build at each company, so late-career hopping becomes counterproductive.
- On [AI-era engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md): LLMs are productivity tools like the many non-AI tools engineers already use; SWE jobs look secure for roughly five years, with longer-term impacts unpredictable.
- For college recruiting, resume projects are just the minimum — persistence and willingness to join smaller companies early accelerates experience building; for internship return offers, companies judge whether you finish well-defined projects quickly with high-quality code, and proposing improvements afterward strengthens your case ([hiring and interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md)).
