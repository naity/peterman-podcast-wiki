# Amazon VP Reveals Everything He's Seen In Corporate Politics | Ethan Evans

Ethan Evans is a retired [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) VP (his second appearance on the show) who spent his career watching "pretty much every possible type of corporate politics" up close. In this nearly three-hour episode he walks through the mechanics of [corporate politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md) topic by topic — empire building, scope stealing, reorgs used to manage people out, mutinies against bad managers, and the precise political wording that gets you what you want without sounding like a threat — with concrete stories from his time running orgs of hundreds of engineers.

## Key takeaways

- Empire building persists because headcount is the one promotion metric nobody can debate: impact is subjective, but "Ethan has 42 people and Ryan has 17" is a fact. After Evans left [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md), some orgs required roughly 80-90 reports for director [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md), creating a "several hundred thousand dollars a year bonus quite literally for accumulating 90 people," and leaders would temporarily park people under someone to check the box.
- Reorgs are "hidden chess": the stated business narrative often coexists with private motives — positioning must-keep people with "something juicier," giving people you want gone less scope, or setting someone up for promotion. Leaders are storytellers constructing defensible narratives for auditing parties ([management](../concepts/management.md)).
- Being quietly dutiful is a career liability: managers under resource pressure unconsciously defer the nice person because they'll accept less. Evans tells coaching clients "you are a very nice person... and that is not a compliment in this context" ([career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)).
- The wording of career pressure matters enormously: instead of threatening to quit, Evans told his manager "I need to understand how important my career is to Amazon because my career is very important to me" — same underlying message, no extortion frame. Delivery, warmth and timing determine whether identical words land as ambition or aggression ([influence-and-leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
- Demanding high performers are paradoxically easier to manage: tell them no and they self-select out; make a deal ("you want this thing, I will help you get it... you're going to show incredible performance on the things I need") and follow through. Evans once got a senior manager promoted to director by having him move to India for three years to build Amazon's dev center there.
- Big companies keep "forward looking slates" of who gets promoted over 6/12/18/24-month windows, and modern quota systems (e.g. at [Google](../entities/google.md), two L8-to-L9 promotions per half in an org) create literal queues — one of Evans' coaching clients calculated "I think I'm number four, I'm not going to make it." Senior IC positions "cost a million dollars a piece, so we don't just want to hand them out like popcorn" ([calibrations-and-ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md)).
- Never mutiny alone: a single report complaining about their manager is easily dismissed as "you're the problem" because agreeing creates three problems for the skip (manage the manager out, backfill, absorb the work). Two or three corroborating reports flip the calculus — Evans only acted on a leader mistreating women on his team once multiple reports surfaced.
- To escape a bad manager without throwing them under the bus: first try a pure business case for a move; if that fails, keep it blame-free ("maybe it's him, maybe it's me... I need a change"). Evans broke weeks of resistance to his own role change by reframing his VP's choice as "keep me on your team or lose me entirely" — via a competing SVP offer and "once I give my word, I have to go."
- Defending scope from raiding orgs: highlight the unglamorous work bundled with the glamorous, respond thornily and fast to attacks, point at strong metrics to delay, and above all "be the better storyteller." Attackers underestimate other teams' complexity — S3 dismissed as "just a big disk drive" — because "respect between two engineering teams is inversely proportional to their distance."
- Influence and politics use the identical skill set; only motive differs ("Darth Vader had executive presence in spades"). Treat cross-functional partners as people with competing pressures, not APIs; use "how can you help me" framing; pre-plan openings like chess ([influence-and-leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
