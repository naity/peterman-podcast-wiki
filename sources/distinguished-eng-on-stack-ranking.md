# Distinguished Eng On Stack Ranking, Competing with Bezos, Regrets | Bryan Cantrill

Bryan Cantrill (creator of DTrace) joined Sun Microsystems as a new grad in 1996, rose to Distinguished Engineer, quit Oracle 45 days after the acquisition, competed head-on with [AWS](../entities/aws.md) as Joyent's CTO, and co-founded Oxide Computer in 2019 to build rack-scale machines. The episode is a bracing tour through tech's boom/bust cycles — the dot-com collapse, 35 rounds of layoffs at Sun — plus his scathing views on [stack ranking](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md), why goaling on [promotion](../concepts/promotions.md) is a recipe for a midlife crisis, and what made Jeff Bezos "the apex predator of capitalism."

## Key takeaways

- Cantrill refused to interview at [Microsoft](../entities/microsoft.md) on principle ("Bill Gates robbed me of my childhood"): DOS/Windows ignored the memory protection that had existed in microprocessors for years, and the company's vaporware announcements and OS bundling were anti-competitive — "not an opinion... a judicial finding of fact." Discovering multitasking Unix in college was mind-bending by contrast.
- He chose Sun (a company he initially didn't want to join) because meeting Jeff Bonwick and Kevin Clark was "a bolt of lightning" — shared passion trumped the company's brand; Sun in 1996 was the only company still investing in operating systems, "Unix's darkest hour."
- On [career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md): "I would much rather be under promoted than over promoted." He never goaled on rank; promotions "just happened along the way." Formalized performance review "never resulted in me having higher performance" — the highlights of his career were hard bugs and impossible projects, never promo moments. Sun's rule that a "superlative" rating forced a promotion produced apologetic reviews withholding the top grade because promos were rationed to every 18 months.
- Sun's Distinguished Engineer process was a "country club" where existing DEs voted on admissions — "the wrong place for democracy" — with a side door for acqui-hired CTOs. He avoided the politics only because CTO Greg Papadopoulos personally presented his case ("I don't expect anyone to vote against this") and it passed unanimously. Oxide has no ranks at all: "ranks are corrosive."
- Stack ranking ("rank and yank," as [Intel](../entities/intel.md) called it) is "organizational cancer": terminating a bottom percentage incentivizes managers to keep dead weight as wood-chipper fodder — he saw this happen at Sun — and it "teaches you that your team are adversaries" when teams, not individuals, do extraordinary things ([layoffs](../concepts/layoffs.md), [calibrations-and-ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md)).
- To an ambitious engineer goaling on Distinguished Engineer or financial independence, he offers a third path: find what gives you meaning, because he can introduce you to miserable DEs and miserable wealthy people. Dot-com winners who cashed out often struggled for a decade afterward ("now what?"); he calls the post-goal void "postpartum in engineering" and actively managed it at Oxide after shipping their first rack ([regrets-and-advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md), [compensation-and-equity](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md)).
- Sun lost 98% of its market value after the dot-com bust (trading below cash at one point) and ran ~35 rounds of layoffs over eight years; engineers scripted the employee directory into a daily "obits" report to track who was gone. McNealy's refusal to lay off early was, he says, a mistake — he waited too long.
- He quit Oracle within ~45 days because he was "ashamed" to work there — Sun had customers' trust and Oracle doesn't ("an octopus that knows how to feed itself") — while other engineers literally queued up to get on the layoff list for rich severance ([corporate-politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md), [big-tech-culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md)).
- Bezos as "apex predator of capitalism": [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) hit the motherlode with cloud, hid AWS margins by not breaking out revenue, and relentlessly cut prices at every re:Invent 2010–2015 — convincing everyone the cloud was a terrible business so no one would enter, while Joyent, competing directly, knew the margins were great. He pressed his advantage "the right way — a great product at a reasonable price."
- [Oxide's founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md): a famous VC who once emailed "I will fund literally anything you put in front of me" cut him off 45 seconds into the pitch ("If you are talking about starting a computer company, I want nothing to do with it... doesn't sound like me"). Cantrill's counter-move is a lesson: when a VC says "how can I help?", have a concrete answer — he asked for an intro to the founder of a failed similar startup and learned that company died from a tiny real market (six active-directory servers per giant bank) plus a prematurely scaled go-to-market team. A founder called Oxide "a suicide mission"; Oxide's minimum viable product was a full rack that took 3+ years. They deliberately avoid both "compete with [Nvidia](../entities/nvidia.md)" and "partner with Nvidia" doors, focusing on general-purpose compute.
- Boom/bust advice for the [AI era](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md): booms and busts are endemic to software; be your own judge. People told him operating systems were done in 1996 and that you couldn't start a computer company in 2019 — ignore both the hype and the pessimism, and don't pick a space just because it's hot.
