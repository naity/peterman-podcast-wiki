# Shopify Distinguished Eng (L10) on Principal+ Engineering, Career Story, Regrets | Ilya Grigorik

Ilya Grigorik is a Distinguished Engineer (L10, a VP-level role) at [Shopify](../entities/shopify.md) whose career ran from founding PostRank out of Waterloo, through its acquisition by [Google](../entities/google.md), to Director of Developer Relations at Google and then back to senior IC work. The episode traces the full arc — the acquisition mechanics, repeatedly seesawing between manager and IC, what Principal+ and Distinguished-level engineering actually requires — and distills his signature career philosophy: "don't be the best, be the only."

## Key takeaways

- Waterloo's co-op rotation (study/work alternating every semester, six internships) is its superpower: it gives students ~6 stigma-free "shots on goal" to try industries, company sizes, and work types before committing, plus pragmatic engineering experience beyond academics — a key theme of [career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md).
- Ilya deferred (and never finished) a University of Toronto grad degree when PostRank — his "better PageRank" that ranked posts by social engagement signals like comments and likes — closed a funding round; he reasoned that actually talking to investors beat taking entrepreneurship courses ([startups-and-founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md)).
- The Google acquisition began casually: the Google Analytics lead PM approached the team after a conference talk. Google, then racing Facebook on social (Google+ era), wanted PostRank's team and social-analytics experience more than its product — closer to an acquihire; the product was rebuilt from scratch inside Google Analytics at vastly larger scale (design changes measured in exabytes) ([hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md)).
- In acquisitions, everyone on the acquired team — founder/CTO included — still went through Google's full interview panel to determine leveling; team, IP, and customers are negotiated as distinct threads of the deal.
- Told he was "on track for director" in Google Analytics, Ilya instead took a lateral step (with likely down-leveling) to be an IC in the "Make the Web Fast" group, trading the clear-cut promotion path for self-directed time, people he looked up to, and a domain he loved — he calls it the right decision in retrospect ([non-linear-careers](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md)).
- His IC standards work (W3C/IETF browser performance metrics) created an ecosystem-adoption problem that "does not scale with one human," so he switched back to management as Director of Developer Relations — a "tour of duty" model where IC vs. manager is chosen per problem, to compress 3–5 years of organic adoption into 1–2 ([management](../concepts/management.md), [influence-and-leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
- His measure of success as a principal engineer is making himself unnecessary — the "Homer Simpson disappearing into the bushes" test: if the team keeps executing well after he pulls back, he succeeded ([mentorship-and-sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)).
- Anyone who asks for a checklist job description for principal engineer is, by definition, not ready: at that level you're "parachuted into foreign terrain" and expected within about seven days to map the land, build alliances with directors/VPs, and find where your skillset applies.
- Distinguished Engineer is VP-equivalent and defined by demonstrated dynamic range: technically from bare metal up to business requirements, and in execution mode from fog-of-war startup iteration to slow, careful platform/API design with years-long second-order consequences ([systems-design](../concepts/systems-design.md)).
- "Don't be the best, be the only": rather than maximizing one domain (fragile if the world shifts), build a portfolio of 80–90% competencies — design, sales, marketing lexicon, writing, management — whose intersection makes you the only person who can solve certain problems; it's "literally probability math." He argues LLMs make acquiring that 80% faster than ever ([ai-era-engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md)).
- The senior person in the room is rarely the most knowledgeable; the job is to find the most knowledgeable people and help them navigate the "messy middle," and to ask curious questions without appearing to attack the team's work.
- Career regret / advice: don't let past success become a filter that stops you publishing and trying things — early embarrassing blog posts and code were the feedback engine of his growth; also, IC↔manager transitions should carry no stigma in either direction ([regrets-and-advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md), [teaching-and-communication](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md)).
- Top recommendation: author Ken Robinson (TED talks on rethinking factory-line education) — choose-your-own-adventure learning over age-binned curricula, which mirrors Ilya's skill-combining philosophy.

## Notable quotes

- "I have this belief that a better way, a more resilient way to build your career is to optimize for being the only person as opposed to the best person." — Ilya Grigorik (~31:24)
- "People that have to ask that question by definition are not principal engineers... the problems become ambiguous enough at that level where you kind of have to figure it out yourself." — Ilya Grigorik (~27:04)
- "If the team is dependent on me... that means that I'm probably doing something wrong because my job is to uplevel the team and make sure that they can deliver this thing self sufficiently." — Ilya Grigorik (~25:11)
- "Don't let the pressure of your success stop you from trying." — Ilya Grigorik (~40:17)
