# Airbnb Staff Eng on How To Not Get Stuck at Senior and Untold Rules of Calibrations

Laurent Charignon is a developer-productivity specialist who was a Staff engineer at [Stripe](../entities/stripe.md), [Airbnb](../entities/airbnb.md), and [Instagram](../entities/instagram.md) ([Meta](../entities/meta.md)), with earlier stints at [Apple](../entities/apple.md) and a spell as an engineering manager at Airbnb. The episode covers the unspoken rules of [calibrations](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md) that you only learn as a manager, his IC-to-manager-and-back transition, how he scaled himself as an uber tech lead over a 140-person dev-productivity org at Stripe, situational-leadership [coaching](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md), and why engineers get stuck at senior. It is unusually concrete about the mechanics of performance review systems and how to dispute them.

## Key takeaways

- Laurent pitched his own transition into [management](../concepts/management.md) at Airbnb — he went to his overloaded manager with a value proposition ("these people need more support, I can do that") rather than waiting to be asked, and argues most engineers hurt their [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) by never explicitly aligning with their manager on what they want.
- As a manager he ran a "surprise factor" metric: before delivering a performance review, he and the report each wrote the expected rating/promotion on paper and revealed them simultaneously — a match was a pass. Positive surprises are failures too, since an undervalued report should have been coached to take more risk earlier.
- Untold rules of [calibrations](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md): e.g. anyone who changes roles less than ~2.5 months before cycle end gets an automatic "meets expectations." This rule cost Laurent a likely staff [promotion](../concepts/promotions.md) when he switched to management mid-cycle. These rules are developed locally per org, mutate over time, and can be disputed.
- How to dispute bureaucracy: never fight the rule in the calibration room where reputations are at stake — go one-on-one, gather evidence, and first learn the story behind the rule; sometimes the story convinces you, sometimes it reveals a stale precedent ([corporate politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md)).
- When leadership wants to measure engineer output by lines of code or PR counts, redirect the question: at Stripe he instead instrumented the journey of a code change (think → write → CI → review → merge) which surfaces actionable org-level friction instead of unfairly ranking individuals ([developer tools](../concepts/developer-tools.md)).
- Joining Stripe as "uber TL" over 35 (later 140) engineers, he built credibility by embedding in each team for weeks at a time, refusing to justify decisions with "it worked at Facebook," and led almost entirely by asking questions — he claims he never outright rejected a design in his whole tenure, telling people what to do only during incidents.
- Coaching insight from a failing Facebook bootcamp mentee: situational leadership — match style (directing, selling, supporting, delegating) to the learner's mastery and motivation. His mentee had already self-studied, so directive coaching was suffocating him; switching to supporting turned him into the strongest of the four ([mentorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)).
- Why people get stuck at senior: the senior-to-staff jump is a bigger mindset shift than staff-to-senior-staff, because staff requires independently owning the full cycle — identify problems, pitch them, solve end to end, document — while seniors typically solve problems others chose ([promotions](../concepts/promotions.md)).
- To find staff-worthy problems, "channel your inner frustration": Stripe institutionalized this as friction logs — screenshot every step of a flow and interrogate each moment of friction; Laurent could find 50–60 improvements in a single code-change flow.
- [Airbnb](../entities/airbnb.md)'s designer-led culture made low-level infra harder to sell, but produced outside-the-box wins like hiring actual firefighters/crisis-management professionals to run [incident management](../concepts/incident-management.md), which he carried to every later job.
- Boredom is a signal something is wrong; he left management (back to IC) once his team ran itself, and left Airbnb partly because of the four-year equity cliff ([compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md)).
- Most career-impacting book: Radical Candor by Kim Scott, for building trust in manager-report relationships.
