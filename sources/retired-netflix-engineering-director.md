# Retired Netflix Engineering Director On Regrets, Video Engineering, Hiring Stories

David Ronca joined [Netflix](../entities/netflix.md) in 2007, built its encoding technology team from one person (himself) to ~55, and grew to engineering director before joining [Meta](../entities/meta.md) in 2019 as a director over Facebook's video processing org (later transitioning to principal engineer). Now retired after a 36-year career, he covers the inside reality of the legendary Netflix culture memo and why it didn't scale, how he hired engineers with a near-perfect batting average, coming into Meta as an outside senior leader, keeping Facebook video alive when COVID made every day New Year's Eve, and how a stage-3 colon cancer diagnosis permanently rewired his views on work-life balance.

## Key takeaways

- [Netflix](../entities/netflix.md)'s "no brilliant jerks" rule was revolutionary in the early 2000s: before Netflix, every company Ronca worked at tolerated indispensable prima donnas, and he watched one "critical" pack-rat engineer quit with no ill effect — the [big-tech-culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md) insight being that companies survive the departure of difficult heroes just fine.
- Patty McCord (chief people officer, who interviewed every early hire) told him in his own interview that Netflix doesn't value 24/7 work — "blow us away with what you can do in an eight hour day" — and later publicly thanked, then reprimanded, a manager for working around the clock. Ronca once ordered a burned-out engineer to take a vacation regardless of outages; the team stabilized the system precisely because they stepped back.
- Netflix's culture didn't scale: one flat "senior software engineer" level plus "personal top of market" [compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md) (match any competing offer) worked with a small all-senior team, but with juniors and growth it produced irrational pay gaps nobody could rationalize without leveling, and no individual credit meant leaders got the recognition — which hurts and eventually drives out your best engineers.
- His [hiring](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md) philosophy: leetcode "does not tell you anything about an engineer." All engineering disciplines share one foundation — the ability to understand complex systems, strong technical intuition, and making good decisions without enough data — and that foundation is innate; the coding can be taught. He proved it by hiring a train-automation engineer and a civil engineer from wastewater treatment (whose sewage plant was, he realized mid-whiteboard, a complex finite state machine); both "killed it."
- Level advice for big tech offers: when in doubt, take the lower level. Come in as an IC5 executing like an IC6 and the bonuses, multipliers, and RSU refreshers will follow; come in over-leveled and struggling and "there's no way to fix that problem."
- The strongest engineer he ever worked with is Ioannis ("Giannis") Katsavounidis — a former signal-processing professor whose work with Ronca birthed content-based encoding and later convex hull encoding, now the encoding model at both Netflix and Meta.
- Joining Meta as an outside director of a well-functioning ~60-person team taught him his biggest [leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md) lesson: two weeks in, a report told him candidly "you've been here two weeks, you're already telling us what we need to do." His summary: shut up and build trust first — if a team is executing well, "your job is to not break it first and then help it move forward second."
- During COVID, Facebook video hit New Year's Eve peak load every single day from April 2020; the team found race conditions and stabilized systems that had never run at that sustained load, while half the planet (3.5B users vs Netflix's ~300M) depended on the apps — the experience that made him finally grasp Meta's real value proposition in the developing world.
- At InterVideo's 24/7 startup culture he worked 10am–10pm (or 2–3am) seven days a week until, in January 2004, he was diagnosed with stage-3 colon cancer (25% five-year prognosis) with three kids who barely knew him. His [advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md): "don't wait till you get cancer or have a heart attack" to build work-life balance into your DNA — crunch must be the exception, not the rule.
- The most valuable thing Meta taught him was objective performance management: [calibrations](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md) where managers must argue with data over who deserves credit, and PSC packets that permanently record individual achievement — the thing Netflix's team-first culture lacked. Handing a direct report a Redefines Expectations rating was "the greatest joy" of his leadership career.
- The book that most shaped him: *12 Secrets to Microsoft Success* — betting the company, good failure, dogfooding, hiring the top 5%, and Bill Gates's deliberate hiring freeze; he argues FAANG leaders repeating that lesson would have seen the 2021 overhiring / 2022 contraction coming.
- [Career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) regrets and advice: he "wasted" his first six years at large failing companies (GM/Hughes, an IBM division sold to Siemens); work on hard problems ("if your job is not difficult, you're not growing"), work for visionary leaders who can also execute, and start financial planning on day one so you reach your 50s financially independent.

## Notable quotes

- **David (quoting Patty McCord):** "We don't value 24/7 work here and if you just come in and work all the time, we're not going to be impressed... if you want to impress us, blow us away with what you can do in an eight hour day." (~00:03:35)
- **David:** "If this company cannot survive without you here on the clock, we got a problem. You need to take a vacation. And I don't care what happens, if things break, they break." (~00:06:10)
- **David:** "When you see a brilliant engineering mind, everything else doesn't matter... Because these things can all be learned. They can be learned in a very short time. But you can't teach that." (~00:27:06)
- **David:** "If it's a broken team, your job is to unbreak it. But if it's a team that's actually executing well, your job is to not break it first and then help it move forward second." (~00:40:19)
