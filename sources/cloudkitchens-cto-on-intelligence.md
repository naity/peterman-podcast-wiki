# CloudKitchens CTO on Intelligence, Regrets, Steve Jobs and Travis Kalanick Stories

Brian Attwell reached Senior Staff at [Uber](../entities/uber.md) by age 25, then joined Travis Kalanick's stealth startup CloudKitchens and became CTO as his org doubled every six months. The episode is unusually blunt: he defends extreme work hours as the only path to very early seniority, argues IQ is the most predictive [hiring](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md) signal, explains how he rebuilt CloudKitchens' leveling ladder around "truth-seeking" instead of scope, critiques [Google](../entities/google.md)'s tolerance of non-performers, reveals CloudKitchens' thesis and stealth strategy, and tells a memorable story of accidentally killing his own project in front of Steve Jobs at [Apple](../entities/apple.md).

## Key takeaways

- Brian attributes his rapid [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) to joining a meritocratic, hypergrowth company plus extreme work ethic: he and a peer (Yi Wang) worked 9 AM to midnight plus weekends (90-100 hours/week) prototyping the options the team was debating, becoming de facto tech leads within months and top-3 committers across Uber mobile — "promotions are inevitable" at that point. He claims growth vs hours worked is superlinear because managers only hand strategic work to those already proven.
- He acknowledges the cost: he once collapsed on the street walking home from Uber, unable to move for 15 minutes; his burnout signal now is becoming grumpy/impatient, caught by his partner. A hospital scare (a medication error had him believing he would die) left him regretting not working *more*, not less.
- On luck: it dominates infrequent decisions (like picking a company every ~3 years) but not daily actions done well; picking a merit-based culture is the biggest lucky/choosy variable.
- On [hiring](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md): IQ correlating with job performance is "one of the most well-researched results in applied psychology"; good interviews force on-the-spot reasoning (e.g., read a 5-page postmortem in 5 minutes and debate restructuring the team). LeetCode is really a gameable proxy for IQ; he is seriously considering putting literal IQ tests in front of interviews, starting with PMs, and CloudKitchens' accounting team already does.
- His prioritization formula: expected value of the initiative × probability of success *with vs without you* ÷ your time cost — which often means the company's #1 priority is the wrong place to be, since it's already well-staffed; priority #3 in a tough spot is where you can be a hero. He advises juniors to mine the "why we care" column of planning spreadsheets to build expected-value intuition.
- He procrastinated on [management](../concepts/management.md) so badly that a report asked after three months, "Wait, Brian, are you my boss? I thought you were just a really helpful engineer." After fixing that, his org doubled every six months, driven by assuming any inherited org has an order-of-magnitude improvement available and truth-seeking with the team (e.g., killing low-value integrations out of 1,200, which cut pages and improved the product).
- CloudKitchens redesigned its leveling ladder after Travis Kalanick rejected the Uber-style draft as "lazy" for over-indexing on scope: Brian gathered ~1,000 real examples of differentiated performance from books and years of [calibration](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md) notes, distilled them into 250 attributes, then used principal component analysis to get 12 skill gradients. The ladder rewards being right when questioning foundational decisions (truth-seeking) rather than position-oriented competencies like "influencing other teams," which he says reward being loud and political and encourage empire building.
- On [big tech culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md): companies like [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) underpay top performers "because they can get away with it"; at Google he saw a non-performing engineer protected because "he's such a nice guy," and multiple managers told him of an unwritten decade-old policy of keeping smart people on staff even if idle so competitors couldn't have them. His law of entropy: it takes lots of energy to raise a standard and almost none to lower it, so standards fall until the company dies or trauma (e.g., the DeepMind existential push) resets them.
- CloudKitchens' thesis, shared with rare permission: in a few decades no one should cook their own food. They've built robots cutting restaurant labor costs 50%, a lunch logistics network cutting meal costs 30-40%, real estate halving cost per meal, and Otter software processing 18% of the world's orders — chasing what he frames as trillions in value in the $2.5T/year US food market. The company stays stealth (no corporate site, no LinkedIn listings, no PR) to avoid distraction from negative tech press and to avoid spawning competitors, as happened publicly in Korea and with a $150M Vision-Fund-backed US copycat.
- Steve Jobs story ([regret](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md) framing): as an Apple intern he built a sunlight-readability OS tweak; when his loud mentor's praise drew Jobs to sit down, Brian downplayed it ("it's like 50, a hundred lines of code"), Jobs rolled his eyes and walked off, and the project died. He doesn't regret that — he couldn't have pitched better at 18 — his real regrets are times he knew the truth mid-project and stayed quiet. Advice to younger self: "be more courageous and less embarrassed... and maybe go to therapy."

- "An interview that anyone can pass regardless of IQ if they can practice enough does not build a strong engineering team." — Brian, ~00:12:30
