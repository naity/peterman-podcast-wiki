# Honest Big Tech Layoff Story After 25 Years

The guest, known by his YouTube pseudonym "Asian Dad Energy," spent roughly 18 years climbing the digital consulting ladder (junior engineer through senior enterprise architect to group technical director) before jumping to a chief architect role at a big tech company, where he worked about seven years until being laid off — a story he vlogged about and which went viral. The episode is an unusually candid look at [layoffs](../concepts/layoffs.md) from the inside, at how the tech consulting industry actually works, and at [big tech compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md) mechanics (RSUs, ESPP) from someone who tripled his pay by switching.

## Key takeaways

- He got advance word through his network both that a layoff round was coming and that he was on the list; he then did a "switcheroo" to put himself at the top of the list in place of a couple of more junior engineers who were slated to be cut — buying them "a mere matter of months."
- His theory on the current [layoff](../concepts/layoffs.md) wave: companies are under pressure to show returns on massive AI investment, and cutting headcount keeps revenue flat while dropping costs, which — with marketing about "AI efficiency" — pumps the stock. He expects more layoffs across the sector because [AI](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md) over-investment hasn't yet yielded clear returns.
- His layoff package was structured and generous: garden leave (still employed, free to job hunt), months of severance, PTO payout, and education/equipment stipends. He believes severance is technically negotiable but wasn't in the mental state to bother.
- Consulting career path he traversed: junior SWE → senior SWE → tech lead → application architect → enterprise architect → senior enterprise architect → technical director. His most cherished growth period was senior SWE through senior enterprise architect, driven heavily by a series of strong [mentors](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md); growth and fun dropped once director-level work became selling to C-suites.
- Consulting has two delivery models: "get in, build, get out" fly-by-night projects (often sold purely to keep the pipeline full, producing dumpster-fire patchwork ecosystems) and multi-year retainer engagements where institutional knowledge makes maintenance and quality possible.
- A key consultant skill beyond tech: reading the topology of the client's [political environment](../concepts/corporate-politics.md) — how client teams and rival vendors interact — and opening space for your agency to keep the order book full.
- On why quality holds up despite non-technical clients who can't judge the work: engineering integrity (he had a literal university course on it), personal pride in the craft, and the firm's reputation — deliver a "steaming pile of turds" and there's no customer two. He says it's your obligation to tell a client their $20M investment is bad.
- People dislike consultants mostly because of management consulting, not technical consulting — management consultants recommend headcount cuts, so "consultants arriving" signals layoffs to client employees.
- Moving from consulting to big tech more than doubled, "closer to tripling," his [compensation](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md): higher base plus sign-on/recurring bonuses, RSUs vesting over 3–4 years, and an ESPP with ~15% discount. Holding his shares through the COVID digital boom made them double/triple in value. His advice: free money (ESPP discount, 401k match) should always be taken — though with spare cash he'd personally buy index funds, being risk-averse.
- After turning 40 he noticed learning new frameworks took weeks instead of days — his Olympic-gymnast analogy for aging in tech — and he leans on AI tools to polish his "halting, clumsy syntax."
- The viral layoff vlog started as therapy: his therapist suggested journaling and he was "just lazy," so he turned on a camera instead. He's now exploring semi-early retirement (health and mood "going up and up"), the YouTube channel, and small coding projects; job applications to big tech went nowhere in the cold market.
- [Speaking advice](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md) for engineers: relax, don't fuse your identity with the subject, and explain it like the audience is five — he tells the story of a brilliant Drupal cache-optimization engineer who couldn't communicate his work to less-technical audiences.
- Advice to his younger self: work hard, don't stop learning, and stay flexible — "if you can stay flexible, you'll survive," the single most profound lesson a mentor taught him.
