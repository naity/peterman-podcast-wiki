# Instagram Senior Staff Eng (IC7) On 3 Promos Through Redefining Expectations (Career Story)

Marius Schulz grew from IC4 to Senior Staff Engineer (IC7) at [Instagram](../entities/instagram.md)/[Meta](../entities/meta.md) by earning a "redefines expectations" rating for each of his three [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) — including as the first and only engineer on Threads Web for its first two months. A front-end specialist with a self-professed "burning passion for the web," he walks through each promo in unusual mechanical detail: why a greatly-exceeds half still didn't get him promoted, how doing next-next-level work reads in [calibrations and ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md), and what the IC4 vs IC5 vs IC6 "way of solving the same problem" looks like. It is one of the most concrete episodes on how Meta's performance machinery actually decides [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md).

## Key takeaways

- Ratings measure delivered impact; promotions (which are lagging) measure sustained next-level behaviors. Marius greatly exceeded expectations as an IC4 yet was blocked by one behaviors miss — being too pushy/insistent with his ideas in cross-functional collaboration — then blocked again when Meta froze all promotions during the early-COVID half.
- His response to blocked promos: keep doing next-level work anyway. "You can't be defeatist... you get promoted once you have for a sufficient period of time demonstrated next-level behaviors" — the growth continued on paper even when the title didn't.
- The senior (IC5) promo came from leading a clearly-classified IC6 project (10+ engineers plus PMs/designers) as an IC4 — doing next-next-level work is what separates "redefines" from "greatly exceeds," and calibration uses "laddering" to articulate why.
- Opportunity was partly luck (a senior engineer's paternity leave triggered a domino of everyone taking n+1 work) but he argues you can increase your "luck surface area" by loudly positioning yourself as the go-to person for a domain — in his case, the web platform expert.
- The staff (IC6) promo came from another high-pressure web project with a hard deadline where he flexed into the product-hybrid archetype: navigating "fog of war" ambiguity with only part-time PM support, then switching to coding-machine mode. His quality hierarchy: cut scope before cutting quality.
- His articulation of leverage: there is an IC4, IC5, and IC6 way to solve almost any problem. IC4 adds one React error boundary; IC5 audits and hardens the whole site; IC6 writes the direction-setting note classifying primary/secondary/tertiary UI failures plus lint rules so the entire company applies it without him ([influence and leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
- He deliberately switched to Jake Bolam's IG Web team when web stopped being a priority in his org — placing himself where his specialty aligned with business priorities, and rewrote the years-old Instagram web notifications panel on arrival.
- Threads Web: recruited via a hush-hush manager intro, he was the sole web engineer for the first 6-8 weeks, literally creating the code-name folder in the monorepo (he regrets the long code name after typing it tens of thousands of times), then hired and TL'd the team through launch of both logged-out and logged-in clients at threads.net. The IC7 promo self-review "was the easiest I ever submitted" because attribution was unambiguous.
- On ownership as DRI: recursive accountability — delegating meaty pieces to senior engineers doesn't absolve the overall DRI; his mentorship model is the driving instructor with a second steering wheel who gradually backs off as trust builds ([mentorship and sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)).
- Coding-machine stats: nearly 2,000 diffs in the Threads launch year (~10 per working day), while noting diff counts are only "directionally interesting" and gameable; not every IC7 diff needs IC7 complexity — sometimes cranking out unblocking work fast is the leveraged move.
- IC7+ promos need a business-shaped hole: without "an IC7-shaped hole that can only be plugged by an IC7-shaped puzzle piece," delivery alone won't get you promoted. He is not pushing for IC8 and rejects the management track because he loves the technical work ("the demigod feeling... I can see the matrix").
- Every level scared him before he reached it — he once swore he'd never consider IC7. Advice to his younger self, who blogged weekly for years no matter what: "Chill a little bit. It's fine. It's gonna be fine" — though that intensity is what got a recruiter to notice him.
