# 26 Year Old Meta Staff Eng (IC6) On Promotions, Redefining Expectations, Secret Equity Bonuses

Simon Kindström went from New Grad (IC3) to Staff Engineer (IC6) at [Meta](../entities/meta.md) in just three years — by age 26 — earning the top "Redefines Expectations" rating twice and receiving discretionary equity ("secret bonuses") along the way. The episode covers the mechanics of his unusually fast [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md), the role writing and his manager played, why business impact was the engine of his growth, and his honest account of trying management and switching back to IC. Note: this early episode's page has no published transcript — only Ryan's show notes with six takeaways (the full 55-minute conversation is audio/video only).

## Key takeaways

- His "Redefines Expectations"-earning work came from refusing to abandon experiments that produced unexpected bad results: he kept investigating root causes, modifying, and rerunning until he either understood the failure or got improved results — persistence through experimental failure was what separated his highest-impact work ([calibrations-and-ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md)).
- Writing well was a major career lever: write about your accomplishments, and put the impactful, interesting parts at the top — engineers commonly sink into technical details their audience finds irrelevant ([teaching-and-communication](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md)).
- Impact drove his [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md): his manager recruited him by explaining the business impact the work would have and how that would translate to career growth; the work then drove multiple significant revenue wins for Meta, which converted directly into top ratings, discretionary equity, and accelerated promotions ([compensation-and-equity](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md)).
- His manager was central to the story — coaching him closely throughout, with deep org knowledge and personal experience of rapid advancement before moving into management — a clear case of [mentorship and sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md) compounding IC growth.
- Scaling himself was the critical Staff-level skill: his delegation failure mode was abdicating ownership of others' outcomes and then scrambling at review time; the fix was the principle "delegate, don't abdicate" — keep accountability while empowering others.
- He tried [management](../concepts/management.md) as a deliberate experiment, found it pulled him away from technical work he loved amid heavy organizational thrash, and transparently switched back to the IC track rather than pretending it worked.

## Notable quotes

- "He didn't give up on those experiments until he understood why." — Ryan Peterman, show notes (on the redefines-expectations story)
- "It's important to put the impactful, interesting parts of the writing at the top." — show notes (Simon's writing advice)
- "Delegate, don't abdicate." — show notes (Simon's delegation principle)
