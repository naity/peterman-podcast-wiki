# Robinhood SWE Turned $1B+ Founder on Non-Linear Careers, Being Jaded About Promos, Startup Learnings

Jayendra Jog interned at Facebook and Pinterest, joined Robinhood as a new grad in 2018 when it was ~100 people, and left after its 2021 IPO to co-found Sei Labs, a crypto infrastructure company whose tokens reached a $10B+ market cap after raising $35M. The episode is a candid look at getting jaded about [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) early, coasting while still getting promoted in a hypergrowth company, the GameStop saga from the inside, and the mechanics and psychology of [leaving a stable job to found a startup](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md).

## Key takeaways

- Jay's [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) hinged on a lucky break: a "LeetCode and Chill" study night covered "number of islands," the exact question he got in his [Facebook](../entities/meta.md) internship onsite — without it, he believes none of his later career would have happened. Careers are [non-linear](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md), moving in step functions rather than linear growth.
- On big tech vs startups: decide what you're optimizing for. Max compensation upside only comes from very early-stage companies that hit it big; learning is faster at a small, quickly growing company. The best controllable heuristic is to surround yourself with really smart people — about a third of his Robinhood new grad class went on to found venture-backed companies.
- He got jaded nine months in: after months of 2 AM nights and a brutal overnight-batch on-call, he received a 5/5 performance rating with no promotion or compensation change attached, concluding effort wasn't commensurate with reward and deciding to "play the game" with minimum effort.
- Golden handcuffs kept him at Robinhood 3.5 years: exercising his options would have cost $400,000, so he waited for the July 2021 IPO; financially, his Robinhood equity ended up an order of magnitude better than an equivalent stint at [Google](../entities/google.md) or Facebook.
- Coasting still produced promotions because in a 10x-growth environment a competent engineer becomes load-bearing by default — he was the only person who knew how to do ~10 different things and got his first mentee 1.5 years in.
- Inside story of GameStop (Jan 28, 2021): Robinhood turned off buys not to manipulate prices but because T+2 settlement required billions in collateral overnight — while it was simultaneously fundraising billions and facing hostile regulators. He thinks Robinhood miscommunicated this "potentially on purpose." The episode inspired Sei: archaic financial rails need rebuilding.
- On founder risk: engineers vastly over-index on the risk of quitting — competent people always find jobs again, and a failed founding year makes you more valuable to early-stage [startups](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md) (though less so to big companies) because you learn product validation, marketing, BD, and go-to-market.
- Fundraising lesson from raising $35M in the post-Terra-collapse trough: get a tier-one lead investor (Multicoin, Solana's biggest backer, led their $5M seed) and "everything else is super easy after that" — other funds herd behind the stamp.
- His fastest-growth year was 2022, the year he started the company; engineering competence needed to found is low ("a competent L4 can do perfectly fine") because most businesses face mundane problems, not Meta-scale ones.
- Advice to his younger self: don't be so scared — think decisions through, then pull the trigger quickly; in a perfect world he'd have left Robinhood earlier. Sei has stayed lean (5-7 people in 2022 to ~60), which he credits for preserving culture and speed.
