# Intern to Microsoft Distinguished Engineer in 11 Promotions (Career Story)

David Fowler went from a Barbados-born Florida Tech student and 2006 [Microsoft](../entities/microsoft.md) intern to Distinguished Engineer (level 70) — 11 promotions over 17+ years at one company. He co-founded NuGet, built SignalR as a nights-and-weekends project that became an official product, and was one of two architects of .NET Core. The episode is a masterclass in "code is currency" bottom-up [career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) at a big company, plus rare insight into how partner/DE-level promotions actually get decided and how Microsoft's culture transformed under Satya Nadella.

## Key takeaways

- Microsoft levels run 59–70+ with two levels per title band (59/60 SDE I ... 63/64 senior, three levels of principal, three partner bands, then Technical Fellow); you can't tell from a title which notch someone occupies, and the principal band spans an immense range of experience ([calibrations-and-ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md)).
- He and a college friend differentiated at the career fair by demoing a game they'd built on a laptop — four internship offers followed; his 2006 interviews were pre-LeetCode puzzles (the Die Hard 3 water-jug problem, "count the gas stations in Florida," adding numbers in base negative two) ([hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md)). He was in the last intern class to visit Bill Gates' house.
- Early-career engine: "code is currency" — troll forums for customer problems, build the fix or prototype first, then show PMs/architects. First promotion came 8 months in; a director later admitted he told people "David has energy, go talk to him."
- NuGet was born in a meeting with Scott Guthrie about competing with Ruby on Rails/RubyGems; David was the founding engineer. SignalR started as his own nights-and-weekends brainchild (inspired by Google Docs co-editing, pre-WebSockets) with PM Damian Edwards until Microsoft made it official and gave him a team of ~8 ([open-source](../concepts/open-source.md)).
- On agency at big companies: "you can just do things." Building a reputation compounds — "the more success you have, the more leeway you get to fail, which gives you space to try more things." Ryan's framing: permissionless agency is a hack to bypass credibility requirements ([big-tech-culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md)).
- He deliberately found an architect whose career he wanted (David Ebbo), attached himself to him, coded his ideas, and mimicked his behaviors until he grew into the same role — a concrete [mentorship-and-sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md) tactic he repeated at every level, later requesting a Technical Fellow mentor (PowerShell inventor Jeffrey Snover) when he decided he wanted DE.
- The senior→principal leap is "doing more through others than yourself": as senior-level co-architect of .NET Core (~30 engineers, everything rebuilt from scratch), trying to co-review every change burned him out within a year; he had to learn delegation, trusting people, letting people fail, and treating not everything as equally urgent ([management](../concepts/management.md), [influence-and-leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
- Distinguished Engineer (level 70) is not a normal promo: technical fellows peer-review a decade-long body of work; his promotion package leaned heavily on ~7 years of deferred impact from .NET Core. Guido van Rossum is at his level; Technical Fellows above (Anders Hejlsberg with four successful languages, Dave Cutler of Windows NT) require industry-wide impact — "at those levels, everyone's a unicorn" ([promotions](../concepts/promotions.md)).
- Career-changing mentor advice: (1) don't ask generic promo questions — go directly to the deciders (CTOs) and ask where you stand; (2) after a strengths/weaknesses assessment, don't fix weaknesses — amplify strengths to superhuman: "no one gets promoted to these roles for being average"; staff your gaps with people who are great at them.
- Anti-meeting-trap discipline: after making partner he fell into all-day meetings and got "really sad"; his mentor's rule — as an IC your job is to think and build, so block a minimum of 8 hours/week and "decline, decline, decline." He still sends PRs daily (currently on Aspire) and refuses to be an architect who doesn't know the code ([regrets-and-advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)).
- CS ≠ software engineering: he'd design a university course where students are dropped into a huge unfamiliar codebase to fix one bug and add one test per assignment ("code archeologist" skills), and interviews should test debugging — handing candidates a crash dump — since diagnosing race conditions at 1am with 100 engineers on a call is the real high-leverage skill ([teaching-and-communication](../concepts/teaching-and-communication.md), [incident-management](../concepts/incident-management.md)).
- Big-company tips: before changing roles, ask when the last reorg was — reorgs are executive "motion" and tell you where the org is heading; and when evaluating a move, ignore what you'd love (that's easy) and study what you'd hate — that's what you'll have to live with ([corporate-politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md)).
- He stayed 17+ years despite better pre-IPO Twitter and GitHub offers (he was "depressed for a week" when Twitter IPO'd) because of accumulated network, autonomy, trust, and a new project every ~2 years by design; but he'd tell a mentee with a hot outside offer to go. Working on .NET Core for 10 years taught him "all the small issues you thought were small... will become big issues" — long tenure builds judgment that job-hoppers never get ([non-linear-careers](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md)).
- Satya-era culture change: from the XKCD org-chart guns (competing teams building the same thing) to incentivized collaboration — reviews dinged you for cloning another team's work, leaders modeled raising hands and pulling quiet people into discussions, and shouting disappeared ([big-tech-culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md)).
- Regrets and advice: early-career arrogance ("being right means nothing" without patience and empathy); passing on a friend's 2008 Bitcoin venture; and for his younger self — negotiate your salary and get multiple offers, something he didn't know was possible coming from Barbados ([compensation-and-equity](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md)).

## Notable quotes

- "I'm a code-first, ask questions later kind of person. My whole thing is code is currency. I can show you a working sample. It's not PowerPoint, it's not a document." — David Fowler (~14:12)
- "The funny thing is you get a chance to be more permissionless the better your track record is. The more success you have, the more leeway you get to fail, which gives you space to try more things." — David Fowler (~24:55)
- "You could work on the things you're not good at and be average at those, or you can work on things that you're really good at and be superhuman at those things... No one gets promoted to these roles for being average." — David Fowler (~47:55–48:16)
- "My GitHub is a graveyard of projects... Everything you build will teach you a new skill that you don't even know you have until later on." — David Fowler (~28:57)
