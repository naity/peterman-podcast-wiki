# Boris Cherny (Creator of Claude Code) On How His Career Grew

Boris Cherny is the creator of Claude Code at [Anthropic](../entities/anthropic.md), but this episode covers the lesser-known career behind it: an economics-major dropout and serial startup founder who joined [Meta](../entities/meta.md) under-leveled, grew from senior to Principal Engineer (IC8) through Facebook Groups and [Instagram](../entities/instagram.md), then joined Anthropic and built the defining AI coding agent. It is one of the richest episodes on [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) through side quests, [influence and leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md) without titles, and what [AI-era engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md) looks like from inside a frontier lab.

## Key takeaways

- Coming in under-leveled was lucky: it gave him space to build cool things without box-checking [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) pressure, and crushing expectations built a reputation that compounded — he advises against pushing for level+1 when switching companies.
- Side quests drove his growth: Undux (a simpler Redux alternative) became Facebook's most popular state-management framework after he scraped internal support groups, tallied complaints by team, and personally gave 20-40 tech talks while biking around campus. He also wrote a TypeScript book and ran SF's biggest TypeScript meetup ([programming-languages](../concepts/programming-languages.md), [developer-tools](../concepts/developer-tools.md)).
- The one technical book he recommends to everyone: Functional Programming in Scala — "thinking in types" changed how he codes more than anything else ([functional-programming](../concepts/functional-programming.md)). He got into terse languages after a motorcycle accident broke both arms and he needed fewer keystrokes.
- His IC6 (Staff) promo came from volunteering Facebook Groups as the guinea pig for Comet (the facebook.com rewrite) without asking permission — product teams that help build infra get to shape its abstractions instead of just consuming them.
- His IC7 promo story centers on public groups: a "one-line change" (participate without joining) with gnarly downstream data-model, spam, and moderation implications — and on earning senior engineer Bob's trust by disagreeing-and-committing to Bob's migration, then later pushing to reverse it once evidence was in.
- Scoping technique: time-box scoping to ~30 minutes (now Claude Code can map the systems for you), give experts a straw man rather than an open question, and for a hard org-wide data-model merge he ran a three-hour two-team architecture "design competition" that produced designs 80% identical — instant consensus on what to execute.
- On [imposter syndrome](../concepts/imposter-syndrome.md) while directing same-level engineers: "levels don't matter at all"; if you never feel imposter syndrome you aren't pushing yourself hard enough.
- His automation philosophy: tally every repeated code-review comment in a spreadsheet and write a lint rule after a few repeats — he eventually automated most of his own code reviews. If you hit a problem 2-3 times, others have it too; that "spidey sense" is the top of the side-project funnel.
- He moved to rural Japan for his wife's dream job, joined Instagram's Tokyo landing team never having used the app, and turned zero-timezone-overlap into a superpower: no meetings, no standing 1:1s, massively more coding than any peer, which fueled the Python-to-Hack migration he seeded by befriending the strongest skeptics over food and beer (later delegated to Jake Bolam; delegation rule from Andy Grove's High Output Management: delegate what you like and know so you can monitor it).
- His IC8 promo was a non-event ("Will said 'I think we should put you up for IC8', I said cool") — levels exist for company reasons, not engineering reasons; he optimized for interesting problems and delightful products.
- Why Claude Code succeeded: manager Ben Mann pushed him to "build for the model six months from now, not today." Claude Code was mediocre until Sonnet/Opus 4 landed; now ~80-90% of Claude Code is written with Claude Code, and Anthropic's per-engineer productivity rose ~70% despite the company tripling ([ai-coding-tools](../concepts/ai-coding-tools.md)).
- On AI slop: hold model code to exactly the same bar as human code; vibe coding is for prototypes/throwaway code, pairing-with-plan-mode is the default, and he still hand-writes the core query loop. Claude Code is also used far beyond code — data scientists run swarms of it, and half of Anthropic's sales team uses it.
- On competition (Codex etc.): he doesn't use competitor products and tells the team not to get sidetracked — more competitors is a sign of success; stay laser-focused on solving users' problems.
- Advice to his younger self: "Just use common sense" — big companies systematically pull you away from it.

## Notable quotes

- "Don't build for the model of today, build for the model six months from now." — Boris Cherny (paraphrasing Ben Mann's advice, ~01:11)
- "At every level, it doesn't matter what level you're at, you should always feel a little bit of imposter syndrome because if you don't, then you're not pushing yourself hard enough." — Boris Cherny (~42:29)
- "You never delegate the thing you don't want to do. ... You always delegate the thing you do want to do and that you know well because then you can monitor the progress." — Boris Cherny (~01:00:49)
- "Even though Anthropic has tripled since the start of the year, productivity per engineer has grown almost 70% per engineer because of Claude Code." — Boris Cherny (~01:12:09)
