# New Grad to Principal Engineer (IC8) at Meta (Career Story)

Adrien Friggeri joined [Meta](../entities/meta.md) (then Facebook) straight from a PhD in social network analysis and grew to principal engineer (IC8), with detours through a failed startup attempt and a year at Clubhouse. He was the original tech lead behind Bento, Meta's company-wide Jupyter notebook platform, brought A/B testing to early [Instagram](../entities/instagram.md) as an IC4, and later became the experiences tech lead across an unreleased smart-glasses hardware line. The episode is a level-by-level walk through his [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) (IC5 through IC8) with unusually concrete detail on how consensus for a principal promo actually gets built, plus honest reflection on the trade-offs of leaving [big tech](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md).

## Key takeaways

- His first manager, Cameron Marlow, corrected his PhD pretension on day one: "Software engineers have power at this company... don't pigeonhole yourself" — Adrien has identified as a software engineer, not a data scientist, ever since, an early lesson in [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) framing.
- His first project analyzed how rumors spread on Facebook: false rumors spread faster than true ones (they are more egregious, hence more viral), and debunking barely limits spread — work that fed into down-weighting reshares in News Feed.
- His IC5 promo came from building Instagram's first A/B testing system as a naive IC4 whom leadership had explicitly told "don't bug us" during the AWS-to-Facebook infra migration — he hacked exposure logging through an allow-listed Facebook API endpoint and recruited a PM for air cover; he didn't even know promotions existed ([developer tools](../concepts/developer-tools.md)).
- His biggest self-described career mistake: never officially joining early Instagram while embedded there — peers from that ~30-person team went on to IC7–IC10 far faster ("a road not taken," not a regret like "I didn't buy Bitcoin in 2013") ([regrets and advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md)).
- Bento began as personal helper scripts to escape icky data-pipeline boilerplate (300 lines down to 20); he negotiated 2–3 months of explicit perf risk with his manager and skip, then built it into the company-wide notebook platform used by ~60% of data practitioners, earning his IC7/TLM2 promo.
- Adoption strategy for internal tools: don't fight existing workflows — capture newcomers at bootcamp/DataCamp in a fast-growing company (network effects convert the stragglers), and provide support for the legacy systems to build trust before migrating users ([influence and leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
- On taking perf risk: the consequences of underperforming are more drastic today than a decade ago, but the strategy is the same — make the exploration an explicitly agreed expectation with your manager and skip, over-communicate, and be ready to hear no.
- Leaving Meta in 2018 to found a startup taught him he wasn't an entrepreneur: founders must delegate the hard interesting problems and scale through others, while he wanted to go deep technically — "I left the company to do a startup, and I ended up taking a sabbatical instead" ([startups and founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md)).
- Both boomerangs back to Meta came through trusted former managers (James Pearce, then Vincent Hardy), and he has never applied for a job through a website — every career change came through relationships ([mentorship and sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md), [non-linear careers](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md)).
- The IC8 promo required deliberate consensus-building: co-writing the promo packet with a manager who had never done one, socializing with multiple VPs a cycle early, and asking a critical D2 for monthly check-ins six months ahead so the decisive voice was invested before the [calibration](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md) room — "you don't have a debate in the room."
- He carved out the "TL for experiences" role on a smart-glasses program by visibly taking coordination load (dozens of teams, ~35 PMs) off the existing uber TL with his blessing — scope is taken off someone's plate through over-communication, not stolen; the label came post hoc after a year of doing the work.
- Advice: be well-rounded ("specialization is for insects" — Heinlein), foster success around you without expecting returns, take more shots to increase your surface area for luck, and be patient — he pushed for IC7 before he was ready and it cost him cycles.

## Notable quotes

- "I often joke that I left the company to do a startup, and I ended up taking a sabbatical instead." — Adrien (~00:29:03)
- "I don't want to make it sound like getting promoted is just a social game. You need to have the underlying work too... It is also way easier to build the consensus ahead of time so that when you go to a performance calibration session, you don't have a debate in the room." — Adrien (~00:43:00)
- "Taking something off someone's plate doesn't mean that you're stealing from them — or it is, if you're not talking to them about it." — Adrien (~00:46:00)
- "I learned very quickly that we're all in this to succeed, and the best way to succeed is to help others succeed." — Adrien (~00:52:00)
