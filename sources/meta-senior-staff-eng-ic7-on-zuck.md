# Meta Senior Staff Eng (IC7) On Zuck Stories, Rapid Career Growth, Code Machine Archetype

[Michael Novati](../entities/michael-novati.md) joined [Meta](../entities/meta.md) (then Facebook) as an intern in 2009 when it had ~200 engineers, skipped a planned HCI PhD, and became Senior Staff (IC7) by age 27 — famously the #1 code committer at the company. Now co-founder of Formation, he shares pre-IPO stories (hackathons with [Mark Zuckerberg](../entities/mark-zuckerberg.md), the IPO bell on campus, ripstick bans), what defines the "code machine" archetype, how judgment and taste — not raw output — gated his growth, and how LLMs are changing the archetype. See [career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) and [big-tech-culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md).

## Key takeaways

- "Move fast and break things" was about breaking *norms* — first-principles thinking — not recklessness; early Facebook was extremely engineer-driven: PMs and designers had to win engineers over, and nearly all internal tools were built in-house.
- Novati was a code machine from day one: within his first week he single-handedly merged two competing internal task tools and announced it to the whole company. Raw output stayed roughly constant across his career; what actually got him to IC7 was accumulated *judgment and taste* — knowing which parts of the codebase are sensitive, how changes ripple into other teams' migrations, and when speed is safe ([career-growth](../concepts/career-growth.md)).
- His canonical IC7-scale wins were relentless solo migrations: manually refactoring away ~5-6,000 "Preparable" framework classes over several months, and eliminating every legacy direct-hit PHP endpoint in favor of the routing framework — projects he argues no team of senior engineers would have driven to completion.
- Trust is the code machine's real currency: after burning credibility with a rushed bug, he told the push team "if this breaks the site, I will resign" — and meant it — as a mechanism to force his own triple-checking and to rebuild trust ([influence-and-leadership](../concepts/influence-and-leadership.md)).
- As IC7 he spent ~30% of his focus on his team (operating like a senior eng there) and ~70% on codebase-wide initiatives, reporting nominally to junior managers while effectively being reviewed by skips and meeting VPs monthly; he ruthlessly declined meetings and stand-ups to protect coding time.
- [Working with Zuck](../concepts/working-with-legends.md): he built emoji-reactions-on-any-post with Zuckerberg at a 2009 hackathon (the code was too bad to ship, but Zuck's idea became the industry norm), and during the 2010 Google Plus lockdown made a company-wide bet that got Zuck to personally merge a PR — in exchange for Novati giving up riding ripsticks in the office.
- His popular-but-cautionary internal newsletter ("Weekly NTI") got real predictions right (a South San Francisco office) but he now says it was a mistake: off-the-cuff open posts (e.g., calling out second-class treatment of non-software-engineer roles, listing which companies were poaching Facebook engineers) created executive damage-control friction — though when he asked Zuckerberg directly, Zuck encouraged him to keep posting ([corporate-politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md)).
- On [AI coding tools](../concepts/ai-coding-tools.md): LLMs are the Vim-to-VSCode leap repeated — he's producing ~5x more code in his 500k-line Formation codebase by prompting for code he already sees in his head. Near-term, tools amplify experienced engineers with strong taste; long-term, AI maintaining its own services behind API contracts may make human-readable code irrelevant — and he thinks we should shape that future rather than resist it ([ai-era-engineering](../concepts/ai-era-engineering.md)).
- What IC7+ engineers had in common (from the IC7+-only group): extreme conscientiousness, attention to detail, and sharpness — conversations moved through new protocols in minutes with rapid, precise follow-up questions, regardless of archetype (code machine, specialist, fixer).
- His self-admitted weakness: he prioritized problems whose solutions he could already see in his head over the org's top priority, which held back his impact; his advice for stuck code machines is to push scope beyond your department and ask senior leaders for wider problems.
- He left Meta after ~10 years when the "all-in" cult-like mutual loyalty faded into a business relationship and vesting-cliff logistics meant an ~80% take-home pay drop was coming ([compensation-and-equity](../concepts/compensation-and-equity.md)); he estimates his career growth was 50/50 luck (landing at 2009 Facebook) and hard work.
- [Advice to his younger self](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md) and to Formation students: stop treating feedback as a grade to pass; the growth loop is do something, get feedback from people whose taste you aspire to (not peers barely ahead of you), and actually action it — that cycle compounds exceptionally fast.
