# Meta Senior Manager (M2) on Manager Career Growth, PIPs, Amazon vs Meta | Stefan Mai

Stefan Mai spent years as an engineer and manager at [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) (early self-service advertising, then ML in supply chain) before becoming a Senior Manager (M2, equivalent to Senior Staff IC7) at [Meta](../entities/meta.md) working on integrity, and now co-runs interview-prep company Hello Interview. Because he's out of big tech, he's unusually transparent about the machinery ICs never see: manager-vs-manager politics and "mutiny," low-performer quotas and stack ranking, whether PIPs are escapable, discretionary equity decisions, and how [Amazon](../entities/amazon.md) and [Meta](../entities/meta.md) cultures differ from the manager's chair.

## Key takeaways

- Mai's first week at Amazon in 2012 set the tone for its culture ([big-tech-culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md)): his manager gathered the team at 6pm and committed them to a nights-and-weekends "death march" for 6–8 months. He calls 50–60 hour weeks "fairly common" for the ambitious in big tech.
- His [management](../concepts/management.md) transition at Amazon came prematurely ("they should not have given in") — it took ~18 months of failing before he got a knack for it. Core early mistake: not knowing how to get reports to open up about their real goals; his fix is active listening, because engineers default to transactional "API-style" one-on-ones.
- Storytelling is a bedrock manager skill: notice and critique the stories you already tell yourself, then narrate your team's trajectory ("here's where we came from, here's the promised land"). A manager gets to define what winning is, and "a team that's winning" makes politics, bickering, and zero-sum mentality largely disappear — those problems fester exactly when the winning slows down.
- Manager-of-managers is a genuinely different job with a real risk of "mutiny" ([corporate-politics](../concepts/corporate-politics.md)): in flat or shrinking orgs, leaf-node managers advance by displacing the person above them, and gripes surface in skip-levels. His director also taught him never to run two teams with overlapping charters as an "experiment" — they undercut each other with friction that dwarfs any gain.
- Senior leaders have fewer, more binary levers: you can't do an underperforming manager's job for them, and coaching too hard reads as doing exactly that — "if Mark has an SVP who's just not making the cut, he's gonna let them go," not run a program.
- Managers are not layoff-proof ([layoffs](../concepts/layoffs.md)): companies target manager-to-IC ratios, and span-of-control reductions (as at Amazon) put managers first on the chopping block.
- On [calibrations-and-ratings](../concepts/calibrations-and-ratings.md): both Amazon and Meta converge on similar systems — public ratings plus a private senior-leader process (Amazon "top tier," Meta discretionary/additional equity) deciding who's a linchpin. Meta pays its upper quintile dramatically more (some engineers cleared 2x their peers); frugal Amazon claws that back. He estimated the review process costs ~$15,000 per engineer per cycle and defends it anyway: "the integrity of the performance review process is the whole ball game."
- Low-performer quotas are real and universal at scale: roughly 7–15% (sometimes up to 20%) are pushed into the bottom bucket ("needs improvement" at Amazon, "meets some/meets most" at Meta), where PIPs engage. The nightmare scenario is a director forcing a manager to retroactively downgrade someone they'd praised all half.
- PIPs are first a legal paper trail, but people genuinely escape them — the trail loses legal credibility if nobody survives it. Amazon HR was "pretty savage" box-checking; Meta HR (at the time) exhausted every avenue before termination to protect its employer brand, and your manager may sincerely be rooting for you.
- On [hiring-and-interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md): AI cheating tools make remote interviews "horrible" and anti-cheat (focus/copy-paste detection) only catches the unsophisticated; he's more optimistic about assessments evolving (design interviews, AI-allowed assignments) than anti-cheat. For [OpenAI](../entities/openai.md)/Anthropic interviews: they notoriously reuse question areas, and their behavioral rounds want raw technical achievement — the classic staff-engineer "I wrote seven docs and aligned six teams" story lands poorly there.
- [Job hopping](../concepts/career-growth.md) helps ambitious juniors (growth comes from discomfort in new environments) but is a red flag at senior levels — hiring managers second-guess staff+ candidates moving every 18–24 months. Beware the inverse trap too: your credibility snowball can become a shackle when you're forever "the person who built that system."
- Advice to his younger self ([regrets-and-advice](../concepts/regrets-and-advice.md), [mentorship-and-sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)): find mentors instead of going it alone, and write explicit plans. Walking into his director's office post-promotion with a list of things he didn't yet know how to do was, per that director, the moment he knew Mai would succeed — "the world is very malleable to those kinds of plans."
