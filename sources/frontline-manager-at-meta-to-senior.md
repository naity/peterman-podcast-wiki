# Frontline Manager at Meta to Senior Director at Snapchat in 3 years (Career Story)

Rong Yan went from frontline manager at [Meta](../entities/meta.md) (Facebook) to Senior Director at [Snapchat](../entities/snapchat.md) in about three years, with a Director stop at Square in between; he is now CTO of AI video startup HeyGen. A former [IBM](../entities/ibm.md) Research scientist with a CMU machine learning PhD, he explains how a single "North Star" goal — becoming CTO of an AI company — drove every unconventional career choice, from leaving research for engineering to deliberately taking the Snapchat camera org over the comfortable data org. The episode is a candid look at [management](../concepts/management.md) growth at the director+ level, executive recruiting, and how much of leadership [career growth](../concepts/career-growth.md) is luck.

## Key takeaways

- Rong's rapid rise was partly strategic job hopping: he joined Square directly on a director offer (smaller companies will let you perform a level up), then grew his team from ~25 to 50+ engineers in about a year.
- The biggest frontline-manager-to-director gap: directing is not managing. Directors add value through strategic direction, cross-department resource gathering, and solving the org's hardest problems — not through knowing the most details. He admits he underperformed his first six months until he made this shift.
- When hired above people who wanted your role, the fix is building trust first (he recommends The Five Dysfunctions of a Team): show each report you are a promoter of their career, understand why they weren't promoted, and unblock things they can't unblock themselves.
- He picked his roles against a lifelong North Star (CTO of an AI company), deliberately choosing the Snapchat camera org over the data org and learning iOS programming from scratch in two months to close his product-side skill gaps — a theme of [non-linear careers](../concepts/non-linear-careers.md).
- From [Facebook](../entities/meta.md)'s six-week bootcamp — where a VP-level hire sat next to him fixing bugs — he internalized that everyone in engineering must stay technical; as a CTO he still writes and reviews code weekly, part of [big tech culture](../concepts/big-tech-culture.md) he carried forward.
- He scales himself by picking the top three priorities each week and treating everything else as less important; the best leaders win on time allocation, not hours.
- Snapchat grew from ~100 employees (he was roughly the 100th) to ~3,000 in two years; his org reached ~250 engineers. Every doubling of a team is "almost like you're building a new company," requiring leaders to abandon old cultural assumptions.
- Evan Spiegel stories: pixel-level product review feedback reminiscent of Steve Jobs, a promise never to fire the first 15 engineers, and buying back the original "Blue House" in 2017 to host board meetings there.
- Senior-level recruiting is almost all inbound and personal-network driven; he has never proactively job-searched and weights personal connections heavily because he wants people he trusts through a company's down cycles — an argument for [mentorship and sponsorship](../concepts/mentorship-and-sponsorship.md)-style network building early.
- On [promotions](../concepts/promotions.md) and luck: manager career growth is heavily constrained by org structure and company trajectory. Don't make promotions the objective — control what you can (impact in your current role) and treat titles/levels as lagging indicators the company owns, not you.
- At HeyGen he tells candidates upfront there is only a ~1% chance of huge success, deliberately filtering for the small fraction of people who will stay through hard cycles ([hiring and interviews](../concepts/hiring-and-interviews.md), [startups and founding](../concepts/startups-and-founding.md)).

## Notable quotes

- "Director is about directing instead of managing. It really opened my mind about the right way to manage managers instead of managing individual contributors." — Rong Yan (~03:06)
- "The one thing I gradually realized is that levels and titles are things the company gives you; they are not yours." — Rong Yan (~24:59)
- "Don't make promotions the objective of your work. ... control the things you can control, which is making an impact in your current position. Everything else will be lagging indicators." — Rong Yan (~35:33)
- "Every single time you double your team, it's almost like you're building a new company." — Rong Yan (~20:48)
